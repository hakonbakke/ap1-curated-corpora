# Parasites perform poorly at high host densities: an experimental test in the salmon louse Lepeophtheirus salmonis

**Document ID:** `2026_prslb_folk-parasites-high-host-density`  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1098/rspb.2025.1782  
**Priority questions:** Q2, Q6

## Key findings

- RFID-tracked **178** adult female lice; **low** vs **high** host-density tanks (Norwegian farm-origin lice; **8.8–9.5 °C**).
- High density: **greater pre-reproductive mortality** (adult loads only **~25%** higher despite **~2×** infection dose); **smaller** females; **later** maturation (**~12–15** degree-days delay); **lower** early and lifetime fecundity.
- Mechanism attributed primarily to **stronger host immunity** under high challenge; **farm-adapted** lice may dominate infection of wild fish—**spillover** may impose **higher virulence** than models assuming wild-type lice parameters.

## Evidence type

- empirical (laboratory experiment)

## Population-level relevance

- Does **not** measure returns; implies **farm conditions** alter **lice demography** (fecundity, survival)—feeds **Q2 larval production** uncertainty and **Q6** harm pathways for wild fish if spillover uses **aquaculture-adapted** parasites.

## Competing or conflicting points

- Complicates **simple scaling** from farm female counts to **larval output**; interacts with debates on **density dependence / Allee** effects raised in TLS critiques (conceptual alignment—not same experiments as Van Nes review).

## Uncertainty / limitations

- **500 L** tanks; males not tagged; three Norwegian farm genotypes only.

## Context

- Geography: Norway (lice sources: Austevoll, Oppedal, Fosså)
- Life stage: adult female lice performance (hosts: farmed fish in tanks)
- System type: experimental aquaculture-like vs naturalised low-density
- Time period: experiment **2021**; publication **2026**
