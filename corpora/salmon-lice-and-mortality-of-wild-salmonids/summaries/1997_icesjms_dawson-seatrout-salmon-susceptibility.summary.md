# Comparison of the susceptibility of sea trout (Salmo trutta L.) and Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar L.) to sea lice (Lepeophtheirus salmonis (Krøyer, 1837)) infections

**Document ID:** `1997_icesjms_dawson-seatrout-salmon-susceptibility`  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1006/jmsc.1997.0309  
**Priority questions:** Q4, Q6

## Key findings

- Under lab challenge (~4,800 copepodids per tank; 7.9–11.4 °C), lice counts diverged by host: by weeks 6–8 sea trout carried significantly more lice than Atlantic salmon.
- Atlantic salmon showed faster immune-mediated clearance; sea trout retained higher long-term abundance; lesion patterns differed by body region and week.
- **No fish mortality** from lice infection occurred in the experiment (limits inference to population-level mortality).

## Evidence type

- empirical (laboratory infection experiment)

## Population-level relevance

- Does **not** estimate smolt mortality, adult returns, or population viability; informs **relative host susceptibility** and why sea trout are used as sentinels / proxies in monitoring (Q4, Q6 pathway).

## Competing or conflicting points

- Interprets early similarity then divergence as largely **non-selective settlement** followed by **differential survival**, not simple “preference” for one host—relevant when extrapolating from farm-origin copepodids to wild exposure.

## Uncertainty / limitations

- Hatchery fish; immune response may differ from wild smolts/post-smolts.
- Single infection context (Scottish farm-origin lice); 8-week duration; no long-term mortality or marine migration realism.

## Context

- Geography: Scotland (ambient seawater)
- Life stage: juvenile fish under experimental challenge (not wild migration)
- System type: laboratory tanks (single-species and mixed-species)
- Time period: experiment reported 1997 (ICES JMS)
