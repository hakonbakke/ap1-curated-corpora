## What this paper does

Johnsen et al. (2021) address the impact of salmon lice (*Lepeophtheirus salmonis*) on the mortality of wild Atlantic salmon (*Salmo salar*) during their critical post-smolt migration phase in Norway. The study utilizes the Virtual Post-Smolt (VPS) model to estimate lice-induced mortality across 401 Norwegian salmon rivers, integrating hydrodynamic models of lice dispersion with genetic assignment of wild post-smolts captured via trawling. This research is significant as it provides a quantitative framework for understanding the ecological impacts of aquaculture on wild salmon populations, particularly in the context of Norway's extensive salmon farming industry.

The study covers the years 2015 to 2019, focusing on the migration routes of post-smolts as they transition from freshwater rivers to marine environments where they encounter varying levels of lice infestation. Given the increasing concerns regarding the sustainability of aquaculture practices and their effects on wild fish populations, this research contributes to the ongoing discourse on environmental management and regulatory frameworks, particularly the Traffic Light System (TLS) used in Norway to monitor and manage lice levels in aquaculture.

---

## Methods and data

The VPS model combines hydrodynamic data on salmon lice dispersion with simulated migration patterns of post-smolts. The model was calibrated using data from wild post-smolts captured by the NALO trawl program between 2015 and 2019, with a total of 2,125 genetically assigned fish used for calibration. The genetic assignment process, based on 31 microsatellite markers across 45 populations, ensured a high assignment probability (>0.80) for the fish included in the analysis.

The model estimates infestation pressure (IP) as the product of migration time and mean lice concentration along the migration route. A total of 1,000 VPS were simulated per river annually, with a migration time window of 40 days. The model also incorporates a mortality conversion table that links the number of lice per fish to assumed mortality rates, which were derived from previous studies (Taranger et al., 2015).

## Key findings

### Mortality Estimates (2019)
- **Total Rivers Analyzed:** 401
- **Mortality Classifications:**
  - **Green (<10% mortality):** 179 rivers
  - **Yellow (10–30% mortality):** 140 rivers
  - **Red (>30% mortality):** 82 rivers

### Management Areas (MA) Classification
- **High Mortality (>30%):** MAs 3 and 4
- **Moderate Mortality (10–30%):** MAs 2, 5, 6, 7, and 10
- **Low Mortality (<10%):** MAs 1, 8, 9, 11, 12, and 13

### Temporal Trends (2012–2019)
- A decrease in the proportion of low-mortality rivers (MA2–6) since 2012–2013.
- MA3 showed a reduction in highly affected rivers (>30% mortality) after a peak in 2016.

### Calibration and Sensitivity
- The model's calibration involved 2,125 wild post-smolts, with 446 discarded due to lack of matching VPS.
- Sensitivity tests indicated that variations in assumed tolerance limits for lice significantly affected mortality estimates, with shifts in management area classifications for 4 out of 13 areas in 2019.

## Limitations and caveats

The authors acknowledge several limitations in their study:
- The mortality thresholds used in the model are primarily based on laboratory and hatchery studies, which may not fully represent wild conditions.
- The model's migration timing is extrapolated for rivers lacking comprehensive smolt-trap series, potentially introducing bias.
- The model does not account for active avoidance behaviors or vertical movements of post-smolts in response to lice concentrations.
- Calibration efforts were concentrated in areas with the highest aquaculture intensity, which may not reflect conditions in less impacted regions.

---

## What this paper does not claim

The authors do not claim that the VPS model provides a definitive measure of actual mortality rates for all wild salmon populations. The model is based on assumptions regarding lice survival and infestation efficiency, which may vary in natural environments. Additionally, the study does not address the long-term ecological consequences of lice-induced mortality on salmon populations or the broader marine ecosystem.
