## What this paper does

This review by Gillson et al. (2022) aims to provide a comprehensive overview of the principal marine stressors affecting *Salmo salar* (Atlantic salmon) from the time they leave freshwater until they return as adults. The authors apply a semi-quantitative 2D classification system, originally developed by Forseth et al. (2017) for Norwegian rivers, to assess the relative threats to English salmon stocks. The study encompasses **42 principal salmon rivers** in England, categorized into **four regional marine plan areas**, and evaluates **13 marine stressors** based on their effects and likelihood of development over the next decade.

The significance of this work lies in its focus on the geographic context of salmon lice as a population threat, particularly in English rivers where salmon mariculture is minimal. By identifying and classifying stressors, the authors provide valuable insights for management strategies aimed at improving salmon conservation and understanding the broader implications of marine stressors on salmon populations across different regions.

## Methods and data

The authors conducted an extensive literature review, scrutinizing peer-reviewed articles and grey literature to identify marine stressors impacting salmon survival. The study areas included all **42 principal salmon rivers** in England, which were grouped into four regional marine plan areas designated by the UK’s Marine Management Organisation (MMO): North East, South, South West, and North West. The classification system used scores from **0 to 3** on two axes: effects (impact on salmon) and development (likelihood of change over the next decade). 

The classification involved expert judgment combined with local knowledge and literature evidence. Each stressor was assessed based on its effects on salmon, habitat zone, spatial extent, and the existence of mitigation measures. The authors also evaluated the knowledge and uncertainty surrounding each stressor, categorizing them as having extensive, moderate, or poor knowledge and small, moderate, or large uncertainty.

## Key findings

1. **High-impact stressors**:
   - **Climate change**: Identified as the most significant threat, affecting ocean temperature, circulation, and prey availability.
   - **Predation**: Significant mortality source for smolts and adults, with marine mammals and birds posing major risks.

2. **Stabilized high-impact stressors**:
   - **Water quality**: Poor water quality remains a concern but is manageable with existing mitigation measures.
   - **Bycatch**: Incidental capture in fisheries is significant but can be mitigated.

3. **Low-impact stressors**:
   - **Salmon mariculture**: Classified as a low-impact stressor for English stocks due to the absence of nearby farms, contrasting with regions like Norway where lice from farms are a major threat.
   - Other low-impact stressors include tidal barrages, artificial light at night, and electromagnetic fields.

4. **No longer important**:
   - **Salmon fisheries exploitation**: Effective restrictions have significantly reduced fishing pressure, making it a non-issue for current salmon populations.

The review notes that post-smolt lice infestations can reduce returns by **0.6% to 39%** where farms exist, highlighting the geographic context-dependence of lice as a threat.

## Limitations and caveats

The findings of this review are specific to English salmon stocks and should not be extrapolated to Norwegian production areas or other regions with different environmental contexts. The classification of stressors relies on expert judgment, and quantitative support for some stressors, particularly predation and artificial light at night, is limited. Additionally, the review synthesizes existing literature without providing new primary data, which may limit the robustness of some conclusions.

## What this paper does not claim

The authors do not claim that the identified stressors are the only factors affecting salmon populations. They acknowledge that other stressors may exist and that the impacts of climate change and predation may interact with other environmental factors in complex ways. The review does not provide a definitive ranking of stressors applicable to all regions, emphasizing the need for localized assessments.

## Relevance to this corpus

This paper is particularly relevant to **Q9**, which addresses the population-level impact of salmon lice from aquaculture. It illustrates the context-dependent nature of lice as a threat, contrasting findings from regions with significant aquaculture presence (e.g., Norway, British Columbia) with those from English rivers where such farms are absent. This distinction is crucial for interpreting findings from different geographic contexts in salmon management discussions.
