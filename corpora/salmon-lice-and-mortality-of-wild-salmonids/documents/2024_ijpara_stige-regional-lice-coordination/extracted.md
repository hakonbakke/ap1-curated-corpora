Parasitic salmon lice (Lepeophtheirus salmonis) are a constraint to the sustainable growth of salmonids in open net pens, and this issue has caused production to level off in recent years in the most aquacultureintensive areas of Norway. The maximum allowed biomass at a regional level is regulated by using the socalled ‘‘trafﬁc light” system, where salmon louse-induced mortality of migrating wild salmon post-smolts is evaluated against set targets. As a case study, we have investigated how a speciﬁc aquacultureintensive area can reduce its louse levels sufﬁciently to achieve a low impact on wild salmon. Analyses of the output from a virtual post-smolt model that uses data on the reported number of salmon lice in ﬁsh farms as key input data and estimates the salmon louse-induced mortality of wild out-migrating Atlantic salmon post-smolts, suggested that female louse abundance on the local farms must be halved in spring to reach the goal implied by the trafﬁc light system. The outcome of a modelling scenario simulating a proposed new plan for coordinated production and fallowing proved beneﬁcial, with an overall reduction in louse infestations and treatment efforts. The interannual variability in louse abundance in spring, however, increased for this scenario, implying unacceptably high louse abundance when many farms were in their second production year. We then combined the scenario with coordinated production with other louse control measures. Only measures that reduced the density of farmed salmonids in open cages in the study area resulted in reductions in salmon louse infestations to acceptable levels. This could be achieved either by stocking with larger ﬁsh to reduce exposure time or by reducing ﬁsh numbers, e.g. by producing in closed units.

- 1. Introduction


Marine farming of salmonids in open cages has a history of approximately 50 years and has grown into an advanced aquaculture industry in many temperate coastal areas around the world. Global production of salmonids has increased more or less linearly over the last 30 years, from approximately 0.5 million metric tons in 1994 to approximately 3.2 million metric tons in 2021 (Fig. 1). Norway is the main producer with a share of production varying between 42 and 66% between the years 1992–2018 (Iversen et al., 2020). In Norway, the standing stock of farmed salmonids has also increased constantly over time, but with a tendency towards levelling off during recent years (Fig. 1).

In the Vestland county of Norway, however, where the present study is based, there has been no overall growth in the production of farmed salmonids since 2010 (Fig. 1). The overruling reason for this lack of further growth in salmon farming is density-dependent host-parasite interactions (Anderson and May, 1991; Krkošek, 2010). The open net pens that dominate the production systems allow contagious contacts between cages and proximate farms, implying that increasing the number of farms or the number of ﬁsh in the farms in an area will promote the production and spread of pathogens within and between farms (Jansen et al., 2012). Both microparasitic pathogens such as viruses and bacteria, and macroparasites such as sea lice, conﬁrm the contagious contacts between proximate salmonid farms since disease outbreaks tend to occur as local epidemics (Kristoffersen et al., 2009, 2014; Aldrin et al., 2011; Jones et al., 2015).

The salmon louse is a natural indigenous ecto-parasite of wild Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar), sea trout (Salmo trutta) and char (Salvelinus alpinus) in Norwegian marine waters. The parasitic life stages of the salmon louse feed on skin tissue, mucus, and blood of their salmonid hosts (Pike and Wadsworth, 1999). Depending on the infestation intensity on a host ﬁsh, salmon lice can do extensive harm and cause mortality (Costello, 2009). In the early phases of Norwegian salmonid farming, incidents with harmful loads of salmon lice led to regulations on maximum allowed louse numbers per farmed ﬁsh. Regulations evolved over time to lower allowances and required weekly counting of louse infestations together with reporting requirements. Furthermore, regulations have evolved towards a stronger focus on environmental sustainability regarding adverse impacts of salmon lice from farms on wild Atlantic salmon (Bøhn et al., 2022; Vollset et al., 2023a). On acknowledging the principle of a density-dependent host-parasite relationship between farmed salmon and salmon lice, a new management system called the ‘‘trafﬁc light” system was ﬁrst proposed in a white paper (Ministry of Trade, Industry and Fisheries, 2015), and effectuated in 2017 (Ministry of Trade, Industry and Fisheries, 2017). This system divides the Norwegian coast into 13 productions areas (PAs) from the south to the north. Based on evaluations over 2 year periods of salmon louse-induced mortality of migrating wild postsmolts of Atlantic salmon, the PAs are downregulated with 6% reductions in allowed biomass when wild post-smolt mortality is expected to be 30% or more due to salmon lice from farms (red light). If wild post-smolt mortality is expected to be between 10 and 30% (yellow light), allowed biomass is not regulated, whereas in PAs where post-smolt mortality is expected to be less than 10% (green light), production may be upregulated by 6%. The salmon louse-induced mortality is assessed annually by an expert group based on a combination of surveillance data and models (e.g., Vollset et al., 2023b). The surveillance data originate from monitoring louse abundance on cultured salmon post-smolts in sentinel cages and on wild salmonids, but have limited geographical and temporal coverage. The models include three virtual post-smolt

models, which estimate salmon louse-induced mortality for outmigrating post-smolts from 401 salmon-producing rivers along the Norwegian coast (Kristoffersen et al., 2018; Johnsen et al., 2021; Stige et al., 2022). These models calculate the spatiotemporal variation in infestation pressure based on farm-reported data on salmon lice, ﬁsh and temperature, calibrate the infestation pressure to surveillance data on louse infestations on salmon postsmolts from trawling and/or sentinel cages, and simulate the migration, louse infestation and louse-induced mortality of virtual post-smolts from each river.

In PA4 on the west coast, production has been downregulated due to red lights ﬁrst in 2020, then again in 2022. The present study is a case study from a part of PA4 in or adjacent to the large fjord Nordfjord (Fig. 2). Nine of the 401 salmon rivers assessed in the trafﬁc light system drain into the Nordfjord, which the postsmolt Atlantic salmon need to migrate through to reach their feeding grounds in the North Atlantic Ocean (Bjerck et al., 2021). Two important questions are thus: what level of salmon louse infestation can be tolerated in the farms in this region in order to reduce the adverse impacts on wild ﬁsh populations to acceptable levels, and how can this level be reached? According to the trafﬁc light system, acceptable levels would comply with the green light, i.e. for expected louse-induced mortality of post-smolts from the local rivers to be less than 10%.

To answer these questions, we ﬁrst established a reasonable goal for controlling the total salmon louse infestation pressure from farms in the study area, by comparing expected louseinduced mortalities for post-smolts from the local rivers (Stige et al., 2022) with the total adult female louse loads in the farms. We then used a stage-structured model for salmon louse dynamics in salmon farms (Aldrin et al., 2017, 2023), to simulate effects of various control efforts. In the simulations, all the farms in the study area were connected with regard to salmon louse transmission. The aims of the study were speciﬁcally to: (i) assess how much the total number of adult female salmon lice in the study area must be reduced for the adverse impacts on wild ﬁsh populations to reach acceptable levels; (ii) assess how a proposed new scheme for coordinated production and fallowing inﬂuences salmon louse

- 2. Material and methods


- 2.1. Study system and analytical approach


We took a hindcast scenario approach, where we investigated how the salmon louse dynamics in the study area in the 8 year period 2015–2022 might have developed under alternative scenarios of salmon louse control, all other factors being equal. The study area includes 19 locations with salmonid production during this period (Fig. 2). For each farm location in the study area and surrounding areas, we had time series of the number and mean weight of salmonid ﬁsh at the end of each month, weekly reported number and stage distribution of salmon lice per ﬁsh, weekly temperature measurements at 3 m depth and daily modelled salinity for 0–3 m depth. Weekly reporting of salmon lice and sea temperature, and monthly reporting of ﬁsh numbers and weights have been mandatory since 2012 for salmonid ﬁsh farms in Norway (Ministry of Trade, Industry and Fisheries, 2012). Salmon lice are counted on a subset of the ﬁsh in a farm each week and separated into adult females, other motiles (including pre-adult lice and adult males) and sessile lice (the youngest parasitic stages). The lice and temperature data are publicly available at the BarentsWatch portal (https://www.barentswatch.no/en/ﬁshhealth/) and the salmonid production data are administered by the Directorate of Fisheries, Norway. The salinity data were extracted from the NorKyst800 ocean model archive (downloaded from https:// met.no, accessed 01 February 2023).

Salmon lice disperse between hosts during pelagic larval stages and, if successfully attaching to a salmonid host, develop through three sessile and two pre-adult motile stages into adult males or females (Hamre et al., 2013; Brooker et al., 2018). The infestation risk for a salmonid ﬁsh depends on the concentration of infective salmon louse larvae in the sea, which we refer to as the ‘‘infestation pressure”, and the behaviours of the lice and the ﬁsh (Murray and Moriarty, 2021). For the farmed ﬁsh, these larvae can originate from the farm itself as well as from neighbouring farms. Possible infestation from salmon lice on wild salmonids is not included in the models, as wild salmonids probably produce less than 3% of the adult egg-bearing female salmon lice in Norwegian coastal waters (Dempster et al., 2021). In the models considered in this study, the contribution of a given farm to the infestation pressure at a given location and time was a function of the number of adult female salmon lice in the farm, sea temperature, and sea distance to the farm (Kristoffersen et al., 2014; Stige et al., 2021). Higher sea temperature leads to a higher egg production rate of each adult female salmon louse and a shorter time lag from the egg stage to the infective larval stage. The contribution to infestation risk decreases according to a statistically estimated function of sea distance (Aldrin et al., 2019), with sea distance being the shortest possible distance between two points without crossing land (Dean et al., 2021). This approach provides a ﬁrm empirical basis for the modelled risk of farm-to-farm spread of lice, but does not account for asymmetric interactions between farms or geographic and seasonal differences in connectivity caused by hydrographic variability (Huserbråten and Johnsen, 2022; Kragesteen et al., 2023). In the hindcast scenarios, the contribution of farms outside of the study area to the infestation pressure was kept as in the original time-series, whereas the contribution of farms within the study area was dynamically modelled. Hence, we modelled parasite spillover between farms in the study area, but ignored possible feedback between the louse control in these farms and infestations from neighbouring areas. This simpliﬁcation was justiﬁed by 89% of the infestation pressure originating from farms within the study area (average for years 2015–2022, evaluated at the 19 farm locations).

2.2. Assessment of a regional louse control goal

The regional louse-control goal was quantiﬁed in terms of the total number of adult female salmon lice in salmonid farms in spring. This goal was chosen rather than, e.g., the production of salmon louse larvae, as the louse larval production is strongly dependent on temperature (Samsing et al., 2016), which cannot be controlled by the farmers. Weekly numbers of adult female salmon lice in each farm were calculated by multiplying the reported numbers of adult female lice per ﬁsh with weekly interpolated numbers of ﬁsh in the farm. The louse numbers were summed over all farms in the study area and averaged over weeks 16–21 to annual values. These weeks coincide with a period of the year when the louse control legislation is stricter to protect out-migrating wild salmon, with a maximum allowed limit of 0.2 adult female salmon lice per ﬁsh compared to 0.5 the rest of the year (Ministry of Trade, Industry and Fisheries, 2012).

To relate the total salmon louse abundance to the environmental impact indicator used in the trafﬁc light system, we regressed annual numbers of adult female salmon lice in spring in years 2012–2022 against estimates of salmon louse-induced mortality for out-migrating wild salmon post-smolts for the same years (assuming a proportional relationship within the data range). Based on this relationship, we assessed which number of adult female salmon lice corresponded to a salmon louse-induced mortality of 10%, which is the threshold between low and moderate environmental impact. Salmon louse-induced mortality was calculated

by one of the virtual post-smolt models used in the trafﬁc light system (Kristoffersen et al., 2018; Stige et al., 2022). Here we averaged the values for the nine rivers which ﬂow out in the study area

- (Fig. 2). Note that the reported numbers of salmon lice in ﬁsh farms are both response data and key input data to the virtual post-smolt model. The regression analysis was therefore not suitable for assessing the strength of the association between salmon lice in ﬁsh farms and louse-induced mortality, but nonetheless useful for setting a measurable louse control goal relevant for the trafﬁc light system.

2.3. Simulating salmon louse dynamics

To investigate how coordination of salmon louse control between farms in the study area might reduce salmon louse levels and frequency of salmon louse treatments, we simulated the salmon louse dynamics using a population model for salmon lice (Aldrin et al., 2017, 2023). This model simulates the day-to-day development in the number of salmon lice in each farm, the weekly counting of lice on a subset of the ﬁsh, the effects of control measures taken by the farmer and the spread of infective salmon louse larvae between the farms.

The population model for lice is described in detail elsewhere (Aldrin et al., 2017, 2023), and we provide only a brief summary here. The main state variables in the model are the number and mean weight of ﬁsh and the number of lice in different developmental stages at each farm. The model divides salmon lice into ﬁve stages, where some of the modelled stages include more than one biological stage. Adult females (AF) reproduce and produce recruits

- (Fig. 3). Recruits (R) include eggs and the non-infective nauplius I and nauplius II larval stages. Copepodids (CO) are infective copepodid larvae in the water, before they have attached to a ﬁsh. As the larvae are pelagic, these stages are not linked to a speciﬁc cage and can originate from adult female lice from the focal farm as well as from surrounding farms. Sessile lice (S) include copepodids that have attached to a ﬁsh and chalimi (chalimus I and II). Pre-adult


lice (PA) include lice in the preadult I and pre-adult II stages. Finally, adult lice (A) are divided into adult males (AM) and adult females.

When salmonid smolts are stocked at sea, they are always free of lice, and the infestation process at a farm after a period of fallowing starts by external infestation of recruits produced by adult females at neighbouring farms. The external infestation pressure is a function of the reported temperature, the average number of adult female lice per ﬁsh in surrounding farms, the number of ﬁsh in the farms and the sea distances to these (Aldrin et al., 2023). From one day to the next, a louse can either die or survive, and if it survives, it can either develop to the next stage or stay in the current stage (Aldrin et al., 2023). Development times depend on temperature; the higher the temperature, the shorter time it takes to develop to the next stage. The infestation success, i.e. the probability for a copepodid larva attaching to a ﬁsh, also depends on temperature and increases with the number of ﬁsh and their size. The mortality from one day to another is divided into natural mortality, mortality due to use of cleaner ﬁsh and mortality due to louse treatments. The model also accounts for stochasticity by including random effects for several of the processes involved, including random counting errors and random variability in louse infestation and survival rates. The parameters of the model have been estimated based on detailed data from 90 production cycles widely spread along the Norwegian coast, including information about numbers and mean weights of farmed ﬁsh, louse counts, louse treatments, addition of cleaner ﬁsh, temperature, salinity and calculated external infestation pressure (Aldrin et al., 2023).

The salmon louse dynamics in the 19 farms in the study area were simulated from 01 January 2014 to 31 December 2022. The ﬁrst year was used as initialisation period, resulting in 8 years of results being analysed. The dynamics were simulated with posterior mean parameter values. For each salmon louse control scenario, we ran 100 simulations with independently generated random effects to quantify the effects of stochasticity in louse dynamics. - 2.5. Coordination of other louse control measures


In a series of scenario simulations, we investigated the effects of coordinated production and fallowing in combination with coordination of other louse control measures. Such coordination included coordinated use of louse skirts, treatment at a critical time in spring, lower treatment thresholds, shorter production cycles, and/or production in closed systems (Table 1). The baseline scenarios these simulations were compared with was the ‘Zonation’ scenario with coordinated production and fallowing and ordinary treatments, but no other louse control measures. As salmon lice was mainly a problem in even-numbered years in this scenario, some of the additional control measures were only applied in even-numbered years.

Plankton-mesh louse ‘skirts’ around net pens can block infective salmon louse larvae near the sea surface from reaching the farmed ﬁsh (Stien et al., 2018). Other technologies to reduce host-parasite encounters include the use of ‘snorkel’ cages, deep lights and submerged feeding to induce the farmed ﬁsh to avoid surface waters (Barrett et al., 2020). The effectiveness of these technologies is variable, partly due to environmental conditions that inﬂuence the depth distribution of the louse larvae and the ﬁsh (Jónsdóttir et al., 2023; Oldham, 2023; Oldham et al., 2023). We here investigated scenarios where we assumed that louse skirts or other shielding technologies stopped 50% of the external louse larvae from reaching the farmed ﬁsh, either in all farms (‘Skirt all’ scenario, Table 1) or half of the farms (‘Skirt half’ scenario). In each of the 100 simulations in the ‘Skirt half’ scenario, we randomly selected seven among 13 active northern farms and two among four active southern farms that used this strategy (i.e., 53% of the farms). The random selection was done by sampling from the farms in each area without replacement. As louse skirts can reduce water exchange and potentially ﬁsh welfare (Jónsdóttir et al., 2023), we also investigated scenarios where louse skirts were only used during the ﬁrst 180 days of the production cycle in all farms (‘Skirt 180 days’ scenario) or only in April-June in even-numbered years (‘Skirt spring’ scenario).

Coordinated application of salmon louse treatments prior to the out-migration time of wild salmon can potentially reduce the impacts on wild ﬁsh. To investigate the effects of this strategy, we investigated scenarios where all farms applied a louse treatment in week 12 or 15 in even-numbered years, either with 80 or 98% effect (scenarios ‘Week 120, ‘Week 150, ‘Week 12 98%’, ‘Week 15 98%’, Table 1). An effectiveness of 98% has been estimated for chemical treatments with imidacloprid, the active substance of a medicine recently approved for salmon louse control (Aldrin et al., 2023). We assumed that the highly effective treatment with 98% effect was only used during the coordinated spring treatment, and that at other times of the year, treatments had 80% effect.

We also investigated the effects of treating at lower louse abundance than the ordinary regulatory limit in spring and summer (‘Week 13–39 0.1 lice’, Table 1). Speciﬁcally, instead of assuming that all farmers used a cage-based treatment threshold of 0.2 adult female lice per ﬁsh in weeks 16–21 and 0.5 at other times of the year, we assumed that all farmers in the area treated at a threshold of 0.1 in weeks 13–39 and 0.5 at other times of the year. The rationale for the choice of treatment limits was that Norwegian ﬁsh farmers who succeed in keeping average louse numbers at the farm below these limits may be eligible for exemptions from biomass

reduction in the trafﬁc light system (Ministry of Trade, Industry and Fisheries, 2017, §12). We also investigated combinations of coordinated louse treatments with 80 or 98% effect in week 12 and a lower treatment threshold in weeks 13–39 (‘Week 12 + 0.1 lice’, ‘Week 12 98% + 0.1 lice’, Table 1). The coordinated treatment was in week 12 to reduce the louse level before the period with a lower treatment threshold.

By growing the salmonids to a larger size before stocking in open net pens, exposure to salmon lice is reduced. A larger size at stocking than what has been usual in the past (around 0.1–0.2 kg) can be achieved by keeping the juvenile ﬁsh in land facilities for a longer period or in closed or semi-closed systems in the sea (Nilsen et al., 2017, 2020). To investigate the effects of this strategy, we examined scenarios with stocking at 0.5 kg, 1 kg or 2 kg. This was done by removing ﬁsh smaller than the assumed stocking size from the simulations and retaining the remaining parts of the time series. The model was then run with the resulting longer fallowing and shorter production period. In the ‘Stock 0.5 kg ﬁxed end’ scenario (Table 1), we ﬁxed the end time of the production cycles, so that the timing of production (after 0.5 kg) was identical to the ‘Zonation’ scenario (Supplementary Fig. S1). In the other scenarios (‘Stock 0.5 kg ﬁxed start’, ‘Stock 1 kg’, ‘Stock 2 kg’, Table 1), we ﬁxed the start time of the production cycles by shifting the time series forward (Supplementary Fig. S1). On average across the time period, stocking at 0.5 kg, 1 kg or 2 kg reduced the total biomass of salmonids in the sea by, respectively, 2, 9 or 29% compared with the historic data and the ‘Zonation’ scenario (Supplementary Table S1). To investigate if the effects of large size at stocking scaled proportionally to the proportion of farms using this strategy, we additionally considered scenarios where half or a quarter of the farms stocked ﬁsh at 1 kg (‘Stock 1 kg half’, ‘Stock 1 kg quart’, Table 1). In each simulation in the ‘Stock 1 kg half’ scenario, we randomly selected seven northern and two southern farms that used this strategy (i.e. 53% of the farms), and in the ‘Stock 1 kg quart’ scenario, three northern and one southern farm (24% of the farms).

By reducing the biomass of farmed salmonids in open-net pens, the louse population in the area is also reduced. Such biomass reductions can be achieved by the use of closed or semi-closed production systems (Nilsen et al., 2017, 2020), and can also be dictated by the trafﬁc light system. In ﬁve scenarios we investigated if effects of such reductions scaled proportionally with the proportion of biomass removed and if the effects depended on whether whole farms were removed or an equal proportion of biomass in each farm (Table 1). In the ‘Closed half’ and ‘Closed quart’ scenarios, we randomly removed half or a quarter of the farms (as described for the ‘Stock 1 kg half’, ‘Stock 1 kg quart’ scenarios). In the ‘Downscale eighth’, ‘Downscale quart’ and ‘Downscale half’ scenarios we removed, respectively, 12, 25 and 50% of the ﬁsh in each farm. The reduction in abundance was done at the same proportion at all times in the production cycle (based on the ‘Zonation’ scenario).

All simulations and analyses were done in the R language and environment for statistical computing version 4.0.3 (R Core Team, 2020, R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria, https://www.R-project.org/).

3. Results

3.1. Assessment of a regional louse control goal

The relationship between the total number of salmon lice in the farms in the area in spring and average salmon louse-induced mortality in the salmon-producing rivers in the area is shown in Fig. 5. The diagonal grey line shows the linear regression line (forced through origin). From this line, we see that 10% mortality corresponds approximately to 0.3 million adult female salmon lice. Note

that a formal test of the statistical signiﬁcance of this relationship is not meaningful, as the numbers of adult female salmon lice in each farm (i.e. the data summarised on the X-axis) are key input data to the virtual post-smolt model (generating the values shown on the Y-axis). Nonetheless, the result suggests that to reduce salmon louse-induced mortality to lower than 10%, the total number of adult female salmon lice in spring has to be reduced to lower than approximately 0.3 million.

- 3.2. Assessment of realism of scenario simulations


The hindcast scenario representing historic production cycles seemed to simulate the dynamics realistically, as the seasonal and interannual dynamics in salmon louse abundance and treatment frequency generally resembled the dynamics in the reported data (Fig. 6A, B). The number of adult female lice was on average 17% higher in the simulation than in the reported data. No such comparison can be made for treatment frequency, as cage-level data are not reported and it is unknown how large proportion of the cages were treated each treatment week. The differences between simulated and historic dynamics can at least partly be explained by the simpliﬁed louse control scheme used in the simulations, which was necessary for comparing louse control scenarios in a standardised way. For example, it is possible that many farmers used a lower treatment threshold than the regulatory limit and that other control measures such as cleaner ﬁsh were used. The higher average louse level in simulated compared with reported data is unlikely to inﬂuence the interpretation of differences between simulated control strategies, but may imply that slightly less control effort may be needed to reach the goal of 0.3 million adult female lice in spring in reality than in the simulations. On the other hand, the louse level seems to depend more closely on the biomass of farmed ﬁsh in the simulations than in the reported data, which could have the opposite effect since several of the control strategies involve reducing the amount of ﬁsh in open-net pens. - 3.3. Hindcast scenario with coordinated production and fallowing

The comparison of the ‘Zonation’ scenario with the historic scenario quantiﬁed the effects of coordinated production and fallowing. The results showed that this strategy reduced the total number of adult female salmon lice by 12%, from an overall average of 1.36 million to 1.20 million adult females (Fig. 6B, C). The treatment frequency decreased by 11%, from 8.5 to 7.6% of the cages treated per week (Fig. 6B, C). The interannual variability in spring increased (Fig. 6B, C): in spring in odd-numbered years, the abundance decreased from 0.54 million to 0.06 million adult females, whereas in spring in even-numbered years, the abundance increased from 0.73 million to 1.06 million adult females. - 3.4. Coordination of other louse control measures


The ‘Skirt’ scenarios simulated preventive louse control measures that stopped 50% of the external salmon louse larvae. If all farms used this strategy all the time (‘Skirt all’ scenario), the total number of adult female lice was reduced by 29% compared with the reference scenario (‘Zonation’) and the number of louse treatments by 35% (Table 2 and Supplementary Fig. S2). The strategy reduced the number of adult female salmon lice in spring in even-numbered years to 0.84 million, which was far from the goal of 0.3 million adult females (Table 1). The reduction in weekly treatment frequency from 7.6% to 4.9% corresponds to a reduction from 4.0 to 2.5 treatments per year for each ﬁsh. If a random subset of 53% of the farms used this strategy, the reduction in louse numbers was 16% and the reduction in treatments 19% (‘Skirt half’ scenario, Table 1 and Supplementary Fig. S2). - 4. Discussion


For the salmonid farming industry to be allowed to increase production in Norway, the negative impact of farm-origin salmon lice on wild salmonid populations must be reduced to acceptable levels. In the current implementation of the trafﬁc light system for regulation of salmonid aquaculture, this requirement implies that salmon louse-induced mortality of out-migrating wild salmon post-smolts must be reduced to less than 10%. In the Nordfjord study area, the estimated salmon louse-induced mortality in recent years has been approximately 20% on average (Fig. 5). Hence, this mortality needs to be halved to reach acceptable levels. Within the range of variation seen in Nordfjord, the relationship between the total population of adult female salmon lice in spring and salmon louse-induced mortality is approximately linear according to the virtual post-smolt model. That means that the virtual post-smolt

model suggests that the total population of adult female salmon lice in spring needs to be halved, to approximately 0.3 million adult females (Fig. 5). In other parts of western Norway, larger reductions may be needed. The average of estimated salmon louseinduced mortality among all rivers in PA4, the production area that Nordfjord is part of, typically varies around approximately 30% according to the annual expert assessments for the trafﬁc light system (Vollset et al., 2023b). The average of estimated salmon louseinduced mortality in the production area to the south, PA3, has been assessed to be above 30% during most years recently (Vollset et al., 2023b). On the other hand, in most parts of Norway, the salmon louse-induced mortality is typically assessed to be either between 10 and 30% or lower than 10% (Vollset et al., 2023b), i.e., similar to or lower than the estimates from Nordfjord. The magnitude of the salmon louse problem in the Nordfjord area is thus comparatively high but not the highest in Norway.

The present results suggested that coordinated production and fallowing according to the proposed zonation scheme is likely to result in fewer lice and louse treatments on average. This result may both be caused by the coordination per se (Murray and Salama, 2016) and by the redistribution of the biomass over fewer farms (Qviller et al., 2024). The results further suggested that the zonation scheme is likely to lead to larger variability between years. When many farms are in the second year of production, the salmon louse problem is aggravated compared with if the production cycles are not coordinated, as also found in other areas (Guarracino et al., 2018). The simulation was based on a proposed zonation scheme developed in a regional initiative involving authorities and farmers. It was beyond the scope of our study to investigate if other zonation schemes than the one proposed could have alleviated this problem. With the proposed zonation, the salmon louse impacts are likely to alter between insigniﬁcant in oddnumbered years and unacceptably high in even-numbered years, instead of being moderate in most years. Although the average impact is lower, it is unclear which alternative causes the largest threat to wild salmon populations. The net beneﬁt of the proposed scheme is therefore questionable, and is likely to depend on which other measures could be taken to level off the large variability between years.

A predominant outcome from the present simulations is that any control measure that resulted in lasting reductions in salmon louse infestations to acceptable levels for wild salmonids, involved reducing the density of farmed salmonids in open cages. Of the strategies tested, only stocking of 1 kg or larger ﬁsh reduced salmon louse levels in spring in even-numbered years to below 0.3 million adult females, i.e., the threshold for acceptably low salmon louse impact. The success of this scenario depended on the assumption that production could be timed to minimise impacts on wild ﬁsh. In practice, farmers also need to consider the profitability and feasibility of the strategy, e.g. the availability of large

ﬁsh for stocking. Our results nonetheless demonstrate the large potential of stocking larger ﬁsh as a strategy for reducing impacts on wild ﬁsh. Also scenarios with closed or semi-closed production systems with no louse emissions in half of the farms contributed to major reductions in salmon louse infestations. However, by converting to closed or semi-closed production systems (Nilsen et al., 2017, 2020) in randomly selected subsets of the farms, more than half of farms would need to use such technology to achieve an acceptable impact on wild ﬁsh. The large variation between simulations with different subsets of farms selected, however, suggests that the selection of farms to close will have an impact. If farms are selected strategically to close those with large contributions to infestation pressure, high connectivity to other farms and/or proximity to wild salmonid migration pathways, a lower proportion of farms may need to close (Urke et al., 2021; Qviller et al., 2024). Downscaling production by 50% was less effective than the closed cage strategy, although the overall mean number of adult female lice was reduced by more than 50%. The difference between downscaled production and closed farm strategies is that the latter strategy decreases the number of farms with open production and thus increases distances between farms. This agrees with the earlier analytical outcome suggested in Aldrin et al. (2019), that few farms with many ﬁsh and large between-farm distances is effective in minimising the overall output of salmon louse infestation.

Coordinated use of a highly effective louse treatment in spring brought the louse level down to close to 0.3 million adult females. The effect was transient, however, and did not persist into summer. Hence, this strategy might be insufﬁcient for reducing impacts on sea trout, which also reside in coastal areas in summer (Finstad et al., 2021). Coordinated use of a highly effective treatment may also have consequences for resistance development, which was not investigated in this study. Low louse levels in both spring and summer were achieved by combining the coordinated use of a highly effective treatment in spring and a lower treatment

threshold through spring and summer. A lower treatment threshold came, nonetheless, with the cost of requiring more frequent treatments, with associated potential negative effects on the welfare and survival of the farmed ﬁsh, as well as increased selection pressure towards resistance (Jansen et al., 2016; Stien et al., 2020; Østevik et al., 2022; Thompson et al., 2024; Tvete et al., 2023). This ﬁnding contrasts a recent modelling study ﬁnding that reducing the treatment threshold did not lead to more frequent treatments (Kragesteen et al., 2023). Possible reasons for this difference are the inﬂuences of lice from other areas and counting errors. Farms from other areas than Nordfjord contributed with approximately 11% of the louse larvae according to the historic data, and necessarily more than 11% in scenarios that succeeded in signiﬁcantly reducing louse levels. Due to this external supply of louse larvae, it may be a futile goal to sustain a low louse level, no matter how many louse treatments are used. A possible way to reduce the treatment frequency is hence to induce more farms to collaborate in using a lower treatment threshold (Kragesteen et al., 2019). However, counting errors (not included in the model by Kragesteen et al., 2023) may also contribute to increased treatment frequency at a low treatment threshold. This is because the proportion of false positive louse counts, i.e. above-threshold counts despite below-threshold true louse abundance, necessarily will increase, while lowering treatment thresholds (Dohoo et al., 2009).

Louse skirts or other preventive technologies that stopped 50% of the external larvae were not sufﬁcient for reducing salmon louse numbers to a level consistent with low impact on wild salmon in this area. In areas with less severe salmon louse problems, however, this strategy might be feasible. Furthermore, the strategy may be useful in combination with other strategies, such as use of cleaner ﬁsh, which were not investigated in the present study. We also note that reducing salmon louse treatments by using louse skirts can be important for improving farmed ﬁsh welfare. The dynamic and environmentally responsive use of shielding technology, together with deep lights and feeding to reduce host-parasite encounters, is particularly promising in this regard (Oldham, 2023; Oldham et al., 2023). Use of louse skirts has furthermore been found to be a cost-effective method for sea louse control (Boerlage et al., 2024).

The present simulation study also has some limitations. There is large stochastic variability in louse dynamics, which leads to considerable uncertainty in scenario simulations. There are also large uncertainties in many parameters in the model used for the simulations due to uncertainties in the data used for parameterisation, e.g. louse counts and treatments (Aldrin et al., 2023). Furthermore, we have not included the use of cleaner ﬁsh as a control measure in the present study, even though cleaner ﬁsh are widely in use to control salmon lice in farms (Overton et al., 2020). There are also large uncertainties in the tolerance of wild salmon and sea trout to salmon louse infestations, and thus the estimates of louseinduced mortality of migrating post-smolts of salmon (Vollset et al., 2023b). The calculation of salmon louse-induced mortality is, however, based on the best available knowledge and in line with assessments in the trafﬁc light system. We argue therefore that the present simulations should reﬂect whether the outcome of the various control strategies would indicate acceptable, moderate or unacceptable levels of infestation pressure of salmon lice in the Nordfjord area, as evaluated in the trafﬁc light system.
