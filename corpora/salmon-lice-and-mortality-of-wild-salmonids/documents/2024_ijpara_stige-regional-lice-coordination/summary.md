# Summary: Stige et al. (2024)

**Full title:** Effects of regional coordination of salmon louse control in reducing negative impacts of salmonid aquaculture on wild salmonids  
**Journal:** International Journal for Parasitology, 54: 463–474  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.ijpara.2024.04.003  
**Document type:** Modelling / management scenario study (hindcast)  
**Priority questions:** Q2, Q5, Q8, Q10

---

## What this paper does

The authors use eight years of mandatory Norwegian farm data (2015–2022) from **19 salmonid locations** in and around **Nordfjord** (production area 4, Vestland) to ask how regional **coordination of louse control** could reduce impacts on **nine local salmon rivers** enough to meet **Traffic Light System (TLS)** targets.

Two linked models are used:

1. **NVI virtual post-smolt model** (Stige et al. 2022) — links farm-reported lice to **lice-induced mortality** of out-migrating wild post-smolts.  
2. **Stage-structured farm lice model** (Aldrin et al.) — simulates lice dynamics and treatments across connected farms under alternative **hindcast scenarios** (all else equal vs. observed history).

“Acceptable” impact is defined as TLS **green light**: modelled post-smolt mortality **<10%**.

---

## Main findings

### Scale of the problem in Nordfjord

- Recent average modelled **post-smolt mortality ~20%** — about **double** the green-light target (<10%).  
- Linear VPS regression (2012–2022): **~0.3 million** spring adult females (weeks 16–21, 19 farms) ≈ 10% mortality.  
- Historic mean spring lice **1.36 million** adult females; zonation scenario **1.20 million** (−12%) but even-year spring **1.06 million** vs odd-year **0.06 million**.

### Coordinated production and fallowing (“zonation”)

- A **regionally proposed zonation plan** (coordinated production starts and fallowing) was hindcast against historical data.  
- **Benefits:** lower **mean** lice abundance and **fewer treatments** overall.  
- **Costs:** **higher interannual variability** in spring lice — especially **unacceptably high** levels in **even-numbered years** when many farms are in **year two** of production (cf. Guarracino et al. 2018).  
- Authors conclude the **net benefit of zonation alone is questionable**; impacts may alternate between insignificant (odd years) and unacceptable (even years).

### Other coordinated measures (combined with zonation)

Scenarios included coordinated **lice skirts** (50% larval blocking), **spring treatments** (98% efficacy), **lower treatment thresholds**, **shorter cycles**, and variants of **larger smolt stocking**, **closed/semi-closed units**, and **downscaled biomass**.

- **Skirt all** (−29% lice, 0.84 M spring even years) and **zonation alone** insufficient for even years.  
- **Week 15, 98% treatment**: spring lice **0.37 M** (−66%); **week 12 98% + 0.1 threshold**: **0.42 M** spring, **0.34 M** weeks 13–39 — still marginal vs 0.3 M goal.  
- **Stock 1 kg**: spring even-year lice **0.27 M** (−75%, below goal); treatments −23%.  
- **Closed half** of farms: **0.40 M** spring (−63%); **50% downscale** per farm: **0.48 M** (−54%) — less effective than closed units (increased inter-farm distance).  
- **89%** of infestation pressure originated within the 19-farm study area; **100** stochastic simulation replicates per scenario.

---

## Limitations

- **Single fjord case study**; PA4 average TLS mortality assessments are often **~30%** — Nordfjord is high but not the worst in Norway.  
- Hindcast **counterfactuals**, not experimental management.  
- Inherits **VPS calibration uncertainties** (Stige 2022; Jansen & Gjerde 2021 debate).  
- Resistance, treatment welfare, and **economic feasibility** are not modelled quantitatively.
