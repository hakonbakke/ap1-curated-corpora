# Salmon lice biology, environmental factors, and smolt behaviour with implications for the Norwegian salmon farming management system: A critical review

## Solveig van Nes1 | Albert Kjartan Dagbjartarson Imsland2,3 | Simon R. M. Jones4

1Marine Prospects AS, Oslo, Norway 2Akvaplan-niva Iceland Office, Kópavogur, Iceland 3Department of Biological Sciences, University of Bergen, High Technology Centre, Bergen, Norway 4Fisheries and Oceans Canada, Pacific Biological Station, Nanaimo, British Columbia, Canada

Abstract

In 2017, a new regulatory management system, the traffic light system (TLS), was implemented to estimate the effects of salmon lice from Norwegian salmon aquaculture on marine survival of wild Atlantic salmon and forms the basis of aquaculture capacity regulation. The TLS relies on observational data and a set of models to estimate the risk for negative impact on wild salmon populations. This review of the literature that forms the basis for the TLS as well as other relevant studies is presented in the context of the currently practiced TLS and suggestions are made for immediate and long-term improvements. The main findings of this review are that: (1) assumed timing and duration of smolt migration contribute to unreliable observational and modelled data and overestimates of infection pressure; (2) production of lice larvae from farmed salmon is overestimated; (3) TLS model systems rely on or are calibrated by the same potentially flawed data; (4) lice-associated mortality in wild salmon smolts may be overestimated; and (5) lice infection levels on farms are not associated with measurable effects on wild salmon. Recommendations to improve the accuracy and reliability of the TLS, and hence its environmental efficiency include the more complete use of available biological and physical environmental variables, adjusting the time period that observational data are registered and modelled data are integrated, adjusting the interpretation of data including recognition of uncertainty in model outcomes, and use of more realistic assumptions concerning lice-induced mortality thresholds.

salmon lice infections on marine cultured salmonids are well documented, and concerns are that these infections pose a threat to the conservation of wild salmon.5–9 In 2017, the “traffic light system” (TLS) was implemented as a new regulatory tool for the management of Atlantic salmon and rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) aquaculture, with the aim to promote growth while increasing environmental sustainability. The TLS divides the coast of Norway into 13 production areas (PAs, Figure 1),10 in which the estimated lethal effects of salmon lice from aquaculture on wild populations of Atlantic salmon are used as indicators of environmental sustainability and hence, for capacity regulation in aquaculture.11 These estimated effects are separately categorized within each PA such that 0–10%, 10–30%, or >30% increased risks of marine mortality are assigned to low (green), moderate (yellow), or high (red) categories, respectively. A green category allows for increased production biomass, whereas yellow or red categories result in no-change or reduced biomass in that PA, respectively.11 Since impacts of farm-origin lice on wild salmon populations cannot be directly measured, the TLS relies on observational data, estimates and models,12,13 which are interpreted by an expert group (EG) charged with the assessment of estimated risk of salmon lice-induced mortality in each PA on a yearly basis. In the process all available evidence used in the TLS are considered along with the uncertainty in each line of evidence to build a single combined evaluation. The EG advises the Ministry which decides on the appropriate green, yellow, or red “traffic light” category for each PA bi-annually.11

The TLS comprises observational data and various models (Figure 2). The observational data include lice counts from individual wild migrating salmon smolts (trawl), wild sea trout (Salmo trutta) (traps) and cultured salmon smolt (sentinel cages). The models estimate lice density, dispersal and infestation on migrating smolts.14–17 These models are informed by numbers of farm-derived infectious lice larvae which are estimated from farm reported numbers of fish, adult female lice per fish and sea temperature.18 The models are calibrated against the observational data. Observed and modelled data (lice load on migrating smolt) are in turn extrapolated from a threshold for salmon lice-induced mortality for individual fish19 to a likely low, moderate or high population effect (lice mortality impact index).20

In this review, we focus on literature describing salmon lice biology, environmental factors, and smolt behaviour that are of relevance for accuracy and reliability of the TLS and of value to the expert community, policy makers and managers.

- 2 | METHODS

We have systematically reviewed the available scientific and grey literature and related datasets that are referred to in the scientific framework that forms the basis for the TLS. Further, search strings were adapted to extend the literature basis and to also include additional relevant studies on interactions between environmental factors, salmon lice dynamics and pathogenicity, and salmon smolt behaviour that were absent in the scientific framework of the TLS.

Based on this literature review, relevant knowledge is presented in the context of four key elements which we consider affect the accuracy and reliability of the currently practiced TLS:

- I. observational data,
- II. estimated production of infective lice larvae from fish farms,
- III. models estimating lice dispersal and infection level of migrating smolts, and
- IV. interpretation of lice burden into a likely mortality effect on individual and population level.


We discuss how assumptions, limitations and uncertainties in these four key points affect the outcome and reliability of assessment end points and how these will influence the reliability of the TLS. Further, based on reviewed literature we provide suggestions for immediate and more long-term improvements that are likely to substantially improve accuracy and increase reliability of the TLS.

3.1.1 | Temporal considerations: Timing and duration of registration and integration of data

Whereas monitoring until the second half of June and the use of two successive monitoring periods are adapted by NALO to reflect divergent life history strategies of all wild salmonid species in Norway,21 data from both periods are also used in the TLS estimation of increased mortality risk for migrating Atlantic salmon smolts.12,13 As will be discussed, assumed duration of migration and inclusion of data from the later time period most likely biases the outcome of lice effects assessments.

- 3.1.3 | The effects of fish size, swimming speed, and water current on the reliability of observational data


In addition to spatio-temporal considerations, the larger smolts used in the sentinel cages (80–100 g) may introduce an increased bias of risk since they are four to five times heavier than wild migrating smolts and infestations are reported as the number of lice per fish without correcting for fish size.13 Also sampled sea trout are substantially larger (average weight of 154 g for sampled sea trout in PA4 during 2019),21 and fish up to 150 g are included in mortality estimates.13 Experimental and field studies demonstrate that the risk of infection is positively correlated to body size.42–45 Since both cultured smolt used in sentinel cages and sampled sea trout are significantly larger they thus have a greater likeliness to become infected as compared to the smaller migrating wild smolt, and data obtained from sentinel cages should be corrected for fish size.

Another source of uncertainty is how well lice counts on fish in sentinel cages represent lice infestations on actively swimming fish. Both natural fish swimming behaviour and water currents are likely affected by the cage size and structure, and studies have shown that swimming speed and water flow highly influence infestation rates.46–51 Samsing49 considered parasite contact time (estimated from swimming speed) and host encounter rate in lice attachment

success and concluded that infection rates were highest at intermediate swimming speeds. A study considering fish size and velocity showed that the smallest fish, of moderate velocity, were most likely susceptible to infection. However, the study concludes that emigrating salmon are most likely to swim above a threshold speed that contributes to reduced infection rate and time of exposure.50 This may imply that rapid swimming by salmon migrating out of coastal waters, usually described as adaptive against predation, could also be adaptive against parasitism. Infestation rates were also highest at the typical swimming speed of farmed salmon in coastal fish farms, which may be a hitherto unrecognized factor contributing to L. salmonis epidemics. Since the cages are stationary local prevailing currents and fish behaviour can bias infections either way compared to wild migrating smolt, adding another source of unreliability to the data from sentinel cages. This is problematic since data from sentinel cages represent both a separate indicator and forms the basis for calibration of data in two additional indicators (Figure 2).

3.1.4 | Sea trout as proxy for infestation pressure on migrating salmon

One of seven indicators used in the TLS to predict population level effects in Atlantic salmon is the infestation levels on wild sea trout caught in net traps as a proxy for the infestation level on migrating wild salmon13,14 (Figure 2). The relatively late timing for registration of lice levels on sea trout captured by trap-netting alone is likely to contribute to overestimates of salmon lice levels, since salmon lice levels increase with temperature after the majority of migrating salmon smolt have left the fjord. In addition, it can be argued that sea trout as species with very different life-history strategy as compared to salmon in general is not a suitable proxy for salmon. For instance, whereas salmon smolt swim relatively fast off-shore, sea trout typically reside in the near shore environment and spend more time in the littoral zone than salmon13,22 where lice larvae accumulate.39–41 Sea trout may therefore accumulate higher lice loads when compared with migrating salmon smolt. More recently, infections on sea trout, expressed per gram host weight, were negatively correlated with the numbers of salmon returning to two rivers in the Osterfjord system.8 Earlier, however,51 the statistical significance of correlations between infections on the sea trout captured in the outer fjord and infestation pressure from farmed salmon varied according to temperature and farm production cycle indicating more research is required characterize the epizootiology of sea lice in this system. Also, as previously specified the average weight of sea trout caught with nets/traps is significantly larger than migrating Atlantic salmon smolt,21 and since the likelihood for fish to become infested is positively correlated to body size,33,43–45 lice load on sea trout is not representative for a likely lice load on salmon smolt. The low predictive value of infestations on sea trout was confirmed in a study using data from four fjords over 12 years which demonstrated that sea lice abundance on sea trout was higher than on salmon and that there is a poor correlation between lice levels on trout and salmon.52 The uncertainty relating to

In summary, data from trawl, sentinel cages, and sea trout traps have several sources of uncertainty which separately or collectively are likely to contribute to significant overestimates of the infection pressure and thus mortality risk in three of the assessment end points. Since all modelled data are calibrated against observational data, this uncertainty and overestimation potentially also affects the four remaining outputs (Figure 2). Accordingly, in Section 4 of this review, we suggest specific improvements that could improve accuracy and increase reliability of the observational data and hence the assessments made in the TLS.

- 3.2 | Reliability of models estimating lice dispersal, infection level, and mortality of migrating smolts


Four of the assessment end points are products of a set of complex models (columns 3–6, Figure 2). The Institute of Marine Research (IMR) dispersion model (indicator column IMR infestation pressure) estimates infestation pressure in a given geographic area which is translated into increased mortality risk for wild populations in categories green, yellow, and red.14 Whereas the three assessment end points IMR, Norwegian Veterinary Institute (NVI), and SINTEF smolt are the outcome of sets of models developed by IMR,15 NVI,16 and the research organization SINTEF.17 Although these virtual smolt models are based on different assumptions and methods in the chain of events, all models simulate salmon lice infection on seaward migrating smolt by estimating the spatiotemporal distribution of salmon lice larvae and the likely resulting infestations on virtual post-smolt that swim from the mouth of the river to the open sea. In turn, mortality is estimated as a function of the simulated concentration and summed up for the whole PA. Hence, the final impact assessment for each PA relies mainly on estimates resulting from various models (Figure 2).

Due to their complexity, a detailed discussion of each model is outside the scope of this review. Although the models have common sources of uncertainty which can impair the quality of their outcomes, and which will be further discussed below in general terms.

3.2.1 | Reliability of estimates of farm origin infestation pressure

All models cover a chain of events starting with estimates of the production of eggs and resulting infectious lice larvae from salmonid fish farms based on the same method as described by Stien et al.18 and thus rely on the same input data and assumptions (Figure 2). In their assessments, the EG considers the estimates of farm origin lice larvae to be reliable for most PAs.12,13 Here, we highlight the effects of assumptions and simplifications on the reliability of the estimated production of sea lice eggs and larvae.

Assumption 1. The number of reproducing female lice at an aquaculture site at a given time is the product of the reported average of sexually mature female lice and number of fish at a given site.

In Norway, the maximum thresholds for number of adult female lice per fish in salmonid fish farms are mandatory, and on each production site, it is a legal requirement to count lice weekly or bi-weekly when the sea temperature is above or under 4 C, respectively.53 Immediately prior to and during smolt migration, mean lice counts should not exceed 0.2 adult females per fish, and for the remainder of the year the threshold is 0.5. Hence, farmed salmon rarely carry high adult lice loads and in the weeks prior to and during smolt migration, loads are at maximum 0.2. At mean counts of 0.2 and 0.5 adult females per fish, indications are that the proportion of reproductively active lice are approximately 18–33% and 40–60%, respectively54 (Table 1). Thus, in contradiction to the linear relationship previously suggested55 and what is practiced in the TLS,18 salmon lice reproduction is not linear with female abundance and at low lice loads on farmed fish only a small proportion of adult female lice are reproductive. For instance, in the Sognefjord in 2019 (https://lusedata.hubocean.earth/), the average number of adult female lice per fish was below 0.1 prior to smolt migration, suggesting that the proportion of reproductively active females during the smolt migration was between 10 and 17.5%,54 in contrast to 100% currently practiced in the TLS. The assumption that all adult female lice are reproductively active contributes to an overestimate of salmon lice egg production from fish farms.

Assumption 2. The number of eggs produced in a PA is the product of the number of fish in farms and the registered number of adult female lice on all salmonids in farms within each PA (and all eggs are viable).

In addition to the assumption that all mature female lice are fertilized, it is assumed that all adult females produce an average of 300 viable eggs (2 egg-strings containing 150 eggs) per cycle. However, the number of eggs produced or hatched may be a poor predictor of the resulting number of viable naupliar lice larvae. Assumption 3. Daily mortality rates for lice larvae are constant, and independent of salinity and predation.

Free-swimming salmon louse larvae develop through two noninfectious nauplii stages to the infective copepodid stage.59,60 Whereas the effects of temperature on developmental rates of the different larval stages are included in the estimates of resulting lice larvae, daily mortality rates are assumed to be constant and independent of environmental factors18 (Table 1). In contrast to attached parasitic stages, free-swimming larval lice stages are more sensitive to low salinity, potentially due to the absence of dietary ions and the increased energetic demands to cope with hyposaline stress.34,47 Nauplius larvae are less active and do not moult to copepodids at salinities <25 psu, only developing successfully at ≥30 psu.32 Although some development to the copepodid stage occurs from salinities of 25 psu and greater, even at full strength sea water of 35 psu only 18.33% of incubated eggs reach the copepodid stage,58 which agrees with the previous mentioned mortality rates suggested

by Stucchi et al.35 The same study proposed that at 25 psu, only 0.9% of the eggs are likely to develop to the copepodid stages, yielding a daily mortality rate of 1.11 d 1.35 An inverse relationship between reduced salinity and larval survival time was demonstrated by Bricknell et al.34 who expressed the tolerance for reduced salinity

- as a reduction in median survival time of copepodids. Only small differences were observed between salinities of 33 and 29 psu, but a dramatic reduction in mean survival time was evident when salinity was reduced from 29 to 26 psu, causing a reduction of survival time from 24 to 11 h. Thus, lice larvae experience reduced activity, impaired development at <30 psu, and severely compromised survival at salinities <27 psu with a halving of survival time at salinities of 26 psu, and no successful development from nauplius to copepodid stage at salinities ≤25 psu32,34,58 (Figure 6). Supporting these findings, changes in gene-expression in copepodids following a relatively brief hyposaline exposure revealed a threshold of transcription
- at 27 psu consistent with distinct stress responses, which was greatest at 25 psu.61 Despite this, the TLS assumes that the mortality rate of lice larvae is independent of changes in salinity and based on experimental studies performed at salinities >30 psu.18 A recent study testing salinity preferences of lice larval stages also demonstrated that nauplii almost completely avoided salinities below 30 psu, while copepodids were more tolerant of lower salinities37 indicating the importance of discriminating lice stages in mortality estimates and need for more data on salinity-dependent mortality. The TLS assumes daily mortality rates of 0.17 d 1 (nauplius) and 0.22 d 1 (infective copepodid) for larvae derived from aquaculture18 (Table 1). However more recent studies report considerably higher mortality rates for nauplii ( 0.32)35 and for nauplii and copepodids ( 0.30)61 (Table 1). Stucchi et al.35 derived their estimates from several experimental studies whereas in Aldrin et al.,62 the mortality rate was based on data from multiple salmon farms and also accounted for larvae that drifted from the farm.

Also, a significant interaction effect between salinity and temperature was demonstrated by Stige et al.,64 who concluded that lice abundance was most strongly reduced when both salinity and temperature were suboptimal. That is, low temperatures were estimated to have the strongest negative effect on lice abundance when salinity was also low, and high temperatures appeared only to have adverse effects when salinity was low.

In addition, the TLS does not consider mortality of lice larvae due to predation. The nauplius I, nauplius II and copepodid lice stages are part of the plankton food web, and as such are exposed to predation. Generally, zooplankton experience an estimated 67–75% loss of biomass (mortality) attributed to predation65 (Table 1). Despite an absence of direct evidence for predation, losses of free-living lice larvae due to predation is likely to be substantial.36 For instance, low infestation levels during infection trials in commercial size sea cages were associated with a high density of Ctenophora which may have caused high predation of free-swimming lice larvae.66

In summary, erroneous assumptions regarding lice recruitment and mortality may accumulate and result in substantial uncertainties. Several parameters are likely overestimated in the recruitment and development of lice (Table 1), and factors likely to have a negative effect on lice recruitment and survival according to reviewed literature are not included in the TLS. Therefore, TLS-driven assessments likely overestimate the production and survival of lice larvae from fish farms, leading to an overestimation of farm derived infection pressure and resulting smolt mortality.

Further, the evidence supports including salinity and temperature as separate variables that should be implemented in modelling of salmon lice dynamics for management purposes. It is also worth noting that the White paper which forms the basis for the implemented TLS specifies the following: “Lice levels will however also vary with factors over which the industry has no influence, such as sea temperature and water salinity. The assessments needed and the models used will therefore have to take such variations into account.”11

- 3.2.2 | Reliability of environmental parameters key to dispersal model


For the IMR dispersal model,14 the assumptions regarding (i) avoidance behaviour of salmon lice, and (ii) a fixed/linear infection rate contribute to additional sources of insecurity in modelled outcome.

Salinity related avoidance behaviour

There is considerable evidence that larval sea lice avoid hyposaline waters, and a recent study demonstrated that whereas nauplii almost completely avoided salinities below 30 psu, the infective copepodid stage was more tolerant of lower salinities and a few copepodids were found above the 16–20 psu halocline.37 These findings were implemented in the IMR dispersal model from 2019 and onwards.13–15,30,31

Although as outlined in Section 3.2.1, several experimental studies indicate survival of free-swimming lice larvae is compromised and stress-related genes are upregulated with exposure to salinities <27 psu32,33,36,58,61 suggesting an increase in avoidance behaviour of lice is likely to appear starting from salinities of 27 psu. Viewing the swimming depth profiles of smolts in figure 2 of Plantalech Manel-la et al.63 and interpreting the likely spatial overlap of smolts and copepodids with the data presented in Crosbie et al.,37 the smolt swimming depth and environmental data may suggest there would be spatial overlap between out-migrating salmon smolts and copepodids in or around the halocline. However, the majority of smolt migration occurs at depths with salinity gradients likely to increase copepodid avoidance behaviour32–34,58,61 and it is likely that the IMR dispersal model in addition to overestimating production of farm derived infective copepodids (Section 3.2.1) also overestimates the share of the produced and surviving lice that are likely to reside in (and not avoid) the same water masses as migrating smolt.

A linear infection rate independent of environmental factors

The assumption by the model that infection efficiency is independent of environmental parameters and only dependent on the estimated concentration of infective lice larvae (copepodids) in the water masses14 is contradicted by available information. The reviewed literature indicates that infection success is highly influenced by several parameters such as salinity, temperature, current speed and smolt swimming speed. For instance, an inverse relationship between salinity and infection success was demonstrated in experimental studies with Atlantic salmon, where infections with copepodids at 34 and 24 psu consistently resulted in reduced settlement success and slower louse development at 24 psu.67 This is in accordance with experiments demonstrating that infection levels at 26 psu were 45% of those at 34 psu, and the levels of resulting chalimus also was significantly lower at 26 psu.34 These observations suggest that reduced salinity levels in upper water layers during smolt migration, as demonstrated in Hardanger and Sognefjord27,63 negatively influence copepodids ability to locate and/or respond to a host cue as well as their

ability to stay attached and moult into the next developmental stage. An inverse relationship between salinity and the recruitment and infectivity of salmon lice larvae is also supported by observational studies of lice levels on Atlantic salmon in fish farms68–70 and in wild salmonid and non-salmonid species.71–73

Also, experimental studies have demonstrated that infection success is highly influenced by temperature. A significant increase in copepodid settlement on host fish at 12 C as compared to 7 C has been demonstrated.67 Similarly, studies performed at 5 and 10 C demonstrated that average infestation rate on Atlantic salmon smolt increased from about 2 to 53% at 5 and 10 C, respectively.74 A similar positive correlation was demonstrated with infection success increasing from 20 to 50% from lowest (3 C) to highest (10 C) temperatures, and fish where infected at 10 C harboured twice as many copepodites as fish infected at 3 C.75 In addition, infectivity nearly doubles from 5 to 10 C, and is almost 3 times greater at 15 as compared to 5 C.76 Even though these studies report of varying magnitude of effects, most likely due to differences in experimental design, all studies were performed at similar and optimal salinities and demonstrate significant effects of temperature on infection success.

A recent study also evaluated infection success and measured effects of temperature on the infectivity of the copepodid and on its ability to stay attached and moult into the chalimus stage. Trials were performed at 4, 8, and 12 C and demonstrated that chalimus levels at 8 and 12 C were three and four times higher as compared to 4 C.77

In summary, the assumption that estimated number of produced copepodids equates with the number of infective copepodids which contribute to the host mortality effect is most likely erroneous. Measurable parameters such as salinity and temperature highly influence the ability of lice to infest and survive on the host, and failing to include these factors as variables in modelling salmon lice dynamics contributes to unreliable model outcomes.

3.2.3 | Reliability of modelled outcomes is affected by time period chosen for smolt migration

The reliability of the outcome of all four models depends upon the assumed time period for migration, and also on the average time smolt spend in the fjord. The chosen time for migration is of essence since copepodid infection pressure increases with season and increasing temperatures. Also, the assumed mean residence time for smolt affects the exposure time to estimated infectious lice larvae and hence the modelled infection levels and predicted mortality in all four models. In the TLS assumed residence time for PA4 is 28 days,13 whereas measurements in two separate fjord systems in PA4 have demonstrated an average residence time of 6.9 and 10– 14 days for the longest fjord migration distance in Nordfjord29 and the Sognefjord,27 respectively. Thus, measurements indicate that exposure time of smolts to infectious lice larvae in the TLS is roughly 2–4 longer than shown by measurements by use of AT, which contributes to overestimates of modelled infection levels in all four models.

The EG already described the uncertainty for all modelled estimates of lice infestation (mortality) of migrating smolt as “first and foremost linked to when the smolts are in the fjords and thus for what period data are to be integrated. Despite this acknowledgement, the assumed timing and duration of smolt migration show a likely mismatch to actual smolt migration that most likely contributes to an overestimate of effects from lice on wild populations for both observational and modelled data in the current TLS.

- 3.2.4 | Reliance on observational data for calibration of models

To estimate the relationship between infestation pressure and likely infestation rate on migrating salmon smolts, the models calibrate simulated infestation pressure from either registered lice counts on trawled wild salmon smolts15,78 or from sentinel cages15–17 (Figure 2). The reliance on observational data is problematic, since observational data have several sources of unreliability, and which separately or collectively most likely contribute to overestimates of infection pressure.

Our findings are in line with the EvalComm, who concluded as follows; “(…) while the models themselves are state-of-the-art, model products are heavily reliant on calibration data, some of which demonstrate high levels of variability and uncertainty (e.g., sentinel cages). This reliance on calibration data potentially impairs the quality of model products.”79

3.3 | Reliability in analyses and interpretation of data

- 3.3.1 | Lice stage and reduction in lice load on fish post attachment must be considered when interpreting the impact of infection


The pathogenic impact of L. salmonis to its host greatly depends on the developmental stage of the parasite. The relatively small copepodid and chalimus parasitic stages elicit relatively minor stressand tissue responses in the host, and mortality is unusual.80–83 However, the pre-adult and adult parasitic stages are associated with increased plasma cortisol and glucose levels84 and more extensive tissue damage due to their larger sizes and increased feeding behaviour.85 Hence, prolonged infections with large numbers of mobile lice can lead to osmoregulatory failure and mortalities.81–83,86,87 Therefore, any estimation of the effects from

salmon lice must consider the natural reduction in lice numbers during development on the host.

The TLS estimates the likely mortality effects of lice burden from observational counts (trawling, fish traps/nets and sentinel cages) of all lice developmental stages which are evenly weighed when estimating these effects at individual and population levels.12,13 Furthermore, all attached lice are assumed to survive and contribute to the liceinduced mortality risk estimated by the IMR dispersal model,14 the virtual smolt model by SINTEF,17 and until 2021 also the NVI virtual smolt model.62 In contrast, the IMR and since 2022 NVI virtual post smolt models corrects for a 40% reduction in levels of newly attached lice when estimating the lice-induced mortality.15,31 Thus, five of seven end points in the assessment matrix estimate their lice-induced mortality from all lice stages without correcting for a natural reduction in lice load due to dislodging or mortality of the early developmental stages, and in the remaining two end points a 40% reduction is assumed (see also Figure 2).

The EGR13 suggests that even if several studies indicate the loss of up to 40% of lice during development and this would have consequences for mortality estimates, it is concluded in most cases this is a likely laboratory artefact caused by the frequent use of sedation and possible “tank-effects.” As a result, the EGR indicates that all lice stages should be included and equally weighed when interpreting effects from varying lice levels on wild salmon in observational data (and thus when estimating likely lice-induced mortality). However, of the studies that are referred to,66,81,88–90 anaesthetics were used in two when sampling fish,67,88 whereas the significant reduction from chalimus to pre-adult stages reported by Grimnes and Jakobsen81 is attributed to lice dislodging or natural mortality. The studies using sea trout89,90 are excluded here because of possible species-specific differences in lice susceptibility. In addition, a number of experimental studies that demonstrate a significant reduction in lice levels from early to later lice stages without the use of anaesthetics are not included in the EGR (Table 2). With the exception of Dalvin et al.,75 these studies report a significant reduction in lice levels on fish in the range of 35–84.6% (Table 2). Reported variation in reduction in lice level on host fish can likely partly be explained by differences in experimental conditions, and also by the duration of the study and which lice stages are included. The magnitude of the loss of lice depends on the range of developmental stages observed in a study. An unweighted averaged reduction of 57.5% is estimated from studies spanning the attached copepodid to preadult stages,44,80,91,92 whereas an average reduction of 80.2% is obtained from studies spanning copepodid to adult stages.82,93 The higher reduction of lice numbers when including adult stages indicates that large losses also occur during the mobile stages.94 It should be noted that the studies referred to in Table 2 are laboratory experiments which are often performed within a stable and optimal temperature and salinity range for salmon lice as well as slow water exchange. Reductions in salmon lice load under natural conditions may be higher than reported (Table 2) because of suboptimal and highly variable conditions of temperature, salinity, water currents and host behaviour.

- 3.3.2 | Mortality of individual salmon smolt is overestimated by TLS


The estimated salmon lice load which is likely to cause mortality for an individual smolt, the threshold for lice-induced mortality, is a key parameter used by the TLS to estimate likely population effects, and which feeds into and potentially affect all end points in the assessment matrix (Figure 2). The basis for the proposed mortality thresholds practiced in the TLS is outlined in Table 3 (references taken from Reference 18). A subgroup of the EG reviewed additional studies and concluded there was no basis for changing the previously proposed thresholds13 (Table 3).

When the threshold values and the lice mortality index were originally proposed, the method was deemed relevant for estimating effects on wild salmonids <150 g in general, thus migrating Atlantic salmon smolt, first-time migrant sea trout and Arctic charr.19,20 These

reports pointed out that the consequences of a given level of lice infestation (lice g 1 fish body mass) may vary among the salmonid species and by fish size. However, the Ministry of Trade, Industry and Fisheries11 defines that the main focus of the TLS “shall be wild salmon,” and that at present we only have sufficient knowledge to assess salmon lice-induced mortality on “seaward-migrating post-smolt salmon.”12,13 Hence, it is problematic that the threshold values intended for all three salmonid species are used for assessment of effects on migrating Atlantic salmon smolt in the current TLS. Only some of the studies that form the basis for the currently practiced thresholds are relevant for evaluating mortality limits for small salmon/seaward migrating smolt of Atlantic salmon which are normally of average weight of 15–25 g (Table 4).

The majority of the studies referred to by Taranger et al.19 are irrelevant for evaluating effects of lice on migrating smolts of Atlantic salmon (Table 3) because the fish under study are substantially larger than and/or in a different developmental stage than migrating smolt (15–25 g) and/or of a different species than Atlantic salmon. For instance, three studies are performed on salmon between 250 and 650 g,95–97 one on sexually mature Arctic charr weighing 700 g,98 and three are performed with sea trout.90,99,100

In tank trials, effects from lice could for instance be underestimated since effects of sublethal levels of lice on smolts predator avoidance and feed intake cannot be measured, as goes for long term effects from sublethal levels of lice, since the performed tank trials are of limited duration. Further, cultured and “well-fed” smolt is likely more robust, and could expose a lower stress response (be more tolerant) to lice as compared to wild migrating smolt. On the other hand, as outlined by Wagner et al.87 some typical laboratory artefacts could contribute to overestimates of effects from lice in tank trials. For instance, since the infection of experimental animals in laboratory studies is performed with a high dose of infectious lice larvae and as a “single pulse” host response could differ compared to natural circumstances where lice infections most likely are more successive. Also, single-pulse and reduced waterflow practiced in laboratory to optimize infection levels can result in an unnaturally high share of lice attached to the gill filaments, likely to give an increased stress response compared with less vascularized and less physiologically active tissues (e.g., skin and fins), which are typical sites of natural infection. Also, increased stress levels due to tank environment and frequent sampling could increase susceptibility and/or response to lice infection. In addition, all referred experimental studies are performed within optimal salinity and temperature ranges for salmon lice, whereas this is likely often not the case in the natural environment and during smolt migration. As has been previously outlined, the ability of lice to stay attached and develop/survive (and thus their likely negative effect) on the host is reduced at suboptimal salinity and/or temperatures.34,67,77

In summary, based on a review of the referred studies and survey observations relevant for Atlantic salmon smolt (Table 3), not one of the studies support an absolute (100%) mortality for individual smolt with a relative infection of ≥0.3 lice g 1(including all lice stages), which is currently practiced in the TLS (Table 5). Further, the indications in all studies are that the proposed expected mortality of 20 and 50% for individual smolt with a relative infection of 0.1–0.2 and 0.2–0.3 lice g 1, respectively, are also most likely overestimates, also when a likely share of indirect mortality due to sublethal effects is considered (Table 5). It is likely the experimental study by Grimnes and Jakobsen,81 indicating a lower/minimum and average mortality threshold at a relative intensity of 0.55–1.16 and 1.14–1.74 mobile lice g 1, respectively, in experiments with adequate replication and statistically robust sample sizes give a more correct estimate of

mortality thresholds as compared to estimates currently practiced in the TLS. This is further supported by a recent study performed with wild origin Atlantic salmon smolt with experimental infections ranging from 0 to 2.4 lice g 1 (all stages), and the use of PIT tagging enabling linking specific infection intensities with mortality risk.83 Based on counts at chalimus II stage, 85% of smolt with less than 0.3 lice g 1 survived. Further, estimated mortality risk was 20 and 50% for smolt with a relative infection intensity of 0.27 and 0.43 lice g 1, respectively. While mortality risk was >99% when infection was above 1.6 chalimus II lice g 1, compared to above 0.38 lice g 1 based on average number of lice over the entire experimental trial (thus including preadult and adult stages).

Given the broad variation in tolerance for lice load suggested from the limited available data and reliance on data from laboratory experiments, more studies should be conducted preferably including field studies to improve the empirical basis for mortality thresholds.

Our findings are in line with the findings of the Eliasen et al.79 who concluded: “The EvalComm is of the view that a solid empirical basis for the thresholds has not been provided to date and that such is required to underwrite key assessments arising from the TLS (…) The TLS is very sensitive to assumptions made in this part of the process, and yet empirical data appear to be largely unavailable. This represents a significant weakness in the TLS and its assessment end points.”

“The absence of any documented reassessment of the validity of the mortality thresholds has the potential to undermine confidence in the operation of the system as a whole.”79

Overall, in spite of a recent meta-analysis of existing mortality data103 and data on mortality thresholds from wild fish83 uncertainties remain.

3.3.5 | Significance of correcting for a likely 50% post attachment reduction in lice infection level on host

As detailed in Section 3.3.1, in most assessment end points all lice are evenly weighted and included in interpretation of lice load when estimating likely effects on smolt health or survival, despite the loss of a high proportion of lice during development from the copepodid to the pre-adult stages (Table 2). Here, we use the same trawl data described in Section 3.3.4 to compare the inclusion of all lice (TLS) with a lice load resulting from a 50% reduction which occurs during development (Table 2) on the outcome of the risk assessment. Since all lice stages are specified in the trawl data, we corrected for a 50% reduction of copepodids and chalimus I in the data set. Using these adjusted lice loads, fish were sorted into infection groups and the resulting proportions entered into the lice index scheme using mortality thresholds as practiced by TLS and by VRL (Table 7).

Correction for the expected reduction in lice load results in a decrease in the estimated mortality risk from 18.9% (TLS thresholds and including all lice, Table 6) to 6.2% (Table 7). Similarly, correction for the expected reduction in lice load results in a decrease from 7.8% (VRL thresholds and including all lice, Table 6) to 2.2%. Thus, independent of chosen mortality thresholds, solely correcting for a 50% reduction in load of the earliest parasitic lice stages would result in “low” (green) as opposed to “moderate” (yellow) impacts, as previously assessed using TLS thresholds.110 Using both correction for a 50% lice reduction and VRL thresholds, the estimated impact is further minimized. It should be noted that analysis of retrieved trawl data108 revealed that registered lice on trawled smolt consisted of 97.5% of the earliest parasitic stages (89% copepodid and 8.5% chalimus I), and hence including all lice would result in a substantial overestimate of likely mortality effects. At present, failure to consider the reduction in lice numbers during development on the host in the interpretation of effects from lice load combined with the use of erroneous thresholds (Sections 3.3.2 and 3.3.4) most likely contributes to significant overestimates in the majority of the assessment end points in the assessment matrix—and thus the outcome of the risk assessment for each PA (see also Figure 2).

- 3.4 | Inconsistency in associations between lice levels in fish farms and wild fish


Estimates of lice larvae production from fish farms in the current TLS are based on an assumption of a linear relationship between the prevalence of adult female lice on farmed salmon and the production of infectious lice larvae.18 Similarly, the association between the prevalence on farmed salmon and the lice levels on nearby wild salmon are assumed to be significantly associated, and thus assuming prevalence of female lice in farms can predict lice levels on nearby wild populations.14–17 Several studies have suggested that these relationships are inconsistent and that variables other than lice levels in fish farms can help explain lice levels on wild fish in surrounding waters.44,111–113

The correlation between spatiotemporal variation in salmon lice levels on 4890 wild sea trout sampled from 41 sites in 15 fjords along the entire Norwegian coast during the period 2004–2010 and the explanatory variables temperature, salinity, fish size, and infestation pressure from fish farms (mean abundance of mature female lice number of farmed fish) was investigated.45,111 While all explanatory variables were shown to influence the probability of lice on wild sea trout, the magnitudes of increasing temperature (20–85%), increasing length of wild fish (50–90%), and decreasing salinity (80– 20%) were greater than that of infestation pressure from farms (70– 95%).45,111 The interaction between farm infestation pressure and temperature was statistically significant.111 Thus, this study showed that under ambient conditions environmental factors and fish length are better predictors for infestation levels on wild sea trout than salmon farm infestation pressure.

Correlations between farm site lice-levels and infestations levels of surrounding wild salmonid populations were performed based on data covering all salmon-producing sites in Norway from 2012 to 2020.112 The study includes nationwide data and focuses specifically on PAs 3, 4, and 5 (which have received red-light status) and PA

- 7, and in weeks 16–21, which are most relevant for smolt migration. First, similar to what we have presented in our review, Jensen et al.118 also found limited causal relation between salmon lice at the farm site level and the infection of wild salmon stocks in a PA. Second, Jensen et al.118 argued that the TLS is enforced by using a highly uncertain measure of the aggregated salmon lice-induced mortality on wild stocks in a PA. As highlighted in our review the measurement methods are based on observable data and/or biological models, but as indicated by Nilsen et al.12 Vollset et al.,13 the estimated mortality in each PA depends on the method which is used. Furthermore, for several PAs, Nilsen et al.12 and Vollset et al.13 indicated that the salmon liceinduced mortality is highly uncertain.

In summary, several reviewed studies and extensive data sets demonstrate inconsistencies in the assumed significant association between lice levels in fish farms and lice levels and wild fish. These findings indicate that levels of lice on wild salmonids cannot be explained solely by infestation pressure from farm-origin lice and that other explanatory factors play an important role (Figure 7). The reviewed data also indicate that temperature and salinity likely are significant contributors to observed variations in infestation levels among wild salmonids along the Norwegian coast, emphasizing the importance of including these parameters in salmon lice modelling for management purposes to a larger extent. The reviewed literature also indicates that regionally, there is poor agreement between predictions on population level effects from aquaculture in the TLS and observed wild Atlantic salmon dynamics. This further supports insecurities in the current TLS and the need for improvements considering the major implications the current TLS regulations has for the salmon farming industry, and that other factors than aquaculture alone are important variables to include in salmon farming management.

### 4 | MAIN FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

This section summarizes and discusses our main findings along with uncertainties that are potentially critical to the outcome of the TLS and makes recommendations to improve its accuracy and reliability.

- 4.1 | The assumed duration and timing for smolt migration contribute to unreliable observational and modelled data outputs


Salmon lice abundance and lice infection pressure increases with temperature from early spring. Hence, the dates that observational data

(Figure 3) are retrieved (Section 3.1.1) and that modelled data are integrated (Section 3.2.4) largely influence the outcome of the impact assessment. The first smolt that migrate with low infestation pressure and likely correspondingly low infection levels are excluded from estimates of lice-induced mortality on population level. Also, the TLS assumes a timing for smolt migration that extends too late in the season, thereby including temperature-associated increases in lice levels which occur after the migration. In addition, the TLS assumes an overly prolonged duration of migration, increasing the exposure to farm derived lice in sentinel cages (Section 3.1.1), and prolonging the period for which modelled data are integrated (Section 3.2.4). Timing, and duration for retrieving observational data and for integration of modelled data contribute to unreliability and most likely overestimates of effects from salmon lice in all seven assessment end points (Figure 2).

- 4.1.1 | Recommendations

The period chosen for integration of modelled data and retrieving observational data should reflect the timing and duration of Atlantic salmon smolt migration based on the most recently available data including acoustic tracking measurements in fjord systems. Reliability of the estimated timing and duration data should be defined, and the sensitivity of modelled outputs should be demonstrated. In future, data on smolt residence time in outer fjord area, migration speed and swimming depths should be retrieved, preferably for more than one fjord systems per PA.

4.2 | Methods for retrieving observational data contribute to overestimates of infection pressure

- 4.2.1 | Limited spatial resolution of data and exclusion of fish size as a variable reduces reliability of data


The areas smolts are trawled from and the stations used for sentinel cages occur towards the outer end of the fjord where conditions for salmon lice recruitment and infectivity are optimal (Section 3.1.2, Figures 4–6). Also, limited spatial resolution and near shore stations for sentinel cages and sea trout traps contribute to TLS unreliability (Sections 3.1.2 and 3.1.4). Thus, observational data are not representative for the PA as a whole with a likely bias towards areas with physical conditions most optimal for salmon lice. Also, since cultured salmon smolt used in sentinel cages and sampled sea trout are substantially larger than migrating wild smolt failure to correct lice burden data by fish size will result in a tendency of the TLS to overestimate exposure risk since the likelihood of sea lice infestation is positively correlated to fish size. Assessments based on these data will tend to overestimate risk of exposure to salmon lice. In addition, since all models are calibrated against observational data, these unknown factors and overestimates in observational data will influence the reliability of all model outcomes.

Recommendations. Observational data from more representative areas within the PAs, reflecting the heterogeneity within and between fjords, should be used to a greater extent. Environmental variables should be included in interpretation of lice load when comparing different zones within a fjord system with varying physical conditions in the upper water layers. Further, lice load data from sentinel cages should be corrected for fish size to avoid overestimates of infection levels. Representative temperature, salinity and water current data should be collected during trawling and from sensors mounted in- and outside of sentinel cages. As temperature and salinity can vary from year to year, it may also be worthwhile to consider the use of rolling average of temperatures and salinities.

- 4.2.2 | Poor reliability of using sea trout as proxy for salmon


Sea trout generally have higher lice levels as compared to salmon likely due to different life-history strategy and to their larger size. Recent evidence of correlations between lice levels on sea trout and nearby farmed salmon indicate a high dependency on temperature and farm production cycle.51 Furthermore, the negative correlations between lice levels on sea trout collected from one location in the outer Osterfjord system and returning wild salmon8 require confirmation in other fjord systems, to confirm the predictive ability of lice infections on sea trout as a proxy for salmon.

Recommendation. Sea trout should not be used a separate indicator in the assessment of effects from salmon lice on wild Atlantic salmon smolt.

4.3 | Production of lice larvae from fish farms is overestimated

Estimated numbers of lice larvae from fish farms are input data to all models (Figure 2), and erroneous assumptions regarding larval recruitment and mortality may result in uncertainties (Section 3.2.1). Larval production from farms is likely overestimated in the TLS by failing to consider mortality factors such as predation, temperature, and salinity (Table 1) during the recruitment and development of lice. This implies that both input data (e.g., production of lice larvae from farms) and data used for calibration (Sections 3.1 and 3.2.4) have sources of uncertainty and are likely overestimates. These overestimates combined will influence the outcomes of sea lice dispersion models and infestation risk of migrating salmon (Figure 2), with the potential to influence four assessment end points in the assessment of mortality risk due to lice of farm origin in a PA.

Recommendation: In predictions of salmon lice larvae production from fish farms, it is recommended that values for the reproductive rate of adult female lice and for egg viability be adapted from the reviewed literature (Table 1). These predictions should incorporate salinity, temperature, and predation to provide more realistic estimates of lice larvae production from fish farms. On longer term,

acquiring more experimental data on these variables will allow a more accurate and reliable parameterization of salmon lice dynamics relevant for management purposes. Measurements of temperature and salinities at 1, 3, and 5 m depth in and outside sea cages in farms will aid improved estimates of production of eggs (5 m) and resulting lice larvae (1–3 m). More data on wild fish as sources of infestation pressure (e.g., returning wild salmon, local sea trout) would be beneficial for a further understanding of salmon lice dynamics in fjords.

- 4.4 | Reliance on input and calibration data with several sources of uncertainty potentially impairs model outcomes

Most assessment end points are estimates resulting from various model systems (Sections 3.2, 3.2.3, and 3.2.4), and which thus highly affect the final impact assessment for each PA (Figure 2). Although methodologies differ, all model systems rely on the same input data from farms (Figure 2) and make the same assumptions for estimates of lice larvae production (Sections 3.2.1 and 4.3). Furthermore, as has been detailed in Section 3.1, observational data as they are currently retrieved are not a reliable source for calibration or validation purposes. In summary, all model systems rely on input- and calibration data which have several sources of insecurity, and in accordance with Eliasen et al.,79 we conclude that reliance on data which demonstrate high levels of variability and uncertainty potentially impairs the quality of all model products.

Recommendation: It is critical to demonstrate and communicate the sensitivity of the modelled outputs and assumptions made about the model parameters. In agreement with Eliasen et al.,79 we recommend model results should be presented as much as possible with confidence intervals through formal uncertainty quantification. The product of these models may appear highly precise and quantitative, but their output has to be used with full cognizance of their limitations, and care must be taken when explaining the model outputs to non-expert audiences.

- 4.5 | Estimates of lice-associated mortality in wild salmon smolts are overestimated


4.5.1 | Failure to consider lice stage and post attachment reductions in lice loads

All infesting lice are assumed by the TLS to develop into pathogenic pre-adult and adult stage (Section 3.3.1) in the interpretation of observational data and from two of four modelled data. In reality, an average of 57.5% reduction in lice numbers occurs between newly attached copepodid and pre-adult stage (Table 2). The inclusion of all lice and the equal weighting of all stages in the TLS is unreliable and contributes to overestimates of mortality in five out of seven assessment end points (Figure 2; Section 3.3.5). In the remaining two outputs, a 40% reduction is adapted, which according to reviewed literature could be too low (Table 2).

Recommendation: Registered (observational data) and simulated (modelled data) lice numbers should be adjusted to reflect expected reductions in lice load post attachment before salmon lice-induced mortality is calculated in all assessments. In addition, confidence intervals should be defined to reflect the insecurity in the final estimate. On longer term, more controlled experimental data are required to understand the effects of current flow, salinity, and temperature on loss of lice during infestations.

- 4.5.2 | Mortality thresholds are unreliable and likely overestimate effects from lice


Based solely on studies that are relevant to migrating Atlantic salmon smolt (Tables 3, 4), a higher threshold level for lice-induced mortality is indicated than is currently practiced in the TLS. Specifically, not one of the referred studies support an absolute (100%) mortality for individual smolt with a relative infection of >0.3 mobile lice g 1 (Tables 3 and 4). Mortality thresholds (Sections 3.3.2 and

- 3.3.3) and mortality scheme (Table 5) inform the conclusions for all assessment endpoints (Figure 2) and therefore will overestimate the lethal effects of infestation. The significance of different mortality thresholds on the outcome of the risk assessment is demonstrated in Section 3.3.4 (Table 6). By using thresholds which are developed by VRL solely for assessment of migrating salmon smolt, the estimated mortality risk is reduced by more than 50% resulting in the estimated population-level effects being reduced from moderate to low. Failure to consider reduced lice load (Section 4.5.1) and use of erroneous mortality thresholds separately, and even more so combined, have major implications for the current assessment outcome in the TLS and contribute to overestimates of effects from lice on wild salmon populations.

Recommendation: Post-attachment reduction in lice numbers should be seen in context with mortality thresholds, and corrections for the earliest parasitic lice stages should be implemented in all assessments of lice-associated mortality in the TLS. The most likely tolerance and mortality thresholds should be re-evaluated based on available literature relevant for Atlantic salmon smolt (Table 4, and cited literature Section 3.3.2). Measures of uncertainty in the estimate should be included. A 100% mortality limit is not supported by any relevant literature and should therefore not be practiced. Additional experimental studies are required to assess the effects of different lice loads under conditions of variable flow, salinity, and temperature more reflective of natural conditions in upper water layers during smolt migration, including sublethal and long-term effects from lice load.

- 4.6 | Lack of association between farm infection levels and measurable effects on wild Atlantic salmon


The TLS was designed with the purpose of assessing impacts to wild Atlantic salmon from salmon lice originating from salmonid fish farms. Available literature indicates (i) inconsistencies between high numbers

of lice at farm sites and the number of severely infested wild salmon in the same area, (ii) that lice levels on wild salmonids were predicted better by environmental factors and fish size than by lice levels on farmed salmon in the same area, and (iii) lack of association between EG sea lice assessments of PAs and return rates of Atlantic salmon in the same area (Section 3.4). These observations emphasize the difficulty in modelling complex ecological systems as well as the importance of improving accuracy and the reliability of the TLS.

Recommendation: A more extensive analyses of the assumed/ predicted cause-and-effect relationship between lice levels on migrating smolts and wild salmon population dynamics is recommended. Also, it is advised that factors affecting the survival of Atlantic salmon in freshwater, fjord and the open ocean are considered in a holistic approach for salmon conservation.

### 5 | CONCLUSIONS

A survey of available scientific and grey literature and related datasets indicates that the efficiency of the TLS and its value to salmon conservation and aquaculture management should be improved. The evidence shows that salmon smolt behaviour, water currents, temperature and salinity, and most likely predation, are important drivers of variations in salmon lice infestation levels among wild salmonids along the Norwegian coast, emphasizing the importance of a greater inclusion of these variables in salmon lice modelling for managements purposes than is currently performed. Thus, the evidence indicates that factors other than lice levels in fish farms drive lice dynamics and that levels of lice on wild salmonids cannot be explained solely by infestation pressure from farm-origin lice. Looking at the greater picture, the reviewed literature also indicates a lack of correlation between the TLS predictions on population level effects from aquaculture and observations of wild Atlantic salmon dynamics, further supporting insecurities in the current TLS. The central role of the TLS in driving policy decisions concerning the salmon farming industry in Norway requires a greater effort is made to ensure that data and assumptions used by the TLS best reflect the current state of knowledge.
