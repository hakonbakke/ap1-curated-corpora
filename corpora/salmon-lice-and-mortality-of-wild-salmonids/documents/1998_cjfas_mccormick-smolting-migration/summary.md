## What this paper does

McCormick et al. (1998) provide a comprehensive synthesis of the movements and migrations of juvenile Atlantic salmon (*Salmo salar*) from fry emergence through to smolt migration. The authors emphasize the interplay between physiological, morphological, and behavioral adaptations that facilitate this critical life stage. The review introduces the concept of a **physiological smolt window** and an **ecological smolt window**, which are crucial for understanding the timing and success of smolt migration. This work is particularly relevant in the context of increasing anthropogenic pressures on salmon habitats, as it highlights the importance of synchronized physiological readiness and favorable ecological conditions for successful marine survival.

The paper is set against a backdrop of significant ecological and environmental changes affecting salmon populations, particularly in North America and Europe. By detailing the physiological changes that occur during smolting, such as increased salinity tolerance and altered behavior, the authors underscore the vulnerability of smolts to various stressors, including pollution and habitat alteration. This review predates the extensive literature on salmon lice and aquaculture but lays the groundwork for understanding how smolt migration timing and routes can influence exposure to coastal parasites, making it highly relevant to current discussions in the field.

## Methods and data

The authors conducted a literature review, synthesizing findings from various studies on the movements of juvenile Atlantic salmon. They categorized the movements into distinct phases, including fry emergence, territorial establishment, and downstream migration. The review draws on empirical data from multiple geographic locations, particularly focusing on studies conducted in North America and Europe. Key physiological metrics, such as gill Na⁺,K⁺-ATPase activity and salinity tolerance, were highlighted as critical indicators of smolt readiness. The authors also discussed the influence of environmental factors, such as temperature and photoperiod, on the timing of smolt migration.

## Key findings

- **Physiological window:** The physiological readiness of smolts is influenced by a neuroendocrine cascade driven by photoperiod and temperature. Gill Na⁺,K⁺-ATPase activity peaks and subsequently declines by approximately **80%** within **~400 degree-days** after peak, indicating a narrowing window for migration readiness.
  
- **Ecological window:** A simulation presented in the paper suggests that aligned migration with favorable environmental conditions can lead to higher adult returns, with returns estimated at **~166** adults when migration timing is optimal compared to **~94** when shifted by two weeks.

- **Migration cues:** In the River Imsa, **91–95%** of smolt migration timing was explained by April–May temperatures, with migration beginning at temperatures between **5.8–11.2°C**.

- **Stressors:** Exposure to acidic conditions (pH 5.2) for **12 hours** can eliminate salinity tolerance in smolts. Rivers with acid conditions yield returns that are approximately **10 times** lower than limed sites. Hatchery-reared smolts exhibit survival rates that are about **half** that of wild smolts in the Imsa River.

- **Migration corridors:** The mainstems and estuaries are heavily impacted by anthropogenic activities such as pollution and damming, which can elevate mortality rates for migrating smolts.

## Limitations and caveats

The review is based on literature available prior to 1998, which may not encompass the latest findings in salmon lice and aquaculture. The authors relied heavily on laboratory studies to discuss physiological changes, which may not fully capture the complexities of wild populations. Additionally, the review does not provide new empirical data but synthesizes existing knowledge, which may limit the applicability of findings to specific populations or environments.

## What this paper does not claim

The authors do not claim to provide a comprehensive analysis of all factors affecting smolt survival or the impacts of salmon lice and aquaculture practices, as these topics were not extensively covered in the literature at the time. They also do not assert that all salmon populations exhibit the same migratory behaviors or physiological responses, acknowledging the variability among different populations.

## Relevance to this corpus

This paper contributes to question **Q4**, explaining how the timing, duration, and route of smolt migration influence exposure to lice in fjord systems. It highlights the importance of physiological readiness coinciding with favorable ecological conditions during coastal transit, which is critical for understanding post-smolt survival in the context of aquaculture and environmental stressors.

## Related evidence in corpus

While the paper does not directly reference specific studies on salmon lice, it provides foundational knowledge that informs current research on the interactions between salmon migration, environmental stressors, and aquaculture practices. The insights on physiological readiness and ecological timing are particularly relevant for ongoing discussions regarding the impacts of aquaculture on wild salmon populations.
