# Summary: McCormick et al. (1998)

**Full title:** Movement, migration, and smolting of Atlantic salmon (*Salmo salar*)
**Journal:** Canadian Journal of Fisheries and Aquatic Sciences, 55(Suppl. 1): 77-92
**DOI:** 10.1139/f98-011
**Document type:** Review article (foundational)
**Priority questions:** Q4

---

## What this paper does

McCormick et al. (1998) provide a comprehensive synthesis of Atlantic salmon biology from emergence as fry through the freshwater-to-seawater transition as smolts. The review covers five main areas: (1) movements of fry from redds; (2) parr establishment and territory defense in streams; (3) spawning movements of precocious male parr; (4) autumn movements to winter habitat; and (5) the smolt migration itself — the highly directed, temporally synchronous downstream movement that defines the transition from juvenile freshwater fish to marine migrant.

The central contribution is a rigorous treatment of **smoltification** — the suite of physiological, morphological, and behavioral changes that prepare Atlantic salmon for seawater entry. The authors describe how smolts become silvery (increased guanine deposition), streamlined, and school-forming; how they lose positive rheotaxis and territorial behavior; and how they undergo fundamental changes in osmoregulation (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase activation in the gill), hemoglobin oxygen affinity, visual pigments, and olfactory sensitivity. These changes convert a territorial, freshwater-adapted fish into a streamlined, ocean-ready migratory machine.

The review introduces and defines the **dual smolt window** concept: (i) a *physiological smolt window* — a limited period during which the fish is physiologically capable of surviving seawater entry, regulated primarily by photoperiod (long-day exposure triggers the endocrine cascade) and temperature (modulates pace); and (ii) an *ecological smolt window* — the period when environmental conditions (temperature, food availability, predator load) favor survival after seawater entry. Survival is maximized when seawater entry falls within both windows.

---

## Main findings

- **Smoltification is an adaptive specialization** coordinated by the neuroendocrine system. Photoperiod is the primary timing cue; temperature gates the pace. Increasing day length from late winter triggers the hormonal cascade (GH/IGF-1, thyroid hormones, cortisol) that drives physiological readiness.

- **The physiological smolt window is narrow.** Fish that miss this window — by entering seawater too early (before physiological readiness) or too late (after the window closes, when osmoregulatory capacity declines) — have reduced survival. The window duration varies by population and year.

- **Temperature and water flow trigger downstream migration** once fish are physiologically ready. Rising spring temperatures and freshets are key environmental cues. Warming events and altered hydrology (dams, flow regulation) can advance or delay migration relative to the physiological window.

- **Ecological smolt window constraints:** Even physiologically ready fish may face poor survival if seawater entry coincides with high predator abundance, low food availability, or stressful temperatures. The timing of seawater entry relative to these ecological factors has a major effect on first-marine-year survival.

- **Behavioral changes during smolting include:** loss of positive rheotaxis (fish no longer actively hold position against current), loss of territorial behavior, initiation of schooling, and sustained directional downstream movement. These changes facilitate collective migration and reduce metabolic cost.

- **Anthropogenic impacts on the smolt migration corridor** (river mainstems, estuaries) are disproportionately severe. Dams delay or block migration, increasing the proportion of fish that miss the physiological window or face elevated predation risk from prolonged estuarine residence. Acidification and pollutants impair gill function and osmoregulatory capacity.

- **Behavioral plasticity of Atlantic salmon** is extensive: precocious male maturation as parr, facultative residency in landlocked populations, and variable smolt age (1–8 years depending on latitude and growth conditions) all demonstrate that anadromy is not fixed.

Smolts entering coastal waters during peak lice density periods face higher exposure; the physiological and ecological smolt windows define when fish enter and how long they remain in the lice-exposure zone. Migration timing, duration, and route — all described in this review — directly determine the lice dose received by individual smolts. |

---

## Limitations

- Published in 1998; does not address salmon lice, aquaculture impacts, or the Norwegian Traffic Light System.
- Does not cover subsequent developments in salmon smolt biology, genomics, or ecological changes due to climate warming.
- Physiological mechanisms are based largely on controlled laboratory experiments; field validation of critical thresholds (e.g., exact osmotic challenge tolerance over time) is limited.
- Some North American-specific details may not fully apply to Norwegian populations, though the core physiology is conserved.

---

## Place in the literature

This paper is a citation classic in Atlantic salmon biology. It establishes the mechanistic framework within which all subsequent work on smolt migration timing, seawater entry, and early marine survival should be interpreted — including the growing literature on salmon lice exposure. The dual smolt window concept is directly relevant to understanding why even small shifts in the timing of seawater entry (e.g., due to climate warming advancing spring temperatures) can dramatically alter the overlap between the physiological readiness of smolts and the density peak of salmon lice larvae in coastal waters.

For the lice-mortality corpus, this paper provides the biological grounding for why timing matters in lice exposure models (Q4). Papers like Bøhn et al. (2020) — which show that timing of smolt migration relative to lice peaks is a key determinant of survival — rest on the physiological and behavioral foundations laid out by McCormick et al. (1998). It is also relevant background for understanding why Norwegian fjords present structured windows of vulnerability: smolts pass through the lice-exposure zone during a brief, physiologically constrained period, making the synchrony between smolt migration and lice density a critical determinant of population-level mortality.
