## What this paper does

Jansen and Gjerde (2021) critically evaluate the IMR Virtual Post-Smolt (VPS) model developed by Johnsen et al. (2021), which predicts salmon lice-induced mortality in *Salmo salar* post-smolts during their migration from 401 Norwegian rivers. The authors compare the VPS model's mortality predictions against observed mortality rates derived from post-smolts caught by trawling in outer fjord areas from 2017 to 2020. This validation is crucial as the VPS model's outputs have significant implications for aquaculture management in Norway, particularly under the Traffic Light System (TLS) that regulates salmon farming based on predicted lice-induced mortality.

The study's findings are particularly relevant given the ongoing concerns regarding the impact of salmon lice, *Lepeophtheirus salmonis*, on wild salmon populations. The authors argue that the VPS model systematically overestimates mortality compared to the observed data, which may lead to overly conservative management decisions affecting aquaculture production. This comment contributes to the broader discourse on the accuracy of predictive models in fisheries management and their implications for environmental sustainability.

## Methods and data

The authors utilized a dataset comprising 7,929 wild Atlantic salmon post-smolts (weighing between 5 and 50 g) collected through the Norwegian surveillance program for salmon lice (NALO) between 2017 and 2020. They excluded 339 tagged fish and 326 fish with scale-loss injuries, which were associated with lower lice counts. The mortality estimation followed the same methodology as Johnsen et al. (2021), where the mean number of lice per fish was multiplied by 0.6 to estimate harmful lice counts. The authors employed a negative binomial distribution with a dispersion parameter of 0.3695 to model the lice counts, assuming a standard weight of 20 g for the fish.

Mortality thresholds were defined as follows: fish with more than 6 lice experienced 100% mortality, those with 4 to 6 lice had 50% mortality, 2 to 3 lice resulted in 20% mortality, and 0 to 1 lice had 0% mortality. The authors then compared the estimated mortality rates from the trawled post-smolts to the predicted VPS mortality for rivers draining into the corresponding fjords.

## Key findings

### Headline comparison
- VPS mortality predictions were higher than observed trawl mortality in 26 out of 27 area-year pairs.
- The discrepancy was most pronounced when trawl mortality was low, indicating that the VPS model may overestimate mortality under certain conditions.

### Supplementary analysis
The authors also addressed the potential for additional lice accumulation after trawling by regressing lice counts against the relative distance (RD) toward the open ocean. They found a significant positive relationship in 11 out of 27 area-years. However, even after adjusting for this effect, VPS mortality estimates remained higher in 22 out of 27 comparisons (χ² p = 0.023).

## Limitations and caveats

The authors acknowledge several limitations in their analysis:
- The mean VPS mortality across rivers may not accurately reflect the trawl catch mix, potentially leading to biases in the comparison.
- The study cannot definitively attribute the observed overestimation of mortality to specific sub-models within the VPS framework.
- The assumption that trawled fish originate from the corresponding rivers is debated, although supported by genetic evidence and migration patterns.

## What this paper does not claim

Jansen and Gjerde do not claim to provide a comprehensive critique of all aspects of the VPS model or its underlying assumptions. They focus specifically on the discrepancies between predicted and observed mortality rates, without asserting that the VPS model is entirely invalid. Additionally, they do not claim to identify the precise causes of the systematic overestimation of mortality.
