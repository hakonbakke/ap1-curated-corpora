Johnsen et al. () present a model that predicts lice-induced mortality of virtual post-smolt (VPS) Atlantic salmon migrating from  Norwegian salmon rivers. An important feature of this model is that the source of lice is farmed salmon. The model predicts lice-induced mortalities exceeding % for VPS from  rivers, –% for  rivers, and less than % for  rivers. These are high numbers and have contributed to mandatory reductions in the production of farmed salmon in two of the  management areas in Norway. In this comment, the model used by Johnsen et al. () is evaluated. Mortality estimates from observed lice numbers on post-smolts caught by trawling in outer fjord areas are compared with the model predicted mortalities for VPS migrating from rivers draining into the corresponding fjords. It is found that Johnsen et al. () systematically overestimates lice-induced mortality for VPS compared to estimates from trawled smolt from corresponding fjords. The present analysis thus contradicts the concluding remark in Johnsen et al. (), that: “The estimated lice level on modelled post-smolts is shown to coincide with the level observed on captured wild post-smolt”.

Keywords: aquaculture, Lepeophtheirus salmonis, management, modelling, Salmo salar.

### Introduction

Johnsen et al. (2021) present a model that predicts salmon liceinduced mortality of virtual post-smolt (VPS) Atlantic salmon migrating from 401 Norwegian rivers. An important feature of this model is that the source of the lice is farmed salmon and that mortality of post-smolts is a function of the lice numbers infesting them and their size. Accordingly, estimates from this model are currently used as a major part of the Norwegian government’s evaluation of the environmental impact of aquaculture (Johnsen et al., 2021). These evaluations determine future production volumes in salmon farmingin13management areas(MAs)along theNorwegian coast, in the new management system called the “Traffic light system” (St. Meld. 16, 2014–2015). This management system implies a 6% reduction in production volumes for farms in MAs where mortalities of wild salmon probably exceed 30% (red light) due to salmon lice

from farms, no growth in production volumes when lice-induced mortality probably is within 10–30% (yellow light), and a permission to increase farm production volumes by 6% when lice-induced mortalityprobablyislessthan10%(greenlight).Inaccordancewith these regulations, Johnsen et al. (2021) reported predictions of liceinduced mortality for VPS exceeding 30% for 82 rivers, 10–30% for 140 rivers, and less than 10% for 179 rivers in 2019. These predictions have contributed to mandatory reductions in the production of farmed salmon in MAs 4 and 5.

Johnsen et al. (2021) developed their model by first deriving a sub-modelforpredictingthenumberofliceinfestingVPSasafunction of exposure to farm origin lice infestation pressure (IP) along their migration pathways. In this sub-model, IP was defined as the migration time multiplied with the mean concentration of infective salmon lice copepodids along the migration route. Only post-smolts that were geneticallyassignedtotheirnativerivers(N=1679)wereusedforparameter estimation. For these fish, migration pathways were simulated with VPS in a migration model. VPS simulations matching the timing of the catch and a migration route starting from the native river outlet, was used to estimate the IP experienced by the trawled postsmolts. The endpoint of migration for post-smolts used to parameterise the infestation sub-model, however, is not clearly defined in Johnsen et al. (2021). Nevertheless, the relationship between IP exposure and observed lice numbers on post-smolts was analysed in a regression model expressing the expected number of lice infesting migrating post-smolts as a function of IP and a specified negative binomial distribution of the number of lice per fish. This infestation sub-model was then used to predict lice numbers infesting VPS from 401 salmon rivers along the Norwegian coast, given the IP exposure and timing of simulated migration pathways from river outlets to the open ocean. Finally, mortality estimates for VPS from the various rivers were calculated by assuming a deterministic lice tolerance depending on the number of lice per fish and that migrating post-smolts weigh 20 g. According to Johnsen et al. (2021), the calibration of the VPS model with observed lice numbers on wild post-smolt salmon caught by trawling in MAs 2–4 ensures that the model predictions of lice numbers on VPS are in accordance with observed lice levels on out-migrating post-smolt salmon from the rivers.

This comment presents a validation analysis of the Johnsen et al. (2021) model. Since estimates of parasite-induced mortality of VPS from rivers are deterministic with respect to lice numbers per fish (Johnsen et al., 2021), mortalities of modelled VPS should also coincide with comparable mortality estimates on captured wild postsmolts. Inspired by this, mortality of post-smolts caught by trawling in the outer areas of fjords along the Norwegian coast was estimated with the same methods as used by Johnsen et al. (2021) to estimate lice induced mortality of VPS. We then compare VPS mortality from rivers that drain into inner fjord areas to mortality estimated for post-smolts caught by trawling in corresponding outer fjordareas.Thesecomparisonsrestsontheassumptionthattrawled post-smolts caught during out-migration to the open ocean come from native rivers draining into the corresponding fjord further in (see Supplementary Figure S1). This assumption is supported by three lines of evidence: (i) the observation that post-smolts caught by trawling in outer fjord areas predominantly were genetically assigned to rivers draining into the corresponding fjord further in, and that only a very small proportion were assigned to rivers draining into an outlying fjord region (Harvey et al., 2019). Furthermore,

- (ii) studies show that the majority of migrating post-smolts move through fjords in a directional manner (e.g. Urke et al., 2013; Bjerck etal.,2021),althoughindividualvariationinroutesandprogression rates of migration also have been reported (Halttunen et al., 2018).
- (iii) Most importantly, the sub-model used to simulate VPS migration in Johnsen et al. (2021) suggests that post-smolts caught by trawl during out-migration come from native rivers draining into the corresponding fjord further in. The probability for migration to a lower fjord-index in their model, i.e. towards the ocean, was five times higher compared to staying at the current position or move to a grid with higher fjord-index, i.e. away from the open ocean.


This implies that it would be unlikely for VPS to migrate through a given trawling area, if it originated from a native river draining into an outlying fjord region. This can clearly be seen in Figure 2 in Johnsen et al. (2021).

### Data and methods

#### Mortality of trawled post-smolts

As part of the Norwegian surveillance program for salmon lice (NALO), outer fjord areas along the coast are trawled to catch outmigrating post-smolts annually. Salmon lice on these fish are specified to developmental stages and counted. Trawling data covering the years 2017–2020 were downloaded from the Norwegian marine data centre (Nilsen et al., 2017a-f; 2018a-f, 2019a-i, 2020af; NMDC; http://prod1.nmdc.no/UserInterface/#/). The data were filtered to contain only the species Atlantic salmon weighing more than 5 g and less than 50 g. Furthermore, tagged fish with any entry different from “0” or “” in the data-column headed “Merket” (tagged) were discarded (n = 339; Johnsen et al., 2021). In addition,fishwithcommentscontainingthestring“skjelltap”(scaleloss) in the data-column headed “Merknader” (remarks) were discarded (ignoring case; n = 326), since these apparently had scale-loss injuries and less lice (mean number of salmon lice per fish = 1.9) than those without scale-loss (mean number of salmon lice per fish = 3.05). Finally, we looked up the string “oppdrett” (hatchery reared fish) to ensure that all fish were of wild origin. We only found the string “oppdrett” for tagged fish, so no extra fish were discarded for this comment. The total number of trawled post-smolt salmon in the resulting dataset was 7929.

The number of salmon lice on a given fish, regardless of developmental stage, was used to estimate the mortality of post-smolts per trawling area and year with the same methods as used by Johnsen et al. (2021) to estimate VPS mortality. First, salmon lice abundance was calculated as the mean number of salmon lice per fish for each trawling area and each year. Salmon lice abundance was then multiplied by 0.6 (Johnsen et al., 2021), yielding an expected number of lice surviving to lice stages with adverse effects on smolts. From this expected number of harmful lice, the probability distribution of lice per fish was calculated, using a negative binomial distribution with a dispersion parameter = 0.3695 (Johnsen et al., 2021). Assuming that post-smolt weights were 20 g for all fish, the probability of mortality for each area/year sample of post-smolts was then calculated assuming 100% mortality for smolt with more than six lice, 50% mortality for smolt with four to six lice, 20% mortality for smolt with two to three lice, and 0% mortality for smolt with zero to one lice (Johnsen et al., 2021). Accordingly, % mortality in the population was calculated as:

M = p(L > 6) + 0.5 ∗ p(4 <= L <= 6)

+ 0.2 ∗ p(2 <= L <= 3) ∗ 100,

where p(L > 6), p(4 < = L < = 6), and p(2 < = L < = 3) is the probability of fish being infested with more than six harmful lice (L), four to six harmful lice, or two to three harmful lice, respectively, as estimated by the negative binomial distribution (Johnsen etal.,2021).Prevalence(%)ofinfestationwascalculatedasp(L>0) × 100. ### Results

The estimated mortality of post-smolts caught by trawling were lower than the average of predicted mortalities for the VPS from rivers draining into the corresponding fjords for 26 out of 27 year by area comparisons (Figure 1, Table 1). Differences between the compared mortality estimates were especially pronounced for the yearbyareasamplesofpost-smoltswithlowmortalityestimates,i.e. those with low salmon lice abundance (Figure 1). For the number of year by area samples falling into the three mortality classes >30%,

10–30%, or < 10%, there was a significant difference between the trawled post-smolts and VPS (X2 (2, N = 54) = 11.46, p = 0.003; Table 2).

While addressing this non-compliance between predicted VPS mortality and mortality estimates from trawled post-smolts, it has been argued that the present validation analysis does not account for further lice infestation of out-migrating post-smolts on their remaining migration pathways to the open ocean, after having been caught (Supplementary Figure S1). In the material and methods section in Johnsen et al. (2021), it is specified that VPS are followed until they reach the open ocean. Furthermore, the post-smolts used to parameterize the infestation sub-model were assigned “a lice pressure equal to the mean lice pressure experienced by matching VPS” (Johnsen et al., 2021). There is no mention or definition of remaining migration pathways to the open ocean for the geneticallyassignedpost-smolts,orhowthisishandledmethodologically. Nevertheless,asupplementaryanalysistoaddressthisissuewasundertaken (see the Supplementary Material). This analysis tests the hypothesis that the further post-smolt salmon are caught in the direction of the open ocean, the more salmon lice they will harbour. Separate regression analyses are performed on the effect of the relative progressed migration distance (RD) towards the open ocean on the number of lice per fish for each year by area sample of the trawled post-smoltsalmon (Supplementary Figures S2 and S3). One possible explanation for the discrepancy between the mortality estimates in Figure 1 could be that trawled post-smolts are not representative for the post-smolts migrating from the rivers draining into the corresponding fjords. This could be the case if trawl catches of post-smolts caught in a fjord region randomly consist of fish originating from rivers within the same region or from outlying regions. If so, this would compromise the migration modelling assumptions in Johnsen et al. (2021). Another possibility is that the averages of estimated VPS mortalities from rivers, which was used in this analysis, are not representative for the trawled post-smolts. We would argue that this is not likely given the biased relationship betweenmortalityestimates.Onthiscondition,trawlcatcheswould

consistently have to be dominated by post-smolt migration from rivers with relatively low mortality estimates for VPS.

The model system by Johnsen et al. (2021) covers many processes and sub-models in the chain of events leading from salmon lice infestation on farmed fish to the predicted lice-induced mortality of VPS from the rivers. Many of these processes, e.g. behaviour of planktonic lice coupled with the lice dispersion model or tolerance to salmon lice in post-smolts, are poorly understood. Biases in the parameterization of the different sub-models may potentially propagate through the modelling system. Due to the complexity of the model system presented by Johnsen et al. (2021), we do not attempt to explain the reasons for the systematic overestimation of lice-induced mortality of the migrating VPS, as compared to observed lice infestations and mortality estimates from post-smolts caught by trawling in the outer areas of the corresponding fjords. However, the present analysis contradicts the concluding remark in Johnsen et al. (2021): “The estimated lice level on modelled postsmoltsisshowntocoincidewiththelevelobservedoncapturedwild post-smolt”.
