# Summary: Jones et al. (2025)

**Full title:** Trends in Abundance of Sea Lice Lepeophtheirus salmonis and Caligus clemensi on Juvenile Wild Pacific Salmon Unchanged Following Cessation of Salmon Aquaculture in Coastal British Columbia
**Journal:** Journal of Fish Diseases, 2025; e14136
**DOI:** 10.1111/jfd.14136
**Document type:** Short communication (peer-reviewed)
**Priority questions:** Q1, Q9, Q10

---

## What this paper does

This short communication reports a longitudinal observational study examining sea lice infestations on juvenile wild Pacific salmon in the Discovery Islands (DI) region of coastal British Columbia, Canada, across 2017–2024. The study was designed to determine whether the removal of Atlantic salmon aquaculture from the area influenced sea lice levels on wild chum salmon (*Oncorhynchus keta*) and pink salmon (*O. gorbuscha*). All 15 Atlantic salmon netpen facilities in the study area were removed between 2020 and 2022, with zero production recorded from 2022 onwards — creating a natural experiment.

Methods followed DFO Broughton Archipelago beach-seine protocols (Hargreaves et al. 2004): up to 30 fish per species per site per month at **16 Discovery Islands sites** (Figure 1), frozen on dry ice, examined at BC-CAHS (Campbell River) with microscopic ID to species (*L. salmonis*, *C. clemensi*) and stage (copepodid, chalimus, pre-adult, adult). **5,967** juvenile salmon examined (3,481 chum, 2,486 pink), 2017–2024. Farm biomass = end-of-May closing biomass at **15 in-study-area Atlantic farms** (adjacent active farms excluded); **0 MT from 2022** after phased removals (2020–2022); two Chinook farms inactive since fall 2022.

The paper explicitly positions itself as an extension of Routledge and Morton (2023), who had attributed a 96% decline in sea lice abundance between 2020 and 2022 to the removal of aquaculture in the DI region. The present study adds the 2023 and 2024 survey data to test whether that attribution was correct.

---

## Main findings

- **Lice infestations persisted and rebounded after aquaculture removal.** In 2024, with zero Atlantic salmon production (0 MT farm biomass) since 2022, pink salmon mean total abundance (0.18 ± 0.03) exceeded all 2017–2019 values; chum salmon (0.15 ± 0.02) exceeded 2017 (0.09 ± 0.01) and 2018 (0.06 ± 0.01).

- **Farm biomass and lice levels decoupled post-2022.** Atlantic salmon biomass peaked at 18,126 MT (2020, 9 active facilities) then fell to 0 MT from 2022, yet 2024 lice levels rebounded — authors argue reported biomass did not adequately represent source infestation magnitude.

- **Year-to-year variability was high.** Chum prevalence 2.9% (2023) to 26.9% (2019); pink 0% (2023, no lice of either species detected) to 17.1% (2020). Chum mean total abundance ranged 0.04 (2023) to 0.39 (2019); pink 0 (2023) to 0.30 (2020).

- **The short-term decline (2020–2023) was not maintained.** Replicates Routledge & Morton (2023) on reduced lice 2020–2022, but 2024 rebound under continued zero aquaculture implicates environmental variability and natural reservoirs.

- *L. salmonis* comprised 57.3% of all lice on chum (25.0–74.2% by year) and 44.2% on pink (22.7–61.4%). Northwesterly juvenile migration and water flow reduce likelihood that two adjacent active farms contributed to observed infestations.

## Limitations and transferability to Norway

- **Short communication format:** Limited methodological detail; no formal statistical model testing the aquaculture–lice abundance relationship.
- **No reservoir host quantification:** The natural reservoir species (resident Pacific salmon, Pacific herring, threespine stickleback) were not sampled, making it impossible to quantify their relative contribution to observed infestation levels.
- **Only two post-removal years (2023–2024):** The time series is compelling but short on the post-removal side; more years are needed to confirm long-term persistence of natural infestations.
- **Environmental confounding:** No analysis of oceanographic or environmental covariates that might explain inter-annual variability independently of aquaculture presence.
- **Species difference:** Chum and pink salmon are Pacific species; they differ ecologically and behaviourally from Norwegian Atlantic salmon (*Salmo salar*) and sea trout (*S. trutta*).

**Transferability to Norway:** While the species and geography differ, the central question — whether farms are the primary driver of sea lice infestations on wild salmonids — is identical to the causal assumption underlying the Norwegian Traffic Light System. This paper, in combination with its Broughton Archipelago companion (Jones et al. 2025, DAO), provides the strongest available natural experiment evidence that the farm-attribution assumption may be substantially incorrect. Norwegian TLS assessments treat farm lice as the dominant or sole source of lice on wild fish; these BC results call that assumption into question. The degree to which natural reservoirs in Norwegian fjords play a comparable role remains an open and important question for the corpus.

## Place in the literature

This paper is the most recent in a long-running debate in BC salmon science about the causal role of salmon farms in wild fish lice infestations, which has parallels in Norway. It directly extends and contradicts Routledge and Morton (2023), who claimed that the initial lice decline following aquaculture removal demonstrated a causal link. The 2024 data decisively weakens that interpretation. The paper aligns with two other recent papers cited in its text: van Nes et al. (2025), who found models may overestimate the aquaculture contribution to wild fish infestations, and Jeong et al. (2025), who found that infestations in several BC areas could not be attributed solely to aquaculture. Together, these form a coherent recent body of evidence challenging the dominant attribution assumption. The companion Broughton Archipelago paper (Jones et al. 2025, DAO) reports an essentially identical finding in a different BC coastal region over an overlapping time period, strongly corroborating the Discovery Islands result.
