## What this paper does

Bøhn et al. (2020) investigate the impact of salmon lice (*Lepeophtheirus salmonis*) on the survival of Atlantic salmon (*Salmo salar*) postsmolts during their migration from freshwater to marine environments. The study specifically examines whether the timing of migration relative to peaks in lice density affects survival rates. Conducted in the Hardangerfjord region of Norway, the research leverages a unique natural experiment created by synchronized fallowing of fish farms, which significantly altered lice densities between the years 2013 and 2014.

The authors released a total of **29,817** coded-wire-tagged postsmolts in May and June of 2013 (low lice density due to fallowing) and 2014 (high lice density). The results are critical for understanding the ecological dynamics between farmed and wild salmon populations, particularly in the context of Norway's aquaculture industry, which has seen a dramatic increase in farmed salmon biomass. This study contributes to the ongoing discourse on the management of salmon lice and its implications for wild salmon populations, especially under the pressures of climate change and increasing aquaculture production.

---

## Methods and data

The study employed a randomized controlled trial (RCT) design, releasing postsmolts in four trials across two years (2013 and 2014) with a total of **n = 29,817** fish. Each trial involved two groups: one treated with SubEX prophylaxis (approximately 50% of the fish) and a control group receiving sham treatment. The fish were released near the mouth of the River Etne, and their returns were monitored over a period of four years using a resistance-board weir trap with approximately **90%** capture efficiency.

Lice infestation pressure was assessed using sentinel cages, which measured lice counts over a 14-day period, and hydrodynamic models from **lakseslus.no** to estimate lice dispersion. The study defined thresholds for lice infestation pressure based on the Norwegian traffic light system, categorizing lice-induced mortality into green (0–10%), yellow (10–30%), and red (>30%) zones.

## Key findings

### Release design

| 2013 | 18 May       | 3,791     | 3,972     | 72±21      |
| 2013 | 9 June       | 3,801     | 3,868     | 74±16      |
| 2014 | 18 May       | 3,819     | 3,818     | 47±11      |
| 2014 | 9 June       | 3,770     | 2,978     | 42±10      |

**559** fish were recaptured in total (1–4 sea winters).

### Return rates and risk ratios

| Trial       | Pattern                       | RR (95% CI)         |
|-------------|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| May 2013    | Controls returned more        | 0.52 (0.35–0.76)    |
| June 2013   | ~equal                        | 1.05 (0.81–1.42)    |
| May 2014    | ~equal                        | 1.13 (0.85–1.50)    |
| **June 2014** | **Catastrophic control loss** | **55.3 (7.7–398)**  |

- **June 2014 control:** 1/2,978 returned (**0.03%**)
- **June 2014 treated:** 70/3,770 returned (**1.86%**)
- Most other groups: **1–2.5%** return (typical marine survival band)

### Lice infestation pressure (sentinel cages)

- May→June increase: **~10×** in 2013 (0.06→0.66 lice/fish/14 d); **~25×** in 2014 (0.37→8.87)
- **2013 fallow** reduced pressure **84–92%** vs 2014 full production
- Lowest pressure consistently at protected inner site Etne

### Statistical model (logistic GLM on return)

- Lice Infestation Pressure: **negative** (estimate −2.76, p<0.0001)
- Treatment (prophylaxis): **positive** (0.66, p<0.0001)
- **Lice × treatment interaction** significant (2.71, p<0.0001) — prophylaxis helps at high lice, can hurt at low lice

### Policy context (Norwegian TLS)

The paper references the TLS colour rules: **green** 0–10%, **yellow** 10–30%, **red** >30% lice-induced mortality of wild salmon; the untreated scenario in June 2014 far exceeds the red threshold. The context of farm biomass is significant: **386 M** farmed vs **~0.5 M** wild returns (2017).

## Limitations and caveats

The study has several limitations:
- The fish used were hatchery-reared F1 smolts, not fully wild fish, which may affect generalizability.
- Lice exposure was inferred from cages/models rather than direct counts on experimental fish during migration.
- The study cannot separate predation or starvation from lice as causes of mortality, focusing only on treatment contrasts.
- The research was conducted in a single intensive fjord system, which may limit the applicability of findings to other regions.

---

## What this paper does not claim

The authors do not claim that all mortality in wild salmon is solely attributable to lice, nor do they assert that prophylactic treatments are universally effective across all contexts. They also do not address the potential long-term ecological impacts of increased aquaculture production on wild salmon populations beyond the immediate effects of lice.

---

## Relevance to this corpus

This study directly addresses questions related to the timing of salmon migration and its interaction with lice density (Q4), providing empirical evidence of mortality risk differentials under varying lice pressures (Q8). It serves as a field benchmark for evaluating the ecological plausibility of TLS mortality bands in extreme fjord environments (Q10).

## Related evidence in corpus

The findings complement existing literature on the effects of lice on wild salmon populations, particularly studies that have documented similar mortality patterns under high lice pressures. This paper can be linked to ongoing debates regarding the management of aquaculture and its impacts on wild salmonid populations, as seen in related studies like Fjelldal et al. (2020) and modelling papers by Johnsen et al. (2021) and Jansen & Gjerde (2021).
