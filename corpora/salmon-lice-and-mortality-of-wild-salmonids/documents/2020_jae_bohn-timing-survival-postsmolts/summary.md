# Summary: Bøhn et al. (2020)

**Full title:** Timing is everything: Survival of Atlantic salmon Salmo salar postsmolts during events of high salmon lice densities
**Journal:** Journal of Applied Ecology, 57: 1149–1160
**DOI:** 10.1111/1365-2664.13612
**Document type:** Research article (randomized controlled trial)
**Priority questions:** Q4, Q8

---

## What this paper does

This study investigates whether the timing of Atlantic salmon smolt migration relative to seasonal peaks in salmon lice density determines lice-induced mortality. The central premise is that lice populations follow temperature-driven seasonal cycles — building up from early spring to peak in late spring/early summer — and that smolts which migrate early (before the lice peak) face radically different infestation pressure than those migrating later.

To test this, the authors designed four large-scale randomized controlled trials (RCTs) in Hardangerfjord, western Norway. A total of n = 29,817 tagged Atlantic salmon postsmolts (first-generation hatchery-reared, River Etne broodstock) were released in May and June of both 2013 and 2014. The year contrast was critical: in 2013, all farms in the outer Hardangerfjord were fallowed (emptied), resulting in near-zero lice pressure; in 2014, farms returned to full production (~95,000 metric tonnes), producing high lice pressure. Within each year, the May vs. June release timing further tested early- vs. late-migration scenarios. In each trial, 50% of fish were treated with SubEX prophylaxis (prevents lice from developing past copepodid stage for ~16 weeks) and 50% received sham treatment. Lice infestation pressure was characterized using sentinel cage data and hydrodynamic dispersion modelling.

Returning fish were captured in a high-efficiency fish trap (~90% capture rate) in the Etne River over 2014–2017 (1–4 sea winters), providing robust recapture data with minimal bias.

---

## Main findings

### Return rates by treatment and trial

In most experimental groups, 1–2.5% of released fish were recaptured upon return, consistent with baseline Atlantic salmon sea survival rates. However, the June 2014 untreated control group was a catastrophic outlier:

- **June 2014 controls (high lice, late migration):** 0.03% return rate
- **June 2014 treated (high lice, late migration, prophylaxis):** 1.86% return rate
- **Ratio:** >50-fold higher mortality risk for untreated fish

### Lice infestation pressure contrast

Sentinel cage data confirmed the planned contrast: lice infestation pressure in June 2014 was dramatically higher than in any 2013 release group. The hydrodynamic model showed >10 lice/fish across most of the smolt migration corridor in June 2014, versus <1 lice/fish in 2013.

### Timing effect

May releases generally experienced lower lice pressure than June releases within the same year. The compound effect of (a) high lice year and (b) late migration timing drove the extreme mortality in the June 2014 control group.

### Growth at return

No significant differences in body size at return were detected between treated and control groups, suggesting that survivors in control groups did not experience measurable growth disadvantages (though the extreme mortality in June 2014 controls precluded robust analysis for that group).

## Limitations

- Hatchery-reared F1 smolts used as surrogates for fully wild smolts; lice susceptibility may differ
- Lice infestation pressure estimated via sentinel cages and hydrodynamic models, not directly measured on experimental fish during migration
- Marine mortality is multi-causal (predation, starvation, disease); lice contribution cannot be cleanly isolated, only the treated vs. untreated differential is attributable to lice
- Study conducted in Hardangerfjord, one of Norway's highest-density aquaculture fjords; results may overestimate risk in lower-density regions
- Prophylaxis window (~16 weeks) means some control vs. treated asymmetry may dissolve in late stages of the marine phase

## Place in the literature

Bøhn et al. (2020) is one of the most consequential papers in the Norwegian salmon lice debate, providing the clearest experimental demonstration that aquaculture-driven lice can cause near-total smolt mortality when ecological timing aligns unfavourably. It resolves part of the apparent conflict in earlier RCT results (e.g. Skilbrei et al. 2013): lice effects are not always detectable, but when smolt migration coincides with lice peaks, the effect is catastrophic. The paper directly contextualizes the Norwegian Traffic Light System, noting that the June 2014 scenario vastly exceeded the red-light threshold of >30% lice-induced mortality.

In combination with physiological mechanism studies such as Fjelldal et al. (2020), which define osmoregulatory thresholds for individual-level mortality, and monitoring methods papers such as Helland et al. (2015), which assess the ability of monitoring programmes to detect such events, this paper anchors the empirical field-experiment end of the salmon lice causal chain.
