# Effects of salmon lice on numbers and size distributions of Atlantic salmon returning to spawn in Norwegian rivers

### Peder A. Jansen1,2 | Henning André Urke1 | Solveig Engebretsen2 | Magne Aldrin2 | Kari O. Helgesen3

1AquaLife R&D, Trondheim, Norway

#### Abstract

- 2Norwegian Computing Center, Oslo, Norway
- 3Norwegian Veterinary Institute, Ås, Norway


- 1. Negative effects of salmon lice from salmon farms on wild salmonids have been a controversial issue for decades and concerns are expressed from virtually all areas where salmon farming coexists with important fisheries for wild salmonids. A key question is to what extent lice infestation from farms reduce numbers of wild mature salmon returning to spawn in the rivers. Here, we study counts and recreational catches of small (<3 kg), medium-sized (3–7 kg) and large mature salmon (>7 kg) returning to spawn in Norwegian rivers, in association with lice burdens on out-migrating post-smolt recruits.
- 2. The expected number of returning mature salmon was modelled as a function of theoretical smolt production in the rivers, river catches one generation back in time, size-classes of returning salmon, year as a factor, a non-linear spatial effect and parasite-induced mortality (PIM) of out-migrating post smolts of salmon. PIM was attributed to small, medium-sized and large salmon assuming they spend one, two or three winters at sea (SW), respectively.
- 3. There was a significant negative effect of PIM on returns of one SW salmon and a negative but non-significant effect on returns of two SW salmon. For three SW salmon, the effect of PIM was significantly positive, but for comparably low numbers, implying an overall negative effect of PIM on returning salmon. The sizespecific effects of PIM were manifested by decreasing proportions of one SW salmon in returning populations with increasing PIM, from ~0.6 for rivers exposed to low levels of PIM, to predictions of <0.2 for rivers exposed to high levels of PIM.
- 4. Synthesis and applications. This study presents a quantitative relationship between infestations of post-smolt recruits and size-structured returns of mature salmon to Norwegian rivers, suggesting that louse infestation from farms may reduce returns of spawners and re-structure the size distribution of mature river populations of Atlantic salmon. #### 1 | INTRODUCTION

Wild stocks and catches of Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) are at historically low levels, especially in the Southern North-East Atlantic but also in the Northern North-East Atlantic (ICES, 2025). Return rates of adult Atlantic salmon from their feeding grounds in the marine environment have declined since the 1980s, even after the closure of marine fisheries. According to the International Council for the Exploration of the Sea (ICES), climatic factors modifying ecosystem conditions and the impact of predators of Atlantic salmon at sea are the main contributing factors for lower return rates of mature Atlantic salmon (ICES, 2025). The status of Norwegian wild Atlantic salmon populations is also reported to be historically low (Thorstad et al., 2024). Furthermore, the number of returning small salmon weighing less than 3 kg has apparently declined more than medium-sized salmon (3–7 kg) and large salmon (>7 kg), and this is especially pronounced for rivers in western and central Norway. There is a conflict of interest between salmon farming and wild salmon due to the spillover of parasitic louse infestation from farmed salmon, especially the salmon louse (Lepeophtheirus salmonis). The conflict and interactions are illustrated in Figure 1.

Negative effects of salmon lice from salmon farms on wild salmonids have been a controversial issue for decades and concerns are expressed from virtually all areas where salmon farming coexists with important fisheries for wild salmonids, such as in Canada, Ireland, Scotland and Norway (e.g. Krkosek et al., 2007; Larsen et al., 2024; Moriarty et al., 2023; Shephard & Gargan, 2017, 2021). Typically, however, there are also reports from many of these areas that question the severity of effects of farm-origine salmon lice on wild salmonids (e.g. Jackson et al., 2011, 2013; Jansen & Gjerde, 2021; Marty et al., 2010; Van Nes et al., 2024). Hence, the extent to which infestations of parasitic lice from salmon farms lead to reductions in adult salmon returns remains a subject of debate (Larsen et al., 2024).

During their early coastal migration, they are small, only about 20–30 g, and vulnerable to predation and parasitic louse infestation. Concurrent to the declining wild stocks of Atlantic salmon, salmon farming in open-net pens in coastal areas has grown rapidly. Norway is the main producer worldwide with a share of production varying between 42% and 66% over the years 1992–2018 (Iversen et al., 2020). From the mid-1990s to the beginning of the 2020s, Norwegian production of farmed salmon increased nearly fivefold, to about 106 tonnes per year, but with a tendency to level off, or even decrease in areas with intensive farming, in later years (Stige et al., 2024). The major constraint on production growth is the parasitic salmon louse which transmits through planktonic stages between open net-pens with farmed salmon. This transmission is a density-dependent host–parasite process (Jansen et al., 2012), which has escalated since farmed hosts now massively outnumber wild natural hosts along the Norwegian coast (Dempster et al., 2021). Also, since salmon farms are located in coastal areas shared with populations of wild salmonids, growing concern and evidence for negative effects of the spillover of salmon louse infestation from farms to wild salmonids are expressed (e.g. Hawley et al., 2024; Larsen et al., 2024; Vollset et al., 2023). Regulations to control salmon lice in areas experiencing high abundances of salmon lice are now directed at reducing the numbers of farmed salmon (FOR-2017_61, 2017).

In the present study, our aim was to explore the effects of salmon louse infestation produced in salmon farms on the returning numbers of small, medium-sized and large Atlantic salmon to Norwegian rivers. Size-structured population data on counts of returning mature salmon and reported catches in recreational fisheries over the years 2019–2024 are modelled as a function of estimates of parasiteinduced mortality (PIM) of out-migrating recruits of post-smolts of salmon for each river and size-class of salmon. The theoretical estimates of PIM are based on relationships between the abundances of reproducing female salmon lice on salmon farms, louse infestations on post-smolts of wild salmon caught by trawl during out-migration in coastal areas in Norway and assumed thresholds for louse-induced mortality (see Johnsen et al., 2021a; Stige et al., 2021, 2022). Hence, the present study bridges the gap between theoretical estimates of PIM due to the abundances of lice in farms and the returning numbers of mature Atlantic salmon in Norwegian rivers.

#### 2 | MATERIALS AND METHODS

The data analysed in the present study were compiled from publicly available data sources and are accessible (Jansen et al., 2025).

#### 2.1 | Size-structured population data on returning mature salmon

The focus of the present analysis is river populations of returning mature Atlantic salmon. Data on river populations of returning

salmon were compiled from two publicly available data sources: 1. wild salmon catch data from recreational fishery; and 2. counts of returned mature salmon in rivers (see Appendix S1 in Supporting Information). Both data sources comprise yearly numbers of small (<3 kg), medium-sized (3–7 kg) and large (>7 kg) salmon. These size classes reflect the number of winters spent at sea before returning to spawn (e.g. Jensen et al., 1999; L'Abée-Lund et al., 2004; Otero et al., 2012). Hence, we assume that small salmon have spent one SW, medium-sized salmon two SW and large salmon three SW (Larsen et al., 2024). The counting data span the period 2019–2024, whereas the catch data extend further back in time.

The recreational catch data comprised official government statistics of salmon caught by rod in the rivers, which are mandatory to report (see Appendix S1). The rivers are open to fishing mainly in the period June–August. Catches are designated caught (not released) or released. In our definition of river populations of returning salmon, we only included salmon designated caught (and not released) and spanning the period 2019–2024. This is because we assume that released salmon would later be counted and hence be part of the count data.

The count data are gathered by different research institutions and teams as part of a national surveillance programme, financed by the Norwegian Environment Agency and publicly available at the website https://bestand.nina.no/#/. Altogether, we compiled data from 143 rivers and 560 river-years. However, we filtered out parts of the data for the present analysis based on our own judgement. This excluded data based on five criteria: 1. rivers with known infections with Gyrodactylus salaris; 2. the counting method was by video; 3. when less than 70% of the anadromous stretch of rivers was surveyed or if this information was missing; 4. when conditions for counting were described as poor; 5. rivers where we could not find estimates of PIM. After filtering out data, the dataset consisted of counts of the three size-classes of returning salmon in 104 rivers and 396 river-years. More detail and reasoning behind the data filtering and the sensitivity of the qualitative results to the filtering are given in the Supporting Information.

The final estimates of the returning numbers of salmon were calculated as the sum of the counted number corrected for survey proportion, and the number of salmon reported caught in recreational fisheries:

The number of salmon returning to spawn depends on the size of the rivers. To account for river size, we compiled data on estimated theoretical smolt production, TS, in rivers (see Appendix S1). The number of salmon returning to spawn may also depend on the actual number of out-migrating smolts in previous years. Data on out-migrating smolt, however, are generally not available. Here, we constructed a proxy variable for out-migrating smolts from weighted recreational catches of salmon in the rivers one generation prior to returning one SW, two SW and three SW salmon. This variable we denote catch (C) in one prior generation (G) weighed (W), that is CGW. More detail on the calculation of CGW is given in Appendix S1.

For salmon rivers along the Norwegian coast, estimates of postsmolt parasite-induced mortality (PIM) due to salmon lice from aquaculture are produced yearly by the Norwegian Veterinary Institute (NVI; Figure 2) and by the Institute of Marine Research, Norway (IMR), as part of the ‘Traffic Light Management System’ (TLS) for salmonid aquaculture in Norway (Eliasen et al., 2021; Johnsen et al., 2021a; Stige et al., 2021, 2022). The NVI data used in this main paper were accessed by downloading the supplementary data in Stige et al. (2023b). For PIM for a given river and year, we used the column headed ‘Doede’ in the NVI data (Figure 2). We also downloaded and tested the effects of alternative indices of salmon louse exposure produced in the TLS (Appendix S1).

Figure 2 reveals a spatial pattern with high levels of PIM in the southwest and lower levels in the northeast and far south. To account for spatial correlation, we included a numeric geographical index (geo) from the first three digits of the river.id. This index increases along the Norwegian coast, from a value of 30 for the two rivers in the far south to 244 for the northeasternmost river (Figure S1; Jansen et al., 2025).

#### 3 | RESULTS

The estimated statistical model (Equation 2) showed a significant (p <0.05) positive effect of 1-PIM for returning one SW salmon, with an estimated coefficient (2.883) suggesting that reduced returns of one SW salmon due to salmon lice exceed estimates of PIM for the given river and year class of smolts (Table 1). The effect of 1-PIM for two SW salmon was positive but non-significant (p =0.1), whereas a significant negative effect of 1-PIM was estimated for three SW salmon (Table 1; Figure 3).

There was a significant negative effect of year 2024 compared to the reference year 2019, such that one would expect a factor 1- exp(−0.45)=36% fewer returning salmon in 2024 compared to 2019. The effect of theoretical smolt production, TS, was such that if TS was doubled, then the expected number of returning mature salmon would increase by a factor 20.51 =1.44, that is, an increase of 44%. A comparable inference for the effect of recreational river catches in the previous generation, CGW, implies that, for a doubling of (CGW+1), the expected number of returning salmon spawners would increase by a factor 20.20 =1.15. If both TS and (CGW+1) were doubled, the expected number of returning salmon spawners would increase by a factor 1.44·1.15=1.66. This is less than 2 because both TS and CGW are only crude proxies for the true migrating smolt population to which the returning spawners are related.

The effect of size-class as a factor implies that 49% (since 1 – exp.(−0.68)=0.49) fewer two SW salmon than one SW salmon are expected to return to a river with PIM=0. Similarly, 91% fewer returning three SW salmon than one SW salmon are expected (1-exp(−2.40)=0.91).

The estimates of the non-linear effect of the geographical index geo were generally high in the far south and north and low in the west and middle parts of Norway (see Figures S1 and S2), ranging from a max of 0.784 in the far south to a min of −0.422 in the middle parts. This range implies an expectation of a factor exp(0.784+0.422)=3.34 more returning salmon in rivers with a maximal estimate of the geo effect compared to rivers with a minimal geo effect. The median of the predicted s(geo)=−0.149 for geo=86, implying about 14% lower returns than to a comparable river with a geo effect of 0.

- 4 | DISCUSSION


4.1 | Association between salmon lice from farms and returning mature salmon

The present analysis suggests that the number and distribution of size-classes of mature salmon returning to Norwegian rivers to spawn are associated with exposure of out-migrating salmon postsmolts to salmon lice from salmon farms. Especially, the numbers of returning small salmon appear to be significantly reduced in rivers where out-migrating post-smolts are heavily exposed to salmon lice. To a lesser degree, returns of medium-sized salmon tended to decrease as a function of PIM, although this result was not significant (p =0.1). Returns of large salmon, however, increased significantly as a function of PIM, but this was from comparably low numbers of returns. Hence, in sum, the numbers of returning salmon were negatively associated with post-smolt exposure to salmon lice. The size-related difference in the effects of salmon lice from salmon farms also appears to influence the size and seaage distribution of the returning populations. The present study thus adds to the evidence of a negative impact of louse infestation from salmon farms on populations of wild salmonids (e.g. Larsen et al., 2024; Otero et al., 2011; Vollset et al., 2023). The apparent effects are at the level of returning mature salmon which is the focus of interest, for example, regarding fisheries of wild salmon and ultimately for conservation (ICES, 2025; Larsen et al., 2024; Thorstad et al., 2024).

In all the various submodels tested, and for all subsets of data (see Appendix S1), the effect of PIM on small one SW salmon was significant. In these different regression models, the estimated coefficient for PIM related to one SW salmon varied from about 1, with a 1:1 relationship between PIM and expected returns, to about 3.1, with expected reductions in returns surpassing estimates of PIM. In the presented main model, we included a smooth spatial effect to account for spatial correlation. This model resulted in a non-significant effect of PIM for two SW salmon and a significant negative effect for three SW salmon. However, in models where the spatial effect was excluded, the effect of PIM was significantly positive for two SW salmon and negative but non-significant for three SW salmon. This was true also for separate models for the various size classes. As expected, including the spatial geo-index effect increased the amount of variance explained by the model but also implied increased estimated uncertainties in the effects of PIM especially for two and three SW salmon.

The predicted proportion of mature small salmon returning after one SW appeared to decrease from around 60% in rivers with low exposure of post smolts to salmon lice, to less than 20% in rivers with high exposure (Figure 4). The proportion of small salmon, or ‘grilse’, in Norwegian rivers and the sea-age at maturity, has been the focus of several previous studies (e.g. L'Abée-Lund et al., 2004; Otero et al., 2011, 2012). Otero et al. (2012) analysed time series of recreational fishing catches of one SW and two SW salmon and found that age at maturity of returning salmon increased over the years 1991–2005, suggesting large-scale changes in the pelagic food web of the north Atlantic that affect post-smolt growth. Such changes in the food web, with possible negative effects on prey availability and growth of salmon in the sea, have recently gained support (Utne et al., 2022; Vollset et al., 2022). Impaired feeding conditions at sea have also been argued to be the reason for a drop in the proportion of one SW salmon returning to spawn in Norwegian rivers (Thorstad et al., 2024) and appear to have caused a general decreasing trend in returns of one SW salmon, both in the Northern East Atlantic and Southern East Atlantic areas (ICES, 2025). Although poor feeding conditions in the sea seem to play an important role in the ability of salmon to mature as grilse, it is unlikely to fully explain the comparably fine-scale association between salmon louse infestation from aquaculture and the estimated reductions in returning one SW and two SW mature salmon presented here. The reductions in returning mature salmon estimated for rivers with heavily infestation-exposed post-smolts are anticipated due to mortality induced by salmon lice from salmon farms (Johnsen et al., 2021a; Larsen et al., 2024; Stige et al., 2021). It is interesting, however, that the effects of estimated parasite exposure appear to vary between one SW, two SW and three SW salmon. This difference may be because salmon lice hamper growth in the early sea phase of post-smolts and thereby delay maturation in surviving fish. The fact that salmon louse infestation hampers salmon growth is well established, both in laboratory experiments (Tveiten et al., 2010) and in experimental field studies (Skilbrei et al., 2013; Vollset et al., 2019). Although we acknowledge that our hypothesis implying that the size-specific effects of PIM may be due to parasitic morbidity hampering growth and thus delaying maturity is speculative, it accords with expectations arising in an individual-based model for this host– parasite system (Vollset, 2019). When Vollset's model was parameterized to allow for salmon lice to affect the growth of the salmon, it had a strong increasing effect on age at maturity. However interesting, we do not speculate further on the population dynamics of this host–parasite interaction here but encourage future studies.

#### 4.2 | Implications for aquaculture management

The present study presents a quantitative relationship between salmon louse infestations in farms and returning mature salmon. Although there are uncertainties with respect to the effects of louse infestations in farms on returns of mature salmon to individual rivers, the quantitative relationship presented herein opens for a more targeted approach to obtaining sustainable salmon farming. In a recent white paper, the Norwegian Government maintains that their goal for the management of salmon farming is that farming on its own merit should contribute less than 10% mortality of wild salmonid populations due to salmon lice (Ministry of Trade, Industry and Fisheries, 2025). With the presented model, this level of reduced returns of mature spawners is reached in rivers where PIM is estimated at 5%–6%, implying that more than 70% of the present riveryear data exceeds this level of estimated louse exposure (Figure 2). Alternatively, since the deposition of eggs during spawning is related to the weight of the fish, the weight of the spawning population could be used as a management criterion. The level of reduced returns of mature spawners is thus reached in rivers where PIM is estimated at ~10%, implying that about 60% of the river-year data exceeds this level. We would argue, however, that the present quantitative relationship between PIM and returning adult salmon will permit more targeted estimates of the necessary reductions in abundances of salmon lice in farms to obtain acceptable levels of returning salmon.

Aiming to ensure environmental sustainability of salmon farming, Norwegian authorities implemented a new management system, the ‘Traffic Light System’ (TLS), as of 2017. The TLS regulates production at the level of 13 production areas along the Norwegian coast (FOR-2017_61, 2017). The sole basis for assessments preceding political decisions in the system is the risk of wild Atlantic salmon mortality due to salmon louse infestation from salmon farms. Decisions every second year on future production under the TLS are regulated by thresholds of <10%, 10%–30% and >30% louseinduced mortality of wild salmon, implying opportunities to increase production by 6%, keep production at the current level or impose reduced production by 6%. As part of the basis for assessments in the system, different models are run providing yearly estimates of parasite-induced mortalities of out-migrating virtual post-smolts of salmon from Norwegian rivers (Skardhamar et al., 2023; Stige et al., 2023a). The magnitude of estimated mortalities of postsmolts in these models and the impact on returns of mature salmon have been questioned. One line of criticism relates to the estimated mortality outcome of the models, that is, the dose–response relationship between louse infestation and post-smolt mortality (Van Nes et al., 2024). A second line of criticism relates to the uncertainties and variable outcomes of various models that have been used to estimate infestation and parasite-induced mortality of outmigrating post-smolts of salmon in the TLS (Johnsen et al., 2021a; Kristoffersen et al., 2018; Stige et al., 2021) and claims of discrepancies between observed and modelled infestations on out-migrating post-smolts (Jansen & Gjerde, 2021; Johnsen et al., 2021b). With respect to diverging interests among various interest groups, an international Evaluation Committee was appointed to evaluate the scientific basis of the TLS (Eliasen et al., 2021). The Evaluation Committee gave recommendations to ensure the transparency and ongoing legitimacy of the TLS approach. Among these, it was recommended to undertake peer-reviewed studies to ‘provide data on in situ effects of lice infestation on wild salmon at an individual and population level’ (Eliasen et al., 2021). The present study complies with this recommendation, and, although there are uncertainties regarding the magnitude of effects, the results are more in support of the model outcomes used in the TLS (e.g. Skardhamar et al., 2023; Stige et al., 2023a) than the critical studies (Jansen & Gjerde, 2021; Van Nes et al., 2024).

Wild stocks and catches of Atlantic salmon are reported to be at historically low levels (ICES, 2025; Thorstad et al., 2024). In the present analysis, the returning numbers of mature salmon in 2024 were reduced by about one-third compared to returns in 2019. This

reduction was not attributed to parasite-induced mortality in the analysis but is probably due to reduced survival in the sea for other reasons. Nevertheless, the apparent effects of parasite-induced mortality adding to probably unfavourable conditions for sea survival for Atlantic salmon (Utne et al., 2022; Vollset et al., 2022) emphasize the need for controlling louse infestations in salmon farms to conserve natural populations of Atlantic salmon in areas with intense farming activity (ICES, 2025; Thorstad et al., 2024).

#### 4.3 | Notable limitations

To our knowledge, the present data from the ongoing surveillance programme on counts of mature salmon in the rivers have not previously been presented in association with farm-origin salmon louse infestation to assess possible effects on wild salmon populations. These data are gathered by different research institutions and teams as part of a national surveillance programme, financed by the Norwegian Environment Agency. Obviously, varying conditions, water flow in different rivers, different teams gathering data, etc. may influence the quality of the data, but we have no reason to suspect that there are any systematic biases in the data. However, we filtered out parts of the data based on our own judgement. The sensitivity of the results to these filtering choices has been thoroughly tested without affecting the qualitative conclusions (see Appendix S1).

One weakness of the present analyses is the need to assume a stringent relationship between salmon size and sea-age. This must misclassify some of the returning mature salmon and probably more so for large salmon than for small salmon. For example, some large salmon spend 4 years at sea, and some may have returned to spawn more than once (Fleming, 1996; Fleming & Einum, 2011; Jensen et al., 1999). However, the proportion of salmon having spent more than three SW seems generally rather small (Jensen et al., 1999). Nevertheless, possible misclassification of sea-age increases the uncertainties of the present conclusions, but we cannot see how we would have obtained the relationship between size distributions of mature salmon and parasite-induced mortality, unless there is a sizespecific effect of salmon lice on returns of small, medium-sized and large mature salmon.

The effects of parasite-induced mortality of post-smolt salmon on salmon returns in the present analysis are, on the one hand, rather robust. The subdivisions of the data into different years gave significant effects of PIM on one SW salmon in all years, except 2021 (see Appendix S1). On the other hand, PIM was not the dominant covariate to explain variance in the data. To predict the returns of mature salmon to individual rivers, theoretical smolt production and recreational catches in the previous generation provided most of the information. This is not surprising since these variables vary by orders of magnitude between small and large salmon rivers. Nevertheless, predictions from the present model suggest an overall reduction in returning salmon spawners for rivers where post-smolts are burdened by salmon lice from salmon farms.

In the regression model (Equation 2), it is assumed that random noise, r,y,s, is independent and normally distributed. However, this assumption is violated to some extent since PIM for a given river and year is related to one SW salmon the following year, two SW salmon the following second year and three SW salmon the following third year. We acknowledge that violation of the independence assumption may underestimate the uncertainty of coefficient estimates. However, separate models for the size-classes where this independence assumption is not violated within each model, gave qualitatively similar results (see Appendix S1, Section 2.2). We argue that the violation of the assumption that the random noise from the full model is independent, has little implication for the estimated coefficients and predictions from the full model. The advantage of the full model compared to the separate models for each size-class is that variables shared by all size-classes are estimated to a common effect.

#### 5 | CONCLUSIONS

The present analysis presents a quantitative relationship between abundances of salmon lice in farms, expressed as theoretical estimates of parasite-induced mortality of out-migrating postsmolts, and returns of small, medium-sized and large mature salmon to rivers. The apparent size-dependent effects imply not only reduced numbers of returning mature salmon to rivers where outmigrating post-smolts are heavily burdened by farm lice, but also that lice burdens will affect the size and sea-age distribution of river populations of returning mature salmon. These effects, appearing at the level of observations of returning mature salmon in Norwegian rivers, add to the evidence of a negative impact of louse infestation originating from salmon farms on populations of wild salmonids.

The quantitative relationship between abundances of salmon lice in farms and returning mature salmon to rivers presented herein opens for a more targeted approach to obtaining sustainable salmon farming. The Norwegian Government maintains that a goal for future salmon farming in Norway is that it should contribute less than 10% additional mortality of wild populations of salmonids. To obtain this goal, substantial reductions in abundances of salmon lice in farms are necessary. The present analysis also opens for estimating the extent of louse reductions that are necessary in farms to obtain goals of acceptable impact on returning adult salmon to given rivers. At present, however, such estimations would come with noticeable uncertainty.

Since the present study is based on correlations between abundances of lice in farms and numbers of returning mature salmon to rivers, we cannot exclude that the results are influenced by other unknown confounding or mediating factors. However, the present study provides predictions of both returning numbers and size and sea-age distributions of mature salmon populations as functions of louse-induced effects. With longer time series of river population data and efforts to reduce farm infestations, there should be opportunities to further explore time-varying effects of salmon lice from farms on returns of mature salmon to rivers, and then hopefully with increasing certainty.
