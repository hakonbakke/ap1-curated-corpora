## Background

This is a **data descriptor paper** — it does not analyse or interpret the data, but documents, validates, and publicly releases a large longitudinal dataset of sea lice monitoring from the coast of British Columbia (BC), Canada. The dataset covers both farmed Atlantic salmon and multiple species of wild Pacific salmonids across 7 DFO (Department of Fisheries and Oceans) fish health sub-zones, spanning 22 years (2001–2023).

BC has been one of the most contested settings for the farm lice–wild salmon debate globally. Key events documented in this dataset include the 2021 closure of Atlantic salmon farms in the Discovery Islands, which creates a rare natural experiment for studying what happens to wild fish lice levels when farm hosts are removed.

---

## Methods

The dataset integrates records from multiple monitoring programmes operating in parallel:
- **Farm monitoring**: Mandatory lice counts reported by all active Atlantic salmon farms under DFO licensing requirements (approx. 100 farms)
- **Wild fish sampling**: Lice counts on wild Pacific salmonids from lethal and non-lethal sampling, conducted through multiple independent programmes

Data were harmonised across programmes, standardised where possible, and documented with provenance metadata. The paper describes data collection methods, quality checks, and known limitations for each contributing programme.

Wild fish sampling covered: chum salmon (*Oncorhynchus keta*), pink salmon (*O. gorbuscha*), coho salmon (*O. kisutch*), chinook salmon (*O. tshawytscha*), and others. Lice counted include both *Lepeophtheirus salmonis* and *Caligus clemensi*.

## Key Findings

**Scale and coverage**
The dataset contains records from approximately **100 farmed Atlantic salmon sites** and **over 365,000 wild fish observations** across 7 DFO sub-zones (3.1–3.7), collected between 2001 and 2023. This is the largest publicly available long-term sea lice dataset for any jurisdiction.

**Spatial and temporal variation**
Sea lice levels show substantial variation across zones and years. No single narrative of "increasing" or "decreasing" lice fits the entire dataset. Spatial variation is pronounced — some zones show consistently higher farm lice burdens than others.

**2015 anomaly**
An unusual spike in lice levels occurred across most monitoring zones in 2015, attributed to unusually warm sea surface temperatures associated with the Pacific "warm blob" — an El Niño-related oceanographic event. This demonstrates the sensitivity of lice dynamics to environmental conditions.

**Discovery Islands closure (2021)**
Farm licenses in DFO sub-zone 3.2 (Discovery Islands) were revoked in 2021 and farms closed. The dataset captures the period before and after closure, providing a quasi-experimental basis for assessing the impact of removing farm lice sources on wild fish infestation levels. The paper presents the data but does not conduct this analysis.

## Uncertainty and Limitations

- Sampling protocols, species identification standards, and temporal coverage differ across the contributing programmes, limiting direct year-on-year and zone-to-zone comparisons.
- The paper explicitly does not model or analyse causal relationships — it provides infrastructure for such analyses.
- One author (Lance Stewardson) is affiliated with a private aquaculture consultancy. No formal COI declaration was found in the reviewed text; funding sources should be verified in the published version.
- The dataset covers BC specifically — different species composition, regulatory framework, and oceanography from Norway.
