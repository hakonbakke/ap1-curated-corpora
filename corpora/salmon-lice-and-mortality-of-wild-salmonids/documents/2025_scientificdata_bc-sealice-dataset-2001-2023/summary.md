## What this paper does

Crawford et al. (2025) present a comprehensive dataset that harmonizes two decades of sea lice monitoring data from British Columbia (BC), Canada, focusing on both wild and farmed salmon populations. The dataset integrates information from various stakeholders, including government agencies, industry, First Nations, non-governmental organizations (NGOs), and academic research programs, which previously operated using incompatible data formats. This harmonization aims to facilitate coast-wide spatial and temporal analyses of sea lice infestations, thereby reducing reliance on smaller or anecdotal datasets that have often influenced aquaculture policy debates in BC.

The dataset spans from 2001 to 2023 and includes a total of 16,920 wild sampling events and 376,764 individual fish assessments. It covers seven fish health sub-zones along the BC coast, with a particular focus on juvenile *Oncorhynchus* species, primarily *O. gorbuscha* (pink salmon) and *O. keta* (chum salmon), which together account for approximately 93% of the wild fish sampled. The dataset is crucial for understanding the dynamics of sea lice infestations and their potential impacts on both wild and farmed salmon populations, especially in light of recent policy changes that have led to a reduction in the number of salmon farms in certain areas.

## Methods and data

The dataset comprises five linked CSV files available on figshare, detailing both wild and farmed salmon lice monitoring data. The wild sampling data includes records from various programs, employing either lethal or non-lethal sampling protocols. The lethal protocols typically involve laboratory identification of sea lice species and stages, while non-lethal methods provide limited identification due to field constraints. The dataset captures a wide range of data, including the number of lice per fish, fish length, weight, and the specific sampling site coordinates.

Key metrics include:
- **Wild events:** 16,920 sampling events
- **Wild fish assessed:** 376,764 individual records
- **Farms:** 96 facilities monitored
- **Farm monthly records:** 10,159 abundance rows

The dataset also documents the median sea lice loads across different fish health zones, particularly during the sensitive out-migration period for juvenile salmon (March–June). The authors emphasize the importance of maintaining data integrity while allowing for maximal temporal and spatial comparisons across the various monitoring programs.

## Key findings

- **Infestation Rates:** Approximately 28% of assessed wild fish were found to have any sea lice, with about 1% exhibiting more than 10 lice.
- **Species Composition:** The majority of wild hosts were juvenile chum and pink salmon, with sticklebacks representing around 3% of the samples.
- **Temporal Trends:** The dataset highlights a significant peak in farmed *Lepeophtheirus salmonis* motile abundances in 2015, attributed to the "warm blob" phenomenon, with subsequent declines observed post-2021 following government farm reductions.
- **Sampling Protocols:** The dataset reveals heterogeneity in sampling protocols, with ten different sources contributing to wild sampling, leading to variations in data quality and species resolution over time.

## Limitations and caveats

The authors acknowledge several limitations in the dataset:
- **No mortality or population outcomes:** The dataset does not provide direct evidence of mortality or population-level impacts due to sea lice infestations.
- **Standardization Issues:** Wild sampling was not standardized across different zones and years, leading to potential biases in the data.
- **Farm Data Limitations:** Farm inventories are not disclosed at the site level, limiting the ability to conduct detailed analyses of individual farm impacts.
- **Species Identification:** Early years of data collection suffered from weak species resolution, particularly for early developmental stages of sea lice.

---

## What this paper does not claim

The authors explicitly state that the dataset does not establish causal relationships between sea lice infestations and population dynamics in wild salmon. It also does not provide a comprehensive analysis of the ecological impacts of sea lice on salmon populations, nor does it attempt to model transmission dynamics between farmed and wild salmon. The dataset serves primarily as a foundational resource for future research and policy discussions rather than a definitive source of causal inference.
