# Summary: Lamberg & Imsland (2022)

**Full title:** Using Merged Pre-Fishery Abundance as a Parameter Evaluating the Status of Atlantic Salmon and Anadromous Brown Trout Populations: A Norwegian Case Study
**Journal:** Fishes (MDPI), 7(5): Article 264
**DOI:** 10.3390/fishes7050264
**Document type:** Methods paper / monitoring framework proposal
**Priority questions:** Q9, Q10

---

## What this paper does

Lamberg and Imsland (2022) tackle a practical problem in Norwegian salmonid management: how do we know if conservation measures — including aquaculture lice regulations under the Traffic Light System (TLS) — are actually working at the population level? Current management relies almost exclusively on Atlantic salmon population metrics (pre-fishery abundance, PFA; spawning targets), but these single-species metrics miss anadromous brown trout (sea trout), which may be equally or more sensitive to salmon lice from farms.

The paper proposes merging salmon and sea trout PFA data into a single regional indicator, standardised by hydrogeographic factors (river length, area, mean discharge, precipitation field) and mean smolt age, to enable ecologically fair comparisons across Norwegian regions. In 2019, the authors collected population data from 27 rivers in 8 coastal river regions stretching from Bjerkreimselva (58.6°N, southwest Norway) to Målselva (69.0°N, northern Norway), using a combination of underwater video surveillance in fish ladders, snorkelling surveys, and catch statistics.

The merged PFA is then compared to the conventional single-species PFA across regions to assess whether including sea trout meaningfully changes the picture of population status. The broader argument is that if sea trout serve as a "lice proxy" — i.e., their populations are more responsive to fjord-level lice intensity than salmon because of their prolonged coastal residence — monitoring their joint PFA with salmon could help detect management impacts earlier and more reliably.

---

## Main findings

- **Merged vs single-species PFA:** In most of the 8 study regions, the standardised merged PFA (salmon + sea trout) differed substantially from the conventional salmon-only PFA. The difference was primarily driven by variation in sea trout population sizes across regions.

- **Inter-regional variation:** The highest standardised merged PFA was approximately **four times higher** than the lowest across the 8 regions, suggesting large regional differences in salmonid production that are masked by salmon-only assessments.

- **Sea trout population status is poorly characterised:** Despite monitoring methods being available and widely used in Norway for 20+ years, no national management model based on sea trout population data has been established — in contrast to the Atlantic salmon spawning target model.

- **River straying:** Surveillance data show that both Atlantic salmon and particularly sea trout regularly stray between rivers, undermining the assumption (built into the spawning target model) that each river hosts a genetically and demographically independent population.

- **Sea trout as lice proxy:** The authors argue that if lice conditions in a fjord improve, the sea trout population in that fjord should grow — making sea trout trends a potential leading indicator of management effectiveness.

## Limitations

- Based on a single year of data (2019) — no trend analysis, no before-after comparison.
- The 8 regions were selected partly based on data availability, not random sampling; regional comparisons may be biased.
- Interspecific competition between salmon and sea trout sharing river habitat is acknowledged as important but not quantified or modelled.
- The standardisation approach (smolt age × hydrogeography) involves assumptions about comparative productivity that are not independently validated.
- The paper proposes merged PFA as a management tool but does not test it against any observed population responses to lice management — it remains a methodological proposal.

## Place in the literature

This paper occupies an important but indirect position in the lice-mortality evidence chain. It does not estimate lice-induced mortality itself, but instead addresses a foundational gap: how do we measure whether lice management is achieving its goals at the population level? The single-species (salmon-only) approach embedded in the current TLS framework is, the authors argue, blind to half the impact — the sea trout population, which may be equally or more affected by fjord-level lice loads and which serves as a more sensitive bioindicator of lice conditions.

The paper complements Hawley et al. (2024), which shows that individual-level migration heterogeneity makes it hard to estimate lice exposure for sea trout; Lamberg & Imsland (2022) argue that we need better tools to measure the aggregate population outcome, even if the individual exposure pathway is complex. Together, these papers make the case that sea trout must be brought into both the risk assessment and the monitoring frameworks for Norwegian salmon lice management.
