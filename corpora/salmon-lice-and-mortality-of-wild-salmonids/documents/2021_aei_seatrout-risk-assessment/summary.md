## What this paper does

Finstad et al. (2021) address the impact of salmon lice (*Lepeophtheirus salmonis*) on wild sea trout (*Salmo trutta*) populations in Norwegian coastal areas, particularly in the context of aquaculture. The authors propose a novel risk assessment method that utilizes **Reduced Marine Living Area (RML)** and **Reduced Marine Feeding Time (RMT)** as sustainability indicators for first-time migrant sea trout. This study is significant as it highlights the vulnerability of sea trout, which spend a considerable portion of their marine phase in coastal waters where salmon lice are prevalent, yet have received less attention compared to wild Atlantic salmon (*Salmo salar*).

The research was conducted in two Norwegian aquaculture production areas: **Hardangerfjord (PO3)** and **Romsdalsfjord (PO5)**, focusing on four rivers: **Oselvo** and **Etne** in PO3, and **Eira** and **Rauma** in PO5. The study spans the period from 2012 to 2017, utilizing a bio-hydrodynamic lice model with a resolution of **160 m** to assess the spatial and temporal impacts of salmon lice on sea trout populations. The findings underscore the need for a holistic approach to environmental management that considers the interactions between aquaculture and wild fish species.

## Methods and data

The authors employed a bio-hydrodynamic model to simulate the dispersion of salmon lice and assess their impact on sea trout. The model was set with the following parameters: a **60 g** smolt size, a **70-day** marine residence period, a migration distance of **20 km** from the river mouth, and a maximum depth of **2 m**. Migration scenarios were evaluated for three dates: **April 24**, **May 15**, and **June 5**.

Impact thresholds were derived from the Taranger index (2015), which indicates that a fish experiences **100% impact** when infested with more than **30 lice**, **50% impact** at **10–30 lice**, and **0% impact** at **≤10 lice**. The authors calculated RML and RMT using the following equations:

- RML = (red area + 0.5 × yellow area) / total area
- RMT = days until RML > 30% − 70 d

The model outputs were validated against sentinel cage data collected from Hardangerfjord between 2012 and 2017.

## Key findings

### RML and RMT Results

#### PO3 (Hardanger / Bjørnafjord)

- **RML (%) by Migration Timing:**
  - Early (24 Apr): **32%**
  - Intermediate (15 May): **49%**
  - Late (5 Jun): **66%**

- **RML and RMT for Focal Rivers:**
  | River   | Early RML | Intermediate RML | Late RML | RMT (late) |
  |---------|-----------|-------------------|----------|------------|
  | Oselvo  | 94%       | 97%               | 98%      | −56 d      |
  | Etne    | 0%        | 17%               | 68%      | −16 d      |

#### PO5 (Romsdal)

- **RML (%) by Migration Timing:**
  - Early: **34%**
  - Intermediate: **45%**
  - Late: **59%**

- **RML and RMT for Focal Rivers:**
  | River   | Early RML | Intermediate RML | Late RML | RMT range   |
  |---------|-----------|-------------------|----------|-------------|
  | Eira    | 83%       | 87%               | 85%      | −19 to −50 d |
  | Rauma   | 40%       | 43%               | 48%      | −6 to −40 d  |

The study found that late-migrating sea trout were significantly more affected by lice, with reductions in marine time observed as high as **71%** for late migrants in the Eira River.

## Limitations and caveats

The authors acknowledge several limitations in their study:
- The model serves as a habitat-loss proxy rather than directly measuring mortality.
- The lice thresholds used were derived from laboratory studies, which may not fully represent field conditions.
- The fixed smolt size of **60 g** does not account for variations in size at migration across different populations.
- The **160 m** grid resolution used in the model is less validated compared to the **800 m** standard.

## What this paper does not claim

The authors do not claim that their model directly measures salmon lice-induced mortality in sea trout. Instead, they focus on RML and RMT as indicators of habitat exclusion and feeding time reduction. The study does not address the potential for sea trout to adapt behaviorally to salmon lice exposure, nor does it quantify the long-term population impacts of reduced marine living area.

## Relevance to this corpus

This paper contributes to understanding the ecological impacts of salmon lice on wild fish populations, particularly in relation to aquaculture practices. It addresses questions related to habitat exclusion (Q1), the effects of lice on fish behavior and ecology (Q2, Q3), and the implications for management strategies (Q5, Q8). The findings can be linked to ongoing discussions about the sustainability of aquaculture and its effects on wild salmonid populations.
