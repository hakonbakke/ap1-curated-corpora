# Summary: Fjelldal et al. (2020)

**Full title:** Effects of laboratory salmon louse infection on osmoregulation, growth and survival in Atlantic salmon
**Journal:** Conservation Physiology, Volume 8, 2020
**DOI:** 10.1093/conphys/coaa023
**Document type:** Research article (laboratory challenge experiment)
**Priority questions:** Q6, Q7

---

## What this paper does

This study addresses a fundamental mechanistic question in the salmon lice literature: at what infection intensity does *Lepeophtheirus salmonis* cause measurable physiological damage and mortality in Atlantic salmon post-smolts, and what are the specific pathways through which lice kill fish?

The experimental animals were F1 wild-origin Atlantic salmon post-smolts (~40 g), derived from eggs stripped from River Etne broodstock (Hordaland, Western Norway) and raised at IMR's Matre Aquaculture Research Station. In February 2018, n=330 PIT-tagged fish were distributed into six tanks (n=55/tank). Three tanks were infected with *L. salmonis* copepodids (1430 copepodids/tank, targeting ~26 lice/fish as an infection pressure), while three tanks served as uninfected controls. The experiment was terminated at 28 days post-infection, when mobile pre-adult stages had developed. At termination, all fish were weighed, measured, and counted for lice; subsamples of 15 fish/tank (45/group) were blood-sampled for plasma analysis of Na⁺, Cl⁻, osmolality, and cortisol.

Infection intensity was expressed as lice per gram body weight (mobile lice/g fish), enabling size-independent dose-response analysis. Statistical analyses included correlations between infection intensity and physiological endpoints across individual fish, with threshold values estimated from regression breakpoints.

---

## Main findings

### Infection intensity achieved

At 28 days post-infection, infected fish had a mean infection intensity of **0.38 mobile lice g⁻¹ fish weight** (range 0.07–0.9 lice g⁻¹). In total, 69% of the copepodids added were estimated to have attached; 29 infected fish died during the experiment (no deaths in controls).

### Growth impairment

- Infected fish: SGR = **0.4% day⁻¹**
- Control fish: SGR = **1.0% day⁻¹**
- SGR correlated negatively with plasma Na⁺, Cl⁻, osmolality and cortisol in infected fish — fish with more severe osmoregulatory disruption grew slower

### Osmoregulatory failure

Infected fish showed significantly elevated plasma Na⁺, Cl⁻ and osmolality relative to controls, indicating failure to maintain ionic homeostasis in seawater. The mechanism: salmon lice damage the skin and mucus barrier, increasing passive ion influx from seawater, compounded by cortisol-driven increases in epithelial permeability.

Estimated threshold infection intensities for osmoregulatory response:
- **0.18 lice g⁻¹** for elevated plasma Cl⁻
- **0.22 lice g⁻¹** for elevated plasma Na⁺

### Moribund fish

Ten fish were moribund at termination, all in infected tanks. Moribund fish occurred at infection intensities **above 0.2 lice g⁻¹** and showed extreme plasma Cl⁻, Na⁺, osmolality and cortisol values. The cortisol–plasma ion correlation in infected fish suggests a positive feedback loop: stress → elevated cortisol → increased membrane permeability → worsening ionic imbalance → further stress.

### Comparison with prior literature

Prior studies found effects at higher infection intensities: Grimnes and Jakobsen (1996) observed an exponential mortality increase at ~1.6 lice g⁻¹ in 40 g salmon; Fjelldal et al. (2019) found effects above 0.3 lice g⁻¹ in Arctic char. The present study identifies earlier sub-lethal physiological signatures at 0.18–0.22 lice g⁻¹ in Atlantic salmon, establishing that damage occurs before clinical mortality becomes apparent.

## Limitations

- Laboratory conditions cannot replicate field stressors (predation, food competition, fluctuating temperature and salinity)
- Outbred farmed lice strain used — virulence may differ from wild-adapted lice populations
- Single nominal infection dose rather than a multi-level titration, limiting confidence in threshold estimates
- Only mobile lice quantified at termination; lice stages not separated
- F1 fish reared under artificial photoperiod and temperature — immune history and baseline physiology may differ from field post-smolts
- Small fish (~40 g); wild smolts range 10–80 g; smaller fish may have higher lice-per-gram burden from the same absolute lice number
- 28-day observation window may miss delayed pathological effects

## Place in the literature

Fjelldal et al. (2020) fills a specific and important gap in the mechanistic chain from salmon lice infestation to wild salmonid mortality. While field studies like Bøhn et al. (2020) demonstrate that lice can cause catastrophic population-level mortality, and monitoring studies like Helland et al. (2015) characterise the challenges of detecting lice levels in wild fish, this paper establishes the physiological mechanism and quantified intensity thresholds at the individual level.

The threshold values (0.18–0.22 lice g⁻¹ for physiological impairment; >0.2 lice g⁻¹ for moribund onset) are directly relevant to the Norwegian salmon lice risk index, which parameterises population-level mortality risk from registered lice infection intensities. The paper explicitly situates its findings in the context of TLS calibration, making it a key reference for Q7 (translating infestation intensity to mortality estimates) and for evaluating whether current TLS thresholds are physiologically grounded.
