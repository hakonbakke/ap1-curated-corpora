## What this paper does

Fjelldal et al. (2020) investigate the physiological impacts of salmon louse (*Lepeophtheirus salmonis*) infection on post-smolt *Salmo salar* in a controlled laboratory setting. The study aims to establish infection intensity thresholds that correlate with osmoregulatory failure, growth impairment, and increased mortality, which are critical for understanding the ecological consequences of lice infestations on wild salmon populations. Conducted at the Institute of Marine Research (IMR) in Matre, Norway, the experiment involved infecting approximately 40 g post-smolts with copepodids at a temperature of 9°C and salinity of 34 ppt.

The significance of this research lies in its potential to inform management practices and conservation strategies for wild *S. salar* populations, particularly in the context of increasing lice infestations due to intensive aquaculture. By establishing specific thresholds for physiological responses, the study contributes to the development of monitoring frameworks that can be used to assess the health of salmon populations in the wild.

## Methods and data

The experimental design involved 330 first-generation wild post-smolts, which were tagged and distributed across six tanks. Three tanks were infected with a total of 4,290 copepodids, resulting in an average infection intensity of approximately 26 lice per fish. The study lasted for 28 days, during which various physiological parameters were measured, including growth rates, mortality, plasma osmolality, sodium (Na⁺), chloride (Cl⁻), and cortisol levels.

Key definitions include:
- **Infection intensity (II)**: Calculated as the number of lice per gram of fish weight.
- **Specific Growth Rate (SGR)**: Measured as a percentage per day.
- **Condition Factor (CF)**: A measure of fish health based on weight and length.

The study identified thresholds for significant physiological changes: 0.18 lice g⁻¹ for plasma Cl⁻ and 0.22 lice g⁻¹ for plasma Na⁺, with moribund fish observed at infection intensities greater than 0.2 lice g⁻¹.

## Key findings

- **Infection Intensity**: After 28 days, the average infection intensity was 0.38 mobile lice g⁻¹, with a range of 0.07 to 0.9 lice g⁻¹.
- **Growth Rates**: Infected fish exhibited a significantly lower SGR of 0.42% day⁻¹ compared to 0.95% day⁻¹ in controls.
- **Mortality Rates**: Total mortality in the infected group was approximately 30% (39 fish), compared to 13% (21 fish) in controls, with some control mortality attributed to Vibrio infections.
- **Physiological Changes**: Infected fish had elevated plasma Na⁺ (190.6 vs. 169.3 mmol L⁻¹), Cl⁻ (167.6 vs. 140.3 mmol L⁻¹), osmolality (380.8 vs. 347.4 mOsm kg⁻¹), and cortisol (193.8 vs. 86.4 ng ml⁻¹) compared to controls.
- **Correlations**: Significant negative correlations were found between infection intensity and SGR (R²=0.36), and positive correlations between infection intensity and plasma Na⁺, Cl⁻, osmolality, and cortisol levels.

## Limitations and caveats

The study was conducted under laboratory conditions, which may not fully replicate the complexities of natural environments. The use of an outbred strain of lice and a single dose of copepodids may limit the generalizability of the findings. Additionally, the study focused solely on mobile lice stages, potentially overlooking the effects of other life stages on host physiology.

## What this paper does not claim

The authors do not claim that the thresholds established in this study are universally applicable to all wild *S. salar* populations or that they account for all potential environmental variables influencing lice impacts. The study does not address the long-term ecological consequences of lice infestations beyond the immediate physiological responses observed.

## Relevance to this corpus

This research is relevant to questions Q6 and Q7 regarding the physiological impacts of salmon lice on wild salmon populations and the implications for aquaculture management. It supports the Norwegian lice risk index and provides empirical data that can inform future monitoring and conservation efforts.

## Related evidence in corpus

This study builds on previous findings regarding the physiological effects of salmon lice on various salmonid species, including earlier work on *Salmo trutta* and *Salvelinus alpinus*. It also aligns with the ongoing discourse on the ecological impacts of aquaculture on wild salmon populations, particularly in relation to lice infestations.
