## What this paper does

Forseth et al. (2017) provide a comprehensive assessment of the major anthropogenic threats to *Salmo salar* populations in Norway, utilizing a semi-quantitative effect × development matrix to rank 15 different impact factors affecting over 400 rivers. This assessment is crucial for prioritizing management strategies aimed at conserving wild Atlantic salmon, which face numerous challenges due to both natural and human-induced factors. The study highlights the significant role of aquaculture, particularly the impacts of escaped farmed salmon and salmon lice, as expanding threats to the viability of wild populations.

The research is situated within the context of Norway's unique ecological landscape, which supports a substantial proportion of the world's wild Atlantic salmon populations. The findings are particularly relevant given the ongoing decline in salmon numbers observed over the past few decades, necessitating urgent and informed conservation efforts. By establishing a framework for evaluating these threats, the authors aim to inform policy decisions and regulatory measures that can mitigate the impacts on salmon populations.

## Methods and data

The authors developed a semi-quantitative classification system that ranks anthropogenic impacts based on two axes: the effect of each factor on salmon populations and the likelihood of future development of these impacts. The study draws on data from various sources, including scientific literature, governmental reports, and expert judgments from members of the Norwegian Scientific Advisory Committee for Atlantic Salmon Management.

The classification system categorizes threats into four groups: expanding population threats, stabilized population threats, expanding loss factors, and stabilized loss factors. The authors defined critical thresholds for population viability, identifying populations as critically endangered or lost when adult returns are reduced by more than 75% over one generation or when there is significant genetic introgression from farmed salmon.

## Key findings

### Top-ranked threats
- **Escaped farmed salmon**: 
  - Composite score: ~0.83 (effect), ~0.80 (development)
  - Affects 51 out of 109 populations, posing a significant threat to genetic integrity.
  
- **Salmon lice (Lepeophtheirus salmonis)**:
  - Composite score: ~0.70 (effect), ~0.58 (development)
  - Associated with reductions in adult returns of over 25% in affected populations.
  
### Aquaculture scale (2015)
- Approximately **382 million** farmed salmon compared to **522,000** wild spawners, resulting in a ratio of **~732:1**.
- Under worst-case scenarios, potential release of **>1 billion** lice larvae per day.
- Experimental estimates indicate **0.6–39%** additional marine mortality in smolts due to lice.

### Stabilized threats
- **Gyrodactylus salaris**, acidification, and hydropower regulation were identified as stabilized threats, with mitigation measures in place.
- Overexploitation was downgraded as a major threat due to effective management strategies.

## Limitations and caveats

The study relies on expert scoring rather than a meta-analysis, which may introduce bias. It is specific to Norway and does not account for developments or changes in the status of salmon populations post-2017. Additionally, the classification system is semi-quantitative and may not capture the full complexity of interactions among multiple threats.

## What this paper does not claim

The authors do not claim to provide a comprehensive overview of all potential threats to Atlantic salmon globally or to predict future population dynamics with certainty. The focus is specifically on anthropogenic impacts within Norway, and the findings should not be generalized without further context.

## Relevance to this corpus

This paper addresses priority questions Q9 and Q10 regarding the impacts of anthropogenic factors on salmon populations, contributing to the broader discourse on conservation strategies. It serves as a foundational document for understanding the regulatory framework surrounding salmon lice management and the implications of aquaculture practices.

## Related evidence in corpus
This study complements other documents that discuss the ecological impacts of aquaculture on wild salmon populations and the management of salmon lice, providing a critical synthesis of current knowledge and regulatory approaches in Norway.
