Annual mean total length (LT) of wild one-sea-winter (1SW) Atlantic salmon Salmo salar of the Norwegian River Imsa decreased from 63 to 54cm with a corresponding decrease in condition factor (K)

for cohorts migrating to sea from 1976 to 2010. The reduction in LT is associated with a 40% decline in mean individual mass, from 2 to 1вЛЕ2kg. Hatchery fish reared from parental fish of the same pop-

ulation exhibited similar changes from 1981 onwards. The decrease in LT correlated negatively with near-surface temperatures in the eastern Norwegian Sea, thought to be the main feeding area of the present stock. Furthermore, S. salar exhibited significant variations in the proportion of cohorts attaining maturity after only one winter in the ocean. The proportion of S. salar spawning as 1SW fish was lower both in the 1970s and after 2000 than in the 1980s and 1990s associated with a gradual decline in post-smolt growth and smaller amounts of reserve energy in the fish. In wild S. salar, there was a positive association between post-smolt growth and the sea survival back to the River Imsa for spawning. In addition, among smolt year-classes, there were significant positive correlations between wild and hatchery S. salar in LT, K and age at maturity. The present changes may be caused by ecosystem changes following the collapse and rebuilding of the pelagic fish abundance in the North Atlantic Ocean, a gradual decrease in zooplankton abundance and climate change with increasing surface temperature in the Norwegian Sea. Thus, the observed variation in the life-history traits of S. salar appears primarily associated with major changes in the pelagic food web in the ocean.

## INTRODUCTION

Climate is a governing variable of the biosphere, and the ongoing climate change has serious effects on the EarthвАЩs ecosystems. With nearly 1вИШ C rise in global warming experienced during the past century, significant shifts in distribution, phenology, behaviour and life history of organisms are already evident (Parmesan & Yohe, 2003). With an expected increase up to 7вИШ C by 2100, the EarthвАЩs biota will be even more seriously affected (Sheridan & Bickford, 2011).

Increasing temperature accelerates biochemical and physiological processes of poikilothermic organisms and affects their body size (Clarke, 2003; P√ґrtner & Farrell, 2008). The вАШtemperatureвАУsize ruleвАЩ states that there is a tendency for poikilotherms to grow faster, but reach adulthood earlier, at a smaller body size in a warmer climate

(Atkinson, 1994; Zuo et al., 2012). Consistent with this rule, several recent studies have demonstrated smaller sizes of poikilotherms associated with climate warming (Daufresne et al., 2009; Ohlberger et al., 2011; Sheridan & Bickford, 2011). This contrasts with the effect when developmental rate is stimulated by increased food quality, where organisms will mature larger, not smaller, for their age (Berrigan & Charnov, 1994). This differing effect between higher temperature and better food quality on body size, called вАШthe Berrigan & CharnovвАЩs life-history puzzleвАЩ, has received much attention in ecological literature (Perrin, 1995; Jonsson et al., 2013; Trip et al., 2014).

Growth rates of fishes fed to satiation increase with temperature up to a maximum point denoted вАШthe optimal temperature for growthвАЩ, after which growth rate decreases gradually with a further temperature increase (Brett, 1956; Elliott, 1994). Fishes living in temperate waters often encounter habitats colder than their optimal temperature for growth (Forseth et al., 2009). For instance, in the North Atlantic Ocean, post-smolts of Atlantic salmon Salmo salar L. 1758 typically exploit habitats that are colder than their optimal temperature (Gudjonsson et al., 2015; Minke-Martin et al., 2015), which for post-smolts is about 14вИШ C according to the study by Handeland et al. (2008). Thus, growth typically increases with increasing ambient temperature given that the fish find enough high-quality food. If resource availability is poor or temperature unusually high, however, increased temperature can give lower growth because maintenance costs increase with temperature. Furthermore, above the optimum temperatures, oxygen content in the water becomes a limiting factor for growth (P√ґrtner, 2010). For instance, S. salar exhibit negative growth at temperatures above 25вИШ C because of this (Jonsson et al., 2001).

Fast-growing individuals typically mature younger than conspecifics that grow more slowly (Alm, 1959), except when growth is exceptionally high. In the latter case, maturity can be delayed (Jonsson et al., 1984, 1999). Early maturation is stimulated if the somatic energy density is high, or impeded if the amount of reserved energy is critically low (Mangel & Satterthwaite, 2008). In addition, the effect of increased temperature varies depending on the energy assimilation of the fish. If the energy assimilation is high, higher temperature increases, not decreases size at maturity. This synergistic effect of high temperature and food quality was demonstrated in experiments with S. salar (Jonsson et al., 2012, 2013). Furthermore, increased growth rate before the onsetofmaturation,whetherthisisowingtoenhancedlipidcontent infoodorincreased water temperature, will decrease age and therefore potentially reduce size at maturity. Thus, within populations of poikilotherms, year-to-year variation in age at maturity can be associated with both ambient temperature and food availability.

The study population, S. salar of the Norwegian River Imsa, typically migrate to the sea between 12 and 30cm total length (LT), 1вАУ3years of age (Jonsson, N. & Jonsson, B., 2014). They are called smolts at the time of seaward migration and post-smolts during the first summer and autumn at sea. Most smolts enter seawater during spring and early summer to pursue oceanic feeding. Juveniles entering seawater at other times of the year survive poorly (Hansen & Jonsson, 1989), and when returning to fresh water for spawning, their homing precision is low (Hansen & Jonsson, 1991). The present fish spend 1вАУ2 (seldom 3) years in the ocean until they attain sexual maturity (Jonsson et al., 1991, 2003). When spawning after one winter in the ocean, they are called one-sea-winter (1SW) fish, and after two or more winters, they are called multi-sea-winter (MSW) fish. They return for spawning during freshets between August and October (Jonsson et al., 2007). The majority ascend the river

during a period of 4вАУ6weeks. In a warmer climate, the proportion of 1SW S. salar is expected to increase because of better growth, or decrease if feeding opportunities are sufficiently good (Jonsson et al., 2013).

In this study, life-history data on S. salar of the River Imsa, monitored from 1976 onwards, were assessed. Age at maturity is given as a proportion of the cohorts that attained maturity as 1SW fish. The mass and length relationship, post-smolt growth and proportion of the seaward-migrating fish that return to the home river for spawning were estimated. Based on this, regressions between life-history variables and surface temperature in the eastern Norwegian Sea were tested. Sea surface temperature during the first period in the ocean is important for growth and survival of S. salar (Friedland et al., 2009, 2014). Furthermore, each year from 1981 onwards, groups of S. salar were reared to smolts in a hatchery, before being released in the river. Adults returning to the River Imsa were used as broodstock. The groups were released to test if environmental conditions influenced wild and hatchery fish similarly.

Based on life-history theory (Roff, 1992), it may be expected that fish length at maturity, mass and length relationship and survival at sea would increase with near-surface temperature during the first year the fish spent at sea. If so, a higher proportion of the cohorts would attain maturity as 1SW fish given that feeding is sufficient. Here, this was tested on a 35year time series of S. salar spawning in the River Imsa and discussed in relation to life-history theory.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS

The River Imsa, south-western Norway empties into the Boknafjorden near the city of Stavanger (Fig. 1). The river, which is 1km long, has a restricted spawning area and is characterized by a temperature ranging from 2 to 3вИШ C in winter and summer maxima of c. 20вИШ C. The water discharge ranges from <2 to 3m3 sвИТ1 in summer to a maximum in late autumn (>10m3 sвИТ1) (Jonsson et al., 1989).

A Wolf trap (Wolf, 1951), apertures 10mm, inclination 1:10, situated 150m above the river

outlet, catches all descending S. salar longer than c. 10cm LT. All ascending fish were captured in a fixed box trap located alongside the Wolf trap on top of a three-step fish ladder (Jonsson &

Jonsson, 2011). The traps were emptied twice every day during the study period.

The River Imsa hatchery uses river water at slightly elevated temperature that ranges from 2 to 8вИШ C in winter to c. 20вИШ C in summer.

The wild fish originated naturally from adults spawning in the River Imsa during November to December after having completed the entire life cycle in the wild. On average, the wild fish smolted at 1 (14%), 2 (78%) or вЙ•3 (8%) years of age and migrated into the ocean where most of their growth occurred (Jonsson et al., 1998a; Jonsson & Jonsson, 2003). From 1976 to 2010, 36201 wild smolts migrated from the river to the sea, and tagging studies suggest that they fed in the north Norwegian Sea (north of 62вИШ N) (Hansen et al., 1993; Jonsson et al., 1993). Of these, 2вЛЕ9% returned to the trap in the river as adults.

The hatchery fish were reared to smolts from adults captured in the upstream trap of the River Imsa. Usually, >10 individuals of each sex were used as parental fish. In early May, the smolts were released at the time when the majority of the wild smolts migrated to the sea (Jonsson, N. & Jonsson, B., 2014). The release site was located c. 100m above the river outlet, just beneath the fish traps in the River Imsa (Jonsson & Jonsson, 2011). Thus, the hatchery fish completed the remainder of their growth to adulthood naturally. During 1981вАУ2010, 182740 (66вЛЕ2% of 1 year-old and 33вЛЕ2% of 2 year-old) hatchery-reared smolts were released. Of these, 1вЛЕ5% returned to the trap as adults. The river is small with a restricted spawning area, and the hatchery fish derived from tagged adult fish returning to the River Imsa.

The majority of the fish attained maturity after one winter and two summers in the ocean (Jonsson et al., 2003), and MSW fish were excluded from the analyses because they were too few for their findings to be representative except for the last few years. In the tests, mean values of the cohorts were used. When appropriate, similar relationships of individual fish within the cohorts were also tested.

The proportion of the tagged S. salar within cohorts that returned to the River Imsa was used as an index of annual sea survival. This was estimated as the number of returning adults divided by the number of smolts forming the cohort. The figures were not adjusted for any possible mortality effect of tags or tagging, because there was no intention of estimating the absolute survival rate of untagged fish.

Near-surface temperatures (0вАУ10m depth) were retrieved from a several decadeвАЩs long realization of a hydro-dynamical model resolving the entire Nordic SEAS (Lien et al., 2014). A transect consisting of 17 stations (ranging from 62вИШ 4вА≤ N; 5вИШ 2вА≤ E off the Norwegian west coast continuing offshore to 64вИШ 7вА≤ N; 0вИШ E), in the feeding area of the River Imsa S. salar, was applied to represent temperature means in the Norwegian Sea (Fig. 1). This transect was monitored three to four times per year by the Norwegian Institute of Marine Research, and the observed temperatures were used to ensure that the model results were unbiased and realistically resolving interannual variability. Mean water temperature was estimated for two periods, summer (April to September) and winter (October to March). April to September is the main growing period of the post-smolts, whereas growth is low or may even temporarily stop from October through March (Peyronnet et al., 2007). In both seasons, sea surface temperature increased significantly during the study period (Fig. 2). Correlations between life-history variables and temperature during the 3month periods were also tested, but these gave no extra information and were therefore omitted from the results.

The data were analysed using SPSS 22 (www-01.ibm.com/support/docview.wss?uid= swg21646821). Simple linear regression analysis was generally used. To ease the interpretation, proportional data were not arc-sine square-root transformed (Warton & Hui, 2011). The linear regressions were tested for deviation from normality using the KolmogorovвАУSmirnov test. Differences in slopes of the linear regression lines between hatchery-produced and wild S. salar were tested using ANCOVA (Snedecor & Cochran, 1973). The data series were differenced by 1year when comparing life-history variables of hatchery and wild cohorts because of their monotonic trends. For the relationship between proportion of 1SW fish and year of seaward migration, the four-parameter Weibull equation (Wahed et al., 2009) was used, estimated by Python programming (www.Python.org).

## DISCUSSION

The time series show that LT, K and post-smolt growth of S. salar have decreased since the 1970s. Similar trends are evident in both wild fish and hatchery fish released as smolts in the river. The patterns are consistent, and the decrease in size appears associated with reduced post-smolt growth and not with smolt size or during the second summer at sea. The reduced growth opportunity may be associated with lower primary and secondary production in the pelagic food web as argued by Beaugrand & Reid (2012). During the first year at sea, S. salar feed largely on pelagic crustaceans (Amphipoda and Euphausiacea) (Jacobsen & Hansen, 2001; Haugland et al., 2006). The abundance of these organisms has decreased concurrently with an increased abundance of competing pelagic fishes (Huse et al., 2012; Utne et al., 2012), among which the Norwegian spring-spawning herring (NSSH) Clupea harengus L. 1758 may be the keenest post-smolt competitor. This hypothesis is supported by negative correlations between C. harengus abundance in the Norwegian Sea and S. salar survival (Mills, 2003). M√§ntyniemi et al. (2012) reported a similar negative relationship between C. harengus recruitment and S. salar post-smolt survival in the Baltic Sea. After the collapse of the NSSH in 1970, post-smolt growth was particularly good in the 1970s. But as the NSSH population recovered gradually (Huse et al., 2012; N√Єttestad et al., 2015), post-smolt growth has decreased as it has in other European rivers (McCarthy et al., 2008; Peyronnet et al., 2008; Friedland et al., 2009).

An additional possible reason for the decrease in growth and K with time could be the recent increase in abundance of sea lice Lepeophtheirus salmonis, which can affect post-smolt growth and survival negatively (Stien et al., 2005; Gargan et al., 2012; Skilbrei et al., 2013). The reported effect of the L. salmonis on growth by these authors, however, was much less than the 40% decrease in mass of 1SW fish. Furthermore, there was no parallel increase in mortality in this study. Thus, L. salmonis appears not be a major contributor to the observed decrease in size of S. salar.

Todd et al. (2008) reported a similar decrease in K of 1SW S. salar from Scotland towards the end of the 20th and the beginning of the 21st centuries. They related reduced K and lipid density of the fish to increased sea surface temperature, associated with a recent climate-driven change in the eastern North Atlantic pelagic ecosystem. Furthermore, they found parallels with this decrease in effects on other top consumers, such as Atlantic bluefin tuna Thunnus thynnus (L. 1758) and seabirds (Todd et al., 2008). The decreased K of S. salar in this study suggests a similar reduction in lipid deposits, as the K correlates significantly with the energy content and therefore lipid density of the returning adults (equation 1). A similar correlation was found for S. salar spawning in the Norwegian River Drammen (Jonsson et al., 1997). Thus, a climate-driven ecosystem change may have affected the present Norwegian S. salar, as reported by Todd et al. (2008) from Scotland.

Although reduced energy assimilation of the post-smolts may be the prime reason for the decrease in growth, temperature may still have had a contributing effect because of associated metabolic costs. Friedland & Todd (2012) reported that S. salar growth was associated with temperature during winter and spring. Furthermore, Hokkaido chum salmon Oncorhynchus keta (Walbaum 1792) have had elevated growth and survival rates since the 1980s (opposite to the present results). Kaeriyama et al. (2014) assumed that this was a direct effect of higher surface temperature because of global warming. In addition, the growth of Chinook salmon Oncorhynchus tshawytscha (Walbaum 1792) in Alaska appears to be strongly influenced by oceanic winter temperatures (Wells et al., 2008). Thus, the possibility that increased temperature may have had an additional effect on the ongoing change in size and growth of S. salar cannot be rejected.

Age at maturity varied during the study period. First, the proportion of 1SW fish increased, but it decreased after 2000. Age at maturity is phenotypically plastic and

highly influenced by growth and size of the fish (Alm, 1959; Jonsson et al., 1984). For S. salar, Friedland & Haas (1996) indicated that late summer growth of the post-smolts was higher in S. salar maturing as 1SW than 2SW fish. Furthermore, Salminen (1997) reported that good feeding opportunities gave relatively more 1SW S. salar than do poorer feeding opportunities in the Baltic Sea. For hatchery S. salar, they found a significant relationship between early maturation and post-smolt growth, as found in rearing experiments with the River Imsa S. salar (Jonsson et al., 2012, 2013). In this study, the relationship was, however, non-linear, with a strong decrease after 2000 paralleled by a drop in K (Fig. 3).

A hypothesis that may explain this non-linearity in age at maturity is that the relatively high proportion of MSW fish in the 1970s was related to the above average S. salar growth during this decade (Jonsson & Jonsson, 2004). Maturation can be delayed if growth is unusually good (Jonsson et al., 1984). Ultimately, this may be an adaptive response, because of an expected increase in reproductive success associated with increased LT. If so, postponing maturation by 1year will probably more than compensate for the expected decrease in survival in accordance with life-history theory (Jonsson et al., 1984). For instance, when juvenile brown trout Salmo trutta L. 1758 switch from zoobenthic to fish feeding, growth rate increases and maturation is delayed relative to those that still feed on zoobenthos (Jonsson et al., 1993, 1999). Similarly, very fast-growing S. salar can delay maturation relative to conspecifics with a more average growth rate (Jonsson & Jonsson, 2004). This relationship between maturation and growth appears to hold across populations (Jonsson et al., 1991) and in other species, such as Arctic charr Salvelinus alpinus (L. 1758) (Larsson et al., 2005) and S. trutta (Forseth et al., 2009). Thus, extremely high post-smolt growth may have contributed to the elevated proportion of MSW fish in the 1970s.

Maturation can also be delayed, however, if growth rate is poor (Alm, 1959; Wilbur & Rudolf, 2006), which may be the situation after 2000. In this period, post-smolt growth was low and a gradually increasing proportion of the cohorts may have lacked the needed energy resources to carry out the return migration and spawn successfully as 1SW fish, relative to the requirements of their genetic programme. This latter assumption is supported by the decrease in K. Maturation can be delayed if food quality is poor and lipid densities are consequently low (Rowe & Thorpe, 1991; Berrigan & Charnov,

- 1994; Duston & Saunders, 1999). This holds for a number of other species, such as S. trutta (Bohlin et al., 1994), O. tshawytscha (Silverstein et al., 1998) and North Sea plaice Pleuronectes platessa L. 1758 (Grift et al., 2007). Hence, both extra high and extra low growth in the post-smolt period may be associated with a reduced proportion of 1SW fish as a phenotypically adaptive response. An increase in age at maturity after 2000 has also been reported from other S. salar populations (Otero et al., 2012), suggesting that this has wider application in east Atlantic populations of the species.

For feeding, S. salar may have moved farther north during recent years. Jensen et al. (2014) reported that considerable numbers of S. salar were observed as far north as the Svalbard Islands, between 78вИШ and 79вИШ N. This is north of the earlier known area of the species. The individuals sampled did not belong to the River Imsa population, but other rivers chiefly in northern Norway. It is known that feeding areas of salmonids have changed because of large-scale climate-driven ecosystem shifts in the ocean (Nielsen et al., 2013), and this may hold for many other species (P√ґrtner & Peck, 2010). For instance, Potts et al. (2014) reported that Argyrosomus coronus Griffiths & Heemstra

- 1995 on the African coast changed their distribution area because of global warming.


Thus, the possibility that the feeding area may have shifted northwards in association with climate warming cannot be ruled out, and if the area has changed, this may have influenced growth opportunities for S. salar.

Marine survival of S. salar in the River Imsa decreased with time before 2000 (Jonsson & Jonsson, 2004), but there is no significant trend in the present material. This may be associated with fisheries regulation and reduced fishing intensity at sea (Jensen et al., 1999). After 1988, all S. salar fishing with drift nets has been forbidden in Norwegian waters. The nets caught 2SW fish in particular (Jonsson et al., 2003). Furthermore, the Faroese S. salar fishery in the North Atlantic was closed after 2000 (www.nasco.int/neac_measures.html). This latter fishery caught mainly S. salar from their second ocean winter on, although large 1SW fish was also targeted. There is no evidence of density-dependent survival among S. salar in the ocean (Jonsson et al., 1998b). Thus, if anything, mean size of 1SW fish should have increased with a reduced fishing pressure on relatively large fish, opposite to the observed trend.

Age at maturity is partly inherited (Gjerde, 1984; Johnston et al., 2014) and selection because of reduced fishing on MSW S. salar may in the long-term change the genetic structure of populations (Hard et al., 2008). A further indication of reduced fishing on large S. salar may be an increased proportion of repeat spawners, as found in some areas (Niemel√§ et al., 2006; Chaput & BenoЋЖƒ±t, 2012). So far, however, little is known about the possible genetic effects on age at maturity of reduced fishing in S. salar (Marty et al., 2014; Uusi-Heikkil√§ et al., 2015).

Survivalappearedlittleinfluencedbywatertemperatureduringthepost-smoltperiod. For instance, there was no support for the hypothesis that mortality changed with increasing temperature for either wild or hatchery-produced S. salar. This appears not to agree with the general view that survival is closely associated with environmental temperature in fishes (Somero, 2004; Griffiths & Harrod, 2007). In this case, however, this effect may be small compared with other sources of mortality, such as predation (Hansen et al., 2003; M√§ntyniemi et al., 2012). In addition, variation in age at maturity may have affected their survival, as many S. salar die after spawning (Jonsson et al., 1997). On the other hand, the retrospective growth analysis from scales exhibited a positive correlation between first year growth and survival in wild S. salar. This parallels observations in other populations of S. salar (McCarthy et al., 2008; Peyronnet et al., 2008; Friedland et al., 2009) and in other anadromous species such as S. trutta (Jonsson & Jonsson, 2009) and O. tshawytscha (Woodson et al., 2013). Growth may mediate survival by the functional relationship between post-smolts and their predators as discussed by Jonsson & Jonsson (2009). The first period of marine life may be particularly demanding for the fish with high mortality when slower growing individuals may be lost because of predation, disease or starvation (Wells et al., 2012). Particularly in years when feeding opportunities are poor, large recruits may survive better than smaller conspecifics. Saloniemi et al. (2004) and Cross et al. (2008) reported that relatively large body size gave survival benefits for juvenile S. salar in years when the mortality was high.

There were close correlations between wild and hatchery-produced S. salar concerning K and age at maturity. This indicates that environmental conditions at sea influenced life-history variables of these fish similarly, and that life-history variation in hatchery S. salar at least partly reflects that of wild fish with similar genetic structure. Thus, hatchery-produced S. salar may be used as an ecological indicator on production conditions for wild S. salar at sea. From the Pacific north-west, it was reported that in

spite of temporal and spatial variation, the general trend in marine survival is similar for wild and hatchery coho salmon Oncorhynchus kisutch (Walbaum 1792) (Coronado & Hilborn, 1998) indicating that this has wider application than for the present population.

Together, this study revealed a significant change in the life history of S. salar of the River Imsa since the 1970s. The main drivers appear to be the ecosystem change with the rebuilding of the pelagic fish abundance, together with a decrease in abundance of large zooplankton and an increase in sea surface temperature in the Norwegian Sea. Smaller energy stores may render S. salar more susceptible to predation and parasitism, and the distribution area may move northwards where growth opportunities may be poorer. The pelagic ecosystem is highly dynamic, and as a predator, the life history of S. salar is highly dependent on other species. With large fluctuations in primary and secondary production in the food web, bottom-up effects on S. salar and other predators are to be expected.
