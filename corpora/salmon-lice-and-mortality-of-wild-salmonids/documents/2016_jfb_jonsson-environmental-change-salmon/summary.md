# Summary: Jonsson et al. (2016)

**Full title:** Environmental change influences the life history of salmon Salmo salar in the North Atlantic Ocean
**Journal:** Journal of Fish Biology, 88: 618–637
**DOI:** 10.1111/jfb.12854
**Document type:** Research article
**Priority questions:** Q9

---

## What this paper does

This paper analyses a 35-year time series (1976–2010) of Atlantic salmon (*Salmo salar*) life-history data from the River Imsa, south-western Norway — one of the most intensively monitored salmon rivers in Norway. The central question is how changes in the North Atlantic Ocean environment have affected the growth, body condition, age at maturity, and marine survival of Norwegian Atlantic salmon. The River Imsa empties into the Boknafjorden near Stavanger and supports a relatively small but extremely well-documented salmon population with near-complete trapping of both descending smolts and ascending adults.

The study draws on two complementary datasets: wild salmon monitored through the entire study period (1976–2010), and hatchery-reared fish (from River Imsa broodstock) released annually from 1981 onwards that then completed their marine life cycle naturally. The parallel wild/hatchery design is crucial because it allows environmental effects on life history to be distinguished from genetic changes over time. Over the full study period, 36,201 wild smolts migrated to sea (2.9% returned as adults) and 182,740 hatchery smolts were released (1.5% returned). Post-smolt growth was backcalculated from the first marine annulus in scales of returning adults, and regressions were fitted between life-history variables and sea surface temperatures in the eastern Norwegian Sea — the main feeding area for this stock based on tagging data.

The paper situates its findings in the context of known North Atlantic ecosystem changes: the collapse and subsequent rebuilding of pelagic fish stocks (capelin, herring, mackerel), declining zooplankton abundance (particularly *Calanus finmarchicus*), and ongoing warming of Norwegian Sea surface temperatures. These changes form the proposed causal framework for the observed life-history trends.

---

## Main findings

- **Body size declined substantially over 35 years.** Mean total length of returning 1SW wild salmon decreased from 63 cm (cohorts migrating 1976–1980) to 54 cm (2006–2010 cohorts), corresponding to a **40% decline in individual mass** (from 2.0 to 1.2 kg).

- **Condition factor (K) also decreased** across cohorts, indicating that fish were not only smaller but in poorer body condition relative to their length.

- **Body size correlated negatively with near-surface temperature in the eastern Norwegian Sea.** Warmer conditions were associated with smaller size at maturity, consistent with the temperature-size rule for poikilotherms in a food-limited environment.

- **Post-smolt growth was positively correlated with marine survival** (return rate to the River Imsa). Years with better post-smolt growth produced higher fractions of surviving adults, linking ocean feeding conditions directly to population-level survival.

- **Proportion maturing as 1SW fish varied temporally**, being lower in the 1970s and after 2000 compared with the 1980s–1990s, associated with declining energy reserves in the post-smolt period.

- **Hatchery fish from the same parental stock showed identical trends from 1981 onwards,** ruling out genetic change as the cause of body-size and survival trends, and confirming that the changes are environmentally driven.

- **Primary causal attribution:** Ecosystem-level restructuring in the North Atlantic — specifically the collapse and rebuilding of pelagic fish stocks, gradual decline in zooplankton abundance, and climate warming — are identified as the principal drivers of the observed life-history changes.

## Limitations and transferability to Norway

- **Single-river study:** River Imsa is a short (1 km), restricted river with a small population. Findings may not be representative of all Norwegian Atlantic salmon populations, particularly those from larger rivers with more complex population structures.
- **Correlation, not causation:** The paper demonstrates correlations between sea surface temperature and life-history traits; the causal mechanisms (direct temperature effects on physiology, indirect effects via food web) are inferred rather than experimentally demonstrated.
- **Confounded environmental drivers:** Temperature, zooplankton abundance, and pelagic fish community structure are correlated with each other; their individual contributions cannot be fully separated.
- **Time period ends 2010:** The most recent data are now 15 years old. North Atlantic conditions may have changed further.
- **No direct measurement of oceanic feeding conditions:** Prey availability in the Norwegian Sea is inferred from temperature proxies and literature, not from direct sampling concurrent with the salmon monitoring.

**Transferability to Norwegian TLS context:** This paper is directly and immediately relevant to Norway — it uses Norwegian fish, a Norwegian river, and Norwegian Sea oceanographic data. It provides authoritative quantitative documentation that Atlantic salmon declines in Norway have large oceanographic components unrelated to sea lice. This is critical context for the corpus: when evaluating whether the Norwegian TLS successfully protects wild salmon, it is essential to distinguish lice-induced mortality from the background mortality driven by North Atlantic food-web changes. A 40% decline in salmon body mass over 35 years is a large signal that must be accounted for in any analysis of population trends.

## Place in the literature

This paper contributes to a broader literature on declining Atlantic salmon marine survival in Norway and the North Atlantic, which includes influential papers by Friedland et al. (2009, 2014) on post-smolt growth and survival. It is authored by B. Jonsson and N. Jonsson (Norwegian Institute for Nature Research, NINA) and J. Albretsen (Institute of Marine Research, IMR) — institutions centrally involved in Norwegian salmon science and contributing to the evidence base used by the Norwegian Scientific Committee and the TLS. The paper does not address sea lice at all; its relevance to this corpus is as a non-lice baseline: it demonstrates that substantial, long-term population-level declines in Norwegian Atlantic salmon are explicable by oceanographic factors alone. For any study claiming that sea lice cause population-level mortality (Q7-Q9), this kind of oceanographic context must be accounted for to avoid attributing background mortality to lice.
