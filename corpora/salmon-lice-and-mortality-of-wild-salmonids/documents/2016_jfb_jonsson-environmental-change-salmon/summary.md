## What this paper does

Jonsson, Jonsson, and Albretsen (2016) investigate the impact of environmental changes on the life history traits of Atlantic salmon (*Salmo salar*) in the North Atlantic Ocean, specifically focusing on data collected from the River Imsa in southwestern Norway over a 35-year period (1976–2010). The study links the size of one-sea-winter (1SW) adult salmon, post-smolt growth rates, maturation strategies, and survival rates to rising sea surface temperatures (SST) in the Norwegian Sea and changes in the North Atlantic food web. This research is crucial as it provides a baseline understanding of salmon life history dynamics in the context of environmental change, independent of aquaculture influences such as sea lice.

The authors analyze both wild salmon and hatchery-reared cohorts, revealing parallel trends in life history traits that suggest environmental factors, rather than genetic drift, are driving observed changes. The findings are significant for understanding how climate change and ecosystem dynamics affect salmon populations, which are economically and ecologically important species.

## Methods and data

The study utilized a combination of long-term monitoring data from the River Imsa, including 36,201 wild smolts and 182,740 hatchery-reared smolts released from 1981 to 2010. The authors employed a Wolf trap to capture descending salmon and a fixed box trap for ascending fish, allowing for the collection of data on total length (LT), mass, and condition factor (K) of returning adults. Key life history metrics, such as post-smolt growth (G) and the proportion of fish maturing as 1SW, were calculated using scale samples and regression analyses.

The authors also correlated life history variables with SST data collected from a hydro-dynamical model of the Nordic Seas, focusing on temperature variations during the first year at sea. Statistical analyses included linear regression and ANCOVA to assess relationships between environmental variables and salmon life history traits.

## Key findings

- **Size Reduction:** The total length of wild 1SW salmon decreased from 63 cm to 54 cm, corresponding to a 40% decline in mass (from 2.0 kg to 1.2 kg) over the study period.
- **Condition Factor Decline:** The condition factor (K) of both wild and hatchery salmon decreased by approximately 8%, with a notable decline after 2000.
- **Post-Smolts Growth:** Post-smolt growth rates declined significantly, with hatchery fish showing a sharper decrease than wild fish.
- **Maturation Trends:** The proportion of salmon maturing as 1SW fish was lower in the 1970s and post-2000 compared to the 1980s and 1990s, with a minimum of about 30% observed after 2000.
- **Temperature Correlations:** SST rose approximately 1°C during the study, with negative correlations observed between LT and summer/winter SST, although temperature explained limited variance in size (r² ≈ 0.13–0.32).

## Limitations and caveats

The study is limited by its focus on a single river, which may not fully represent broader population dynamics across different environments. The authors also excluded multi-sea-winter (MSW) fish from most analyses due to their low numbers, potentially overlooking important life history variations. Additionally, while the study correlates environmental changes with life history traits, it does not establish direct causation, and the inferred feeding grounds for salmon are based on modeling rather than direct observation.

## What this paper does not claim

The authors do not claim that the observed changes in salmon life history traits are solely due to temperature increases or that genetic drift is a significant factor. They emphasize the role of ecosystem changes, particularly in the pelagic food web, as a primary driver of the observed trends. The study does not address the impact of aquaculture-related factors, such as sea lice, on salmon populations, focusing instead on natural environmental influences.

## Relevance to this corpus

This research is essential for understanding the baseline conditions of salmon populations in relation to environmental changes, particularly in the context of Q9, which addresses the impacts of various factors on salmon survival and growth. The findings contribute to the ongoing debate about the relative influences of climate change and aquaculture on salmon populations, providing a critical reference point for future studies.

This study complements other research on the effects of environmental changes on salmon populations, particularly in relation to food web dynamics and growth rates. It may also relate to discussions on the impacts of sea lice and aquaculture practices, although it does not directly address these issues.
