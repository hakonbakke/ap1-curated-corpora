# Summary: Jonsson et al. (2016)

**Full title:** Environmental change influences the life history of salmon *Salmo salar* in the North Atlantic Ocean  
**Journal:** Journal of Fish Biology, 88: 618–637  
**DOI:** 10.1111/jfb.12854  
**Authors:** NINA (B. & N. Jonsson), IMR (J. Albretsen)  
**Priority questions:** Q9

---

## What this paper does

35-year (1976–2010) trap and hatchery-release time series from **River Imsa**, SW Norway, linking 1SW adult size, post-smolt growth, maturation and survival to **Norwegian Sea SST** and North Atlantic food-web change.

## Scale of monitoring

| Group | Smolts to sea | Adult return rate |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| Wild | 36,201 | **2.9%** |
| Hatchery (1981–2010) | 182,740 | **1.5%** |

## Key quantitative trends

- Wild 1SW **LT 63 → 54 cm** (r²≈0.51); **~40% mass** (2.0 → 1.2 kg).
- **K** and post-smolt **G** declined; sharp drop after 2000.
- **%1SW** lower in 1970s and post-2000 than 1980s–1990s (Weibull cohort fits).
- **G** positively predicts adult LT and wild marine survival; hatchery tracks wild (LT r²=0.37, K r²=0.70, %1SW r²=0.71).
- SST (0–10 m, 62–64°N) rose ~**1°C** over study; LT negatively correlated with summer/winter SST (slopes ~−29 to −56 mm °C⁻¹) but temperature explains limited variance (r²≈0.13–0.32).

## Interpretation

Parallel wild/hatchery trends imply **environmental** not genetic causation. Authors emphasise pelagic restructuring (mackerel/herring, zooplankton) over direct temperature-size effects. **No lice or aquaculture metrics.**

## Relevance to corpus (Q9)

Essential **non-lice baseline**: large marine growth and survival variation in Norwegian salmon must be partitioned before attributing population change to lice/TLS.

## Limitations

Single river; inferred feeding grounds; MSW fish excluded from most analyses.
