The link between host density and parasite population size is wellestablished. However, while high host density facilitates parasite transmission, it may negatively impact parasites through more intense competition and/or more intense host immune defence. We assessed the performance of individually tagged female salmon lice (Lepeophtheirus salmonis), an ectoparasite of Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar), under experimental low and high host density conditions, the latter mimicking salmon aquaculture conditions. Lice at high density exhibit greater pre-reproductive mortality, reduced growth, delayed maturation, smaller adult sizes and lower fecundity. Adult size in salmon lice correlates negatively with age at maturity and positively with early fecundity, lifetime fecundity and lifespan. Thus, lice in high-density host populations perform poorly across all life-history traits, probably owing to stronger immune responses of hosts initially exposed to higher parasite doses. This study suggests density-dependent regulation of parasites in intensive agriculture, where hosts are farmed at high densities. Our results further suggest that salmon lice in aquaculture may be selected for greater resistance to host immune defence. As host populations now consist overwhelmingly of farmed fish, continued research is essential to understand the implications for wild salmonids, which are frequently infected by lice originating from farms.

### 1. Introduction

High host density facilitates transmission and allows for greater parasite abundances [1–4]. While interspecific variation in parasite life histories is well-documented in some taxa [5–9], intraspecific variation remains little explored [10]. A handful of studies find that parasite growth, reproduction or survival may decrease in a density-dependent manner (e.g. [11–14]). The positive effects of host density on parasite populations may therefore be more nuanced than usually thought, highlighting the need for experimental studies comparing parasite life histories under contrasted environmental conditions [15].

In the last decades, increasing efforts have been made to understand how parasites may be responding to man-made changes in their environment [16– 20]. For parasites, a major type of global change is intensive agriculture, where hosts are cultivated in large numbers and at high densities while being well-fed. great evolutionary potential, in which rapid adaptive changes are taking place [20,21]. In addition to resistance to control strategies including the application of drugs, antibiotics or pesticides [22–24], life-history traits also seem to have evolved in response to farming practices [21,25]. Empirical evidence remains scarce, however, for how these new ecological conditions affect individual variation in parasite performance. Yet this knowledge is essential for understanding whether and how selection on parasites is changing as global food production keeps accelerating, with control strategies often consisting of dispensing drugs based on average abundance thresholds set without accounting for potential density-dependent effects (e.g. [26]).

An example of a parasite associated with dramatic agriculture intensification is the salmon louse (Lepeophtheirus salmonis), a marine copepod that is an obligate ectoparasite of salmonid fishes. The expansion of Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) aquaculture drastically increased the availability of hosts suitable for salmon lice. In contrast to the wild salmonid hosts that occur at low densities and are migratory in coastal waters [27], farmed fish are indeed kept year-round in marine cages at extremely high densities, favouring persistently high rates of parasite encounter [16,28]. Consequently, salmon lice have become pests on salmon farms [29,30], and spillover from farms to the natural environment is now a major conservation concern for wild Atlantic and Pacific salmon [31–33].

Salmon lice reproduce sexually and mate on the host, and it is common for multiple individuals to infect the same host. Life history is linked to virulence, with early maturing and more fecund female lice inflicting more damage to their host than individuals with relatively slower paces of life [34]. Common garden studies indicate that salmon lice in aquaculture have evolved greater investment into early reproduction at the expense of future fecundity and survival, as well as greater virulence [25,35]. A negative relationship between adult parasite load and parasite fecundity has been found, suggesting within-host competition among parasite females [13]. These studies, however, rely primarily on group-level comparisons and do not allow for investigating phenotypic variation or testing for the effects of aquaculture conditions on individual parasites, because parasites could not be tracked individually. With new technology, this is now possible.

Here, we use a recently developed tagging method for parasites [36] to elucidate the combined effect of host population density and rate of host exposure to infection on the performance of parasites. Using an experimental approach where Atlantic salmon were maintained in replicate populations under conditions mimicking natural (low density with a lower infection dose) or aquacultural conditions (high density with high infection dose), we monitored life histories of individual parasites and explored how body size relates to other life-history traits across our treatments. Our aim was to compare how salmon lice sampled from, and thus presumably adapted to, aquaculture would perform under high (aquaculture) and low (ancestral) host and parasite densities.

### 2. Methods

#### (a) Study system

The salmon louse, L. salmonis, is a horizontally transmitted ectoparasitic copepod with a direct life cycle that parasitizes salmonid fishes in marine waters. Salmon lice graze the mucus, skin and ultimately feed on the blood of their hosts, which on heavily infected fish causes severe open wounds leading to reduced growth, osmotic stress and secondary infections [29,37,38]. The transmission stage is the non-feeding, planktonic copepodid stage [37]. Salmon lice have eight developmental stages separated by moulting, five of these on their host, with mobile pre-adult and adult stages [39]. Females undergo their final moult into the adult stage slightly later than males (about 45 versus 35 days post-infection (DPI) at 8–9°C [40]) and mate shortly afterwards, with the male cementing a pair of spermatophores to the ventral side of the female’s genital segment near the genital openings [41]. The female salmon louse retains these spermatophores for egg fertilization over consecutive clutches, although repeated mating and multiple paternity are observed [42].

Starting soon after moulting to adulthood (about 60 DPI at 8–9°C [40,43]), female salmon lice begin to extrude eggs in pairs of cylindrical strings enveloped within an external matrix that remain attached to the female until hatching [29]. Female salmon lice can produce several successive egg string pairs over their lives at a regular interval dependent on temperature, previously documented as up to 11 clutches and an average of every 10 days at 10°C ([43]; up to 22 clutches in this study, see below). In salmon lice, the first clutch is characteristically small, followed by larger subsequent clutches (e.g. electronic supplementary material, figure S1; [13,43]).

#### (b) Experimental design

Our experimental treatment consisted of manipulating both the density of hosts and the infection dose, as these often go hand in hand. In addition, to investigate geographical variation in salmon louse life histories and their responses to treatment, we collected egg strings from at least 50 ovigerous females from three different farmed locations along the coast of Norway, namely Austevoll (60.08869° N, 5.26662° E) and Oppedal (61.06737° N, 5.56413° E) in Vestland county and Fosså (59.2687° N, 6.1409° E) in Rogaland county. The eggs were hatched in the laboratory and incubated for 14 days following Hamre et al. [44], at which point they had reached the infective copepodid stage. These were used to infect naïve post-smolt Atlantic salmon kept in 1 × 1 m, 500 l tanks receiving filtered and UV-treated seawater from the nearby harbour (2–6 l min−1 flow rate). The fish hosts were kept in groups of either five fish (‘low host density’ treatment) or 15–16 fish (‘high host density’ treatment), with one treatment pair per geographical origin.

To further increase differences in exposure rate between treatments, the fish in the low host density tanks were infected at full tank volume with an average of only 15 copepodites per fish (five fish per tank), while fish in the high host density tanks

were infected by first lowering the water level of the tank by about two-thirds and then adding an average of 30 copepodites per fish (15 fish per tank for Oppedal and Fosså lice, 16 fish for Austevoll lice). All fish were commercial post-smolt Atlantic salmon purchased from the same producer (Industrilaboratoriet, Bergen, Norway). The infection for Oppedal and Austevoll lice used salmon from the same production batch (infection dates, Oppedal: 12 November 2020; Austevoll: 22 December 2020), while the salmon infected from Fosså lice (on 2 July 2021) came from a later batch (see the electronic supplementary material, table S1).

#### (c) Laboratory sampling procedure

Starting at day 54–61 DPI (Oppedal: 60–61; Austevoll: 59; Fosså: 54) when female L. salmonis had begun to produce eggs, hosts and parasites in each of the six tanks were sampled weekly according to the following procedure. Each salmon host was individually netted and sedated in 1 ml l−1 metomidate and 0.3 ml l−1 benzocaine, until it was calm enough for us to measure its length and weight and collect the adult salmon lice from it. On the initial sampling day, each salmon was surgically implanted with a radio-frequency identification (RFID) tag (i-Tag 162, BTS-ID®). The weight and length of each salmon host were recorded, along with its RFID number and the number of adult male and female salmon lice it carried. The salmon was subsequently placed into a dark holding tank to recover until salmon lice could be placed back onto it.

The salmon lice collected from each fish host were placed together on labelled paper moistened with seawater in a Petri dish lit from below and photographed using a Canon EOS 800D camera with a 60 mm macro lens mounted on a vertical stand. Female salmon lice were arranged in rows with egg string pairs extended to facilitate measures of egg string length and pressed briefly flat immediately before photographing so that all lice were in focus (electronic supplementary material, figure S1). On initial sampling days, female salmon lice were RFID-tagged by glueing a p-Chip (p-Chip Corp., Princeton, NJ, USA) on the dorsal side of the genital segment, following Folk & Mennerat [36]. Females who were found without an RFID tag during subsequent samplings were re-tagged and matched to their previous ID by visually comparing their unique pigmentation pattern against previous photos (see [36] for details on tagging procedure, tag retention and visual identification of re-tagged lice).

At each sampling, female RFID tags were scanned, and egg strings were carefully detached from the females with a pair of fine forceps. Each pair of egg strings was immediately placed into a numbered flow-through incubator tube filled with seawater. After this, the salmon lice were returned to their original host, and the salmon was placed back into its original tank. The egg strings were then photographed at higher magnification (electronic supplementary material, figure S2; see also §2d(v)) and monitored daily for hatching. Egg production starts in the ovaries, located in the cephalothorax, and is followed by vitellogenesis in the oviduct, leading to the abdominal genital complex. Mature, fertilized eggs are extruded in the form of egg strings enclosed in a matrix, and although independent from the female, they remain attached externally to her genital complex [41]. There is to our knowledge no evidence, either from this or other studies, that detaching egg strings manually affects reproduction in salmon lice.

Each 500 l tank was equipped with a 1 mm mesh filter under its outlet flow, which was checked daily for dead and dislodged salmon lice. These were photographed using the same procedure as during population sampling and their egg strings were placed into incubator tubes. Egg strings that were not attached to a female louse were assigned to a female from that tank whenever possible, based on the hatch date and developmental indication. Out of 178 adult female lice initially sampled, 46 were never recaptured in the outlet filters. This might be because of being trapped and decomposing in an inaccessible portion of the outlet pipes or consumption by the hosts (although never observed directly).

The total sampling period covered by this dataset extends from 11 January 2021 to 30 December 2021. As salmon hosts were growing during this period, for welfare reasons, they were replaced with new, naïve hosts when they reached 45 cm in length (approx. 1 kg). The timing of host replacement was picked at the earliest possible occasion, depending on delivery date and a minimal acclimation period of two weeks (Austevoll: after 80 and 160 days; Fosså: at 150 days; Oppedal: at 204 DPI). A total of 99 Atlantic salmon smolts were used for this study, including the 61 salmon initially infected. Replaced hosts were euthanized under anaesthesia, and salmon lice were manually transferred onto a new host. In addition, as part of a larger research project, the salmon hosts were re-infected to start a second generation within each tank, so the relatively long-lived salmon lice (77 out of 178 females) were living for the latter part of their lives alongside a younger generation of conspecifics. Owing to their smaller size (figure 1), adult male salmon lice could not be tagged and are therefore not included in this study.

#### (d) Variables investigated

##### (i) Parasite load

The highest number of salmon lice in each tank was observed on the first day of sampling (54–61 DPI), by which time all parasites had reached their adult stage. By continuously extracting blood to produce eggs, female salmon lice cause most of the damage to their hosts [13,34]. As an estimate of parasite load, we therefore took the initial count of female adult salmon lice per fish.

##### (ii) Timing of life-history events

Like most arthropods [45], the developmental rate in L. salmonis depends on temperature. The average temperature of the seawater flowing into the tanks and incubators was 9.04°C (8.8–9.5°C) in the 54 first DPI during which the lice developed and 8.92°C (8.55–9.5°C) during the entire study period. To account for possible effects of temperature variation on metabolism, we express the timing of life-history events (namely, here, the age of female lice at reproduction and their lifespan) in degree-DPI

##### (iii) Adult somatic and reproductive size

Adult body size of female L. salmonis was measured from photos taken during sampling sessions, using the segmented line tool in ImageJ [46]. As a measure of somatic body size, we used the distance from the eyespot to the posterior ridge of the cephalothoracic segment (cephalothorax length; figure 1a). Reproductive body size was measured by the distance from the anterior to the posterior edge of the genital segment (genital segment length; figure 1b). These measurements were selected because of the consistent and repeatable visibility of these physical features with our photography set-up.

##### (iv) Age at reproduction

The most accurate phenological indicator in our study is the date of hatching of the eggs, as this was monitored daily, contrary to egg extrusion, which was only assessed weekly (i.e. during population sampling). We used the timing of hatching of the second clutch (in DDPI) as a proxy for female age at reproduction because the second clutch was the one for which we had the most observations (n = 144), as not all first clutches hatched or were sampled. The hatching times for the first and second clutches were strongly correlated (electronic supplementary material, figure S3).

##### (v) Early fecundity

We estimated the number of eggs produced in the second clutch as a measure of early fecundity. The length of each of the two egg strings in a clutch was measured from photos taken during sampling (figure 1c; electronic supplementary material, figure S1), using the segmented line tool in ImageJ [46]. Mean egg length was calculated from photos of up to five non-overlapping lengths of typically 20 eggs taken using a Canon camera (EOS 600D or EOS R) connected to an Olympus microscope (SZX9 or SZX12), with the accompanying magnification (20–40×) recorded and calibrated accordingly (figure 1d; electronic supplementary material, figure S2). The total number of eggs in each egg string was then estimated by dividing the total egg string length by the average egg length. Finally, the total number of eggs in the entire clutch was the sum of counts for each egg string, rounded to the nearest integer.

##### (vi) Lifetime fecundity

Lifetime fecundity is taken here as the sum of all clutch sizes produced from the second clutch up until the last clutch produced by each female prior to death. A previous assessment [36] found no significant impact of the tagging procedure on female louse survival or egg extrusion. A total of 127 females were included in this analysis, after excluding 21 females for whom we could not ascertain that their lifetime fecundity was not affected by accidental loss of eggs (also see §2d(vii)).

##### (vii) Adult lifespan

Mortality for female salmon lice was recorded as either the day when a louse was found in the outlet filter of an experimental tank, or as the first day it was noted as missing. A preliminary study showed that while adult female lice infrequently disperse among hosts [47], they seem more likely to do so in the days immediately following their transfer onto a new host, typically

when hosts needed to be replaced [48]. The status was therefore censored for females that had been lost shortly after host replacement, as these cases might correspond to unsuccessful dispersal attempts from females (14 out of 178 females). For all other lice, the adult lifespan was set to the time when they were lost from the host population.

#### (e) Statistical analysis

Here, we use hypothesis testing rather than Bayesian estimation owing to the experimental design, where the aim was to test the effects of the host density treatment on parasites (for a broader discussion, see [49,50]). All statistical analyses were carried out in the R statistical programming environment (v. 4.3.3; [51]).

##### (i) Parasite load

To test whether parasite load differed between the high- and low-host-density treatments, we used generalized linear models (GLMs) fitted with a Poisson distribution with the number of adult female salmon lice per fish as a dependent variable. The models included host density (two levels) and the geographical origin of the parent generation (three levels) as categorical variables. An interaction between treatment and geographical origin was originally included, but not kept in the selected models, as it was not significant and did not improve the fit of the models. A negative binomial model was initially considered, but not retained as the distribution of female parasite loads did not deviate significantly from a Poisson distribution (p = 0.78; electronic supplementary material, figure S4).

##### (ii) Somatic and reproductive body size

To test the effect of host density on adult female size, we fitted linear mixed-effects models with either somatic or reproductive body size as a covariate, using the ‘lme4’ package [52]. Host density treatment and geographical origin were included as factors. Although L. salmonis do not moult again as adults, a visual analysis suggested that body size kept increasing during a short period past the final moult (electronic supplementary material, figure S5). Repeated measurements for each individual female were therefore used, with time post-infection (in log-transformed DDPI) as a covariate and female ID as a random factor. The ID of each female’s original host (assumedly the host on which the female developed) was also included as a random effect. Finally, to test for density effects on growth, we included the interaction between treatment and time. An interaction between treatment and geographical origin was included initially, but not kept as it was not significant and did not improve the fit of the models. The dispersion and normality of residuals were validated using the ‘DHARMa’ package [53]; this indicated a quadratic effect of time, which we consequently also included in the final model.

##### (iii) Age at reproduction

The effect of host density treatment on age at reproduction was tested with linear mixed-effects models, with second clutch hatch time as the dependent variable and each female’s original host ID as a random effect. The model included treatment and geographical origin as factors. In addition, female somatic or reproductive body size (measured when the second clutch was collected) was included as a covariate, resulting in two different models, one for each size measure. After inspecting the models’ residuals, the size measures were log-transformed to normalize the distribution of the residuals, and an additional quadratic term for somatic body size (cephalothorax length) was added. The interaction between treatment and geographical origin, as well as that between treatment and size, was also tested, but as they did not improve the fit of the models and were not significant, they were not kept.

##### (iv) Early fecundity

To test the effect of host density treatment on early fecundity, we used GLMs fitted with a negative binomial distribution (glm.nb), using the ‘MASS’ package [54]. The egg count estimate for the second clutch of each female was used as the dependent variable. Host density treatment and geographical origin were included as factors; their interaction was initially tested but not included in the final model because it did not improve the model fit and was not significant. To account for a potential negative effect of parasite load on fecundity [13], the number of female salmon lice recorded on the same host was included as a covariate. In addition, to investigate the size–fecundity relationship, log-transformed measures of female size at second clutch (cephalothorax length or genital segment length) were included as covariates, resulting in two separate models. Three females (out of 139) were excluded from these analyses as only one egg string was collected from their second clutch (instead of two), and we could not rule out that this might have been an artefact.

##### (v) Lifetime fecundity

The effect of host density treatment on lifetime fecundity was tested using a GLM fitted with a negative binomial distribution (glm.nb), using the ‘MASS’ package [54]. Host density treatment and geographical origin were included as factors; their interaction was tested but not included in the final model, as it did not improve the model fit. Parasite load, here taken as the number of female salmon lice per host at initial observation, was included as a covariate. Finally, log-transformed measures

of female size at second clutch (cephalothorax length or genital segment length) were included as covariates, resulting in two separate models. For each of them, models including linear and quadratic terms for size were compared, and only the best-fit model for each size variable was retained.

##### (vi) Adult lifespan

The effect of treatment on adult mortality was tested by comparing the observed lifespan of adult female salmon lice between the high and the low host density treatments, with a Cox proportional hazards regression model using the ‘survival’ package [55]. The start time was defined as the first day that adults from each geographical origin were sampled, and the stop time was the recorded day when the louse was lost from the population, both expressed in DDPI. Treatment and geographical origin were included as factors; the interaction between these was considered but not included in the final models because it did not improve model fit and was non-significant. The latest available measure of live female size (cephalothorax length or genital segment length) was also included as a covariate and log-transformed. Host density treatment in interaction with female size was included for genital segment length but not cephalothorax length, as the interaction did not improve the fit of the model and was not significant. Model diagnostics used the ‘survminer’ package.

### 3. Results

#### (a) Parasite load

Prevalence was 98% (45 out of 46 hosts) in the high host density group and 93% (14 out of 15 hosts) in the low host density group. The mean number of adult female lice per host was slightly lower in the low host density treatment (mean ± s.e., high density: 3.11 ± 0.26; low density: 2.33 ± 0.40), but the difference was not statistically significant (p = 0.13; table 1; for details see the electronic supplementary material, table S1).

#### (b) Somatic and reproductive body size

Both somatic and reproductive body size were larger for females in the low (n = 35) than in the high (n = 143) host density treatment (95% confidence interval (CI): somatic size [0.17, 0.41], reproductive size [0.37, 1.00]; figure 2). Female body size increased over time shortly after moulting, before levelling out (electronic supplementary material, figure S5). Females in the low host density treatment showed less initial growth before reaching their asymptotic size (table 2).

#### (c) Age at reproduction

Female salmon lice in the low (n = 29) host density treatment reproduced earlier than females in the high (n = 115) host density treatment. The effect of treatment was highly significant in both models (95% CI: somatic size as covariate [−21.4, −8.78], reproductive size as covariate [−18.7, −6.00]; figure 3a,d). Female age at reproduction correlated negatively with both size metrics (95% CI: somatic size, linear [−5116, −248.9], quadratic [63, 2167]; reproductive size, linear [−88.1, −29.8]; table 3a).

#### (d) Early fecundity

Early fecundity, here the number of eggs produced in the second clutch, was greater in the low host density treatment (n = 28) than in the high host density treatment (n = 107). This difference was significant whether accounting for somatic or reproductive body size (95% CI: somatic size model [0.12, 0.34], reproductive size model [0.04, 0.23]; figure 3b,e). Early fecundity increased both with somatic body size (95% CI [1.50, 3.99]) and reproductive body size (95% CI [1.66, 2.68]) but was not related to parasite load (table 3b).

#### (e) Lifetime fecundity

Lifetime fecundity was greater in the low host density treatment (n = 28) than in the high host density treatment (n = 99), and the difference was significant in both models (95% CI: somatic size model [0.21, 0.91], reproductive size model [0.18, 0.93]; figure 3c,f). Lifetime fecundity also increased with body size (95% CI: somatic size, linear [3.1, 12]; reproductive size, linear [14, 74], quadratic [−33, −4.8]) but was not related to female parasite load (table 3c). The most fecund female, in the low host density treatment, lived for 303 DPI (1789 DDPI) and extruded 22 viable clutches, which is twice as many as the highest number of clutches previously reported for this species [43].

#### (f) Adult lifespan

Females lived for up to 240 DPI (1419 DDPI) and 322 DPI (1890 DDPI) in the high and low host density treatment, respectively. The longest-lived female appeared to be an outlier, as she never produced any eggs. ### 4. Discussion

Our study aimed to understand how a parasite is impacted by conditions found in the aquaculture of its host, as compared to conditions representing the ancestral environment for the parasite. We found that increased host density combined with increased host exposure to infection negatively affects the performance of the ectoparasitic salmon lice, L. salmonis. Despite a much greater initial dose of infective larvae, adult parasite loads were not significantly greater, indicating increased juvenile mortality under the high-density conditions. Female parasites developing in the high host density treatment achieved smaller adult sizes and, as size correlated with other life-history traits, they also reproduced later and were less fecund. All in all, the main effect of the high host density treatment on parasites was reduced juvenile survival and growth, with cascading consequences throughout the adult stage, ultimately impacting their lifetime reproductive success.

#### (a) Effect of treatment on parasite load and performance

The treatments employed in our experiment differed in two main ways, namely the number of hosts (5 versus 15 fish per tank) and the initial dose of infective copepodites the hosts were exposed to (15 versus 30 copepodites per fish). This experimental design aimed at resembling natural conditions, where host density and parasite abundance often go hand in hand. In the high host density treatment, double infection doses, administered in a reduced water volume, probably facilitated initial attachment of greater numbers of larval parasites to the hosts. In the absence of any regulation mechanism, one might expect the intensities of parasite infection at maturity to be at least doubled in the high host density treatment. This was apparently not the case, as average adult parasite loads differed by only 25% (electronic supplementary material, table S1). This is consistent with recent experimental work showing dose-dependent mortality of developing lice caused by host immunity ([14]; see also [12] for similar findings in parasitic nematodes). More specifically, at the site of parasite attachment, a dose-dependent increase was found in the expression of interleukins, i.e. host proteins involved in the activation and migration of immune cells ([56] and references therein). We therefore argue that host cellular immunity may have played a role in the greater mortality of juvenile salmon lice in the high host density treatment.

Salmon lice faced with stronger host immunity may, in turn, have synthesized greater amounts of defence proteins such as labial gland proteins LsLGP3, known to induce the apoptosis of host epidermal leucocytes [57]. #### (b) Does direct competition or host stress impact parasite performance?

Besides host immunity, the observed differences in parasite survival and development could result from direct competition among developing lice for resources or differing host stress levels between treatments. Because lice do not become mobile grazers until the pre-adult stages, direct competition among lice is unlikely prior to this. Our study does not allow us to address this, as we did not count the pre-adult lice stages. A previous study, although not accounting for growth rate or adult size, showed that the number of adult female lice on a fish correlated negatively with fecundity [13]. In the present study, we do not find any significant correlation between adult female parasite load and fecundity (table 3b,c), once accounting for the effects of treatment and body size. We therefore argue that the differences in fecundity in both this and the prior study probably resulted from differing environments during the development of salmon lice rather than direct competition.

There is no prior evidence that host chronic stress affects growth in salmon lice, and studies of Atlantic salmon fail to provide clear support for a link between host density and chronic stress. Indeed, while correlative evidence suggests a link between host chronic stress and density in Atlantic salmon [63,64], an experimental test failed to unequivocally support this [65]. Acute stress, on the other hand, was experimentally found to increase susceptibility to salmon lice [65], and conversely, greater infection doses appear to cause higher plasma cortisol levels [56]. In our study, the infection procedure differed between treatments, and salmon in the high host density tanks may have been under greater acute stress during infection owing to temporarily lowering the tank water level, in addition to being exposed to greater infection doses. Fully disentangling the contributions of host stress and immunity is therefore not possible with our study design.

#### (c) Correlations among life-history traits

Adult body size correlates strongly and positively with fecundity, as previously observed in this and other species [43,62,66– 68], and with age at reproduction (consistent with [34]). The overall picture is that bigger individuals consistently perform better on all measures of adult performance, namely earlier reproduction, greater fecundity and longer lifespan (see also the electronic supplementary material, figure S6 for growth and reproductive rates). Consistent with patterns found in non-parasitic species [69–72], in this parasite, adult body size stands out as a key trait that reflects individual variation in resource use [73], summarizes early life conditions and affects lifetime performance.

#### (d) Body growth of salmon lice

Since each female louse was marked, we could track the sizes of individual lice during the period following the final moult. This revealed that genital size, but also somatic size, kept increasing until reaching a final maximal size. Genital growth consistent with sexual maturation past the final moult was previously known [29,41,74], but the increase in somatic body size was unexpected. Cephalothorax length has previously been assumed to remain unchanged after the final moult [74]. Rather than a change in the cuticle itself, this increase in linear size probably captured volumetric changes in body condition that occurred as females were preparing for egg production, in a similar manner to free-living copepods [75].

#### (e) Evolutionary implications

Our study gives insight into individual variation in parasite life-history traits in relation to the parasites’ environment, here manipulated via host density and infection dose. The high-density treatment caused a generally lower parasite performance across the three geographical origins of the lice. This confirms that for a relatively long-lived parasite, like the salmon louse that evolved on wild salmon at low (natural) population densities, aquaculture conditions represent a marked change in their environment. Salmon host populations now consist overwhelmingly of intensively farmed salmon, with sympatric wild salmon playing a minor role as hosts, while being the recipients of frequent infections originating from aquaculture populations. The epidemiological consequences for wild Atlantic and Pacific salmon are well-documented, with elevated abundance of salmon lice larvae that originate from farms in coastal waters, which the wild salmon pass during outward migration. This has a severe impact on salmon survival and recruitment [31–33]. Our results additionally suggest that this parasite may face harsher conditions on farms owing to stronger indirect competition mediated by immune responses of well-fed hosts, while transmission is facilitated by high host densities. This combination of stronger within-host competition with growth-reducing effects and enhanced opportunity for transmission may select for increased virulence [16,76,77]. Indeed, as common garden studies showed, lice from aquaculture evolved greater investment in early reproduction at the expense of adult survival, resulting in greater virulence [25,35]. This may represent an additional cause for concern for wild salmonids. Experimental studies of parasite evolution under the two types of environments compared here are now needed to better understand how parasites will adapt to increasingly intensively farmed host populations and to predict the future impacts of spillover events to wild hosts in natural environments.
