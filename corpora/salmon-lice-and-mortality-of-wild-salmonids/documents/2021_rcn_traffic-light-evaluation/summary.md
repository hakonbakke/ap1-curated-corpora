# Summary: Eliasen et al. 2021 An evaluation of the Scientific Basis of the Traffic Light System for Norwegian Salmonid Aquaculture

**Full title:** An evaluation of the Scientific Basis of the Traffic Light System for Norwegian Salmonid Aquaculture  
**Journal:** Expert committee report (not journal peer review)  
**DOI / URL:** https://trafikklyssystemet.no/  
**Document type:** Expert committee report  
**Priority questions:** Q7, Q8, Q9, Q10  
**Funding / COI:** The Evaluation Committee (EvalComm) was appointed by the Research Council of Norway at the request of the Norwegian Ministry of Trade, Industry and Fisheries. No aquaculture industry representation was listed.

---

## What this paper does

The report evaluates the scientific foundation of the Traffic Light System (TLS) used in Norwegian salmon aquaculture to regulate growth and mitigate the impact of sea lice on wild salmonids. Convened by the Research Council of Norway, the Evaluation Committee (EvalComm) comprised eight international experts from five European countries, with a focus on assessing the scientific models, methods, and the extent to which TLS recommendations are grounded in empirical evidence. The evaluation process began in late 2020 and included 14 online meetings, culminating in this final report published on December 21, 2021.

The significance of this evaluation lies in its identification of critical weaknesses in the TLS framework, particularly regarding the mortality thresholds established by Taranger et al. (2011), which lack a solid empirical basis. The report emphasizes the need for improved documentation of uncertainty and expert elicitation processes, as well as the absence of performance monitoring in the TLS. By addressing these issues, the report aims to enhance the transparency and legitimacy of the TLS, which is considered one of the most sophisticated salmon risk assessment systems globally.

## Methods and data

The EvalComm reviewed approximately 100 documents, totaling over 1,000 pages, related to the TLS. The assessment focused on six key areas: knowledge inclusion, mortality threshold estimates, modelling frameworks, uncertainty estimation, uncertainty communication, and expert elicitation. The committee engaged with the Norwegian Expert Group (ExpGrp) and Steering Group (SG) to clarify operational processes and ensure comprehensive documentation was available for review.

The mortality thresholds central to the TLS are derived from the work of Taranger et al. (2011), which the authors acknowledged as lacking solid empirical evidence. The report highlights the need for regular reassessment of these thresholds and suggests that in situ studies on the effects of sea lice on wild salmon should be prioritized. The modelling frameworks used in the TLS include advanced hydrodynamic and life history models that simulate sea lice dispersion and salmon smolt migration, but these models are noted to be calibration-limited and sensitive to assumptions made regarding mortality thresholds.

## Key findings

1. **Knowledge Inclusion**: The report notes a commendable ambition to include diverse knowledge sources in the TLS, but documentation of outreach and grey literature rules is weak. Recommendations include robust reporting of knowledge inclusion processes and explicit policies for evaluating submitted knowledge (R1, R2).

2. **Mortality Threshold Estimates**: The mortality thresholds lack a solid empirical basis and have remained unchanged since the TLS's inception. The committee recommends regular reassessment of these thresholds and the need for peer-reviewed studies on the effects of sea lice on wild salmon (R5, R6).

3. **Modelling Framework**: The modelling systems (IMR, NVI, and SINTEF) are advanced but heavily reliant on calibration data, which can introduce variability and uncertainty. The report suggests prioritizing sensitivity analysis over further model refinement (R7).

4. **Uncertainty Estimation and Communication**: The report identifies significant gaps in the quantification and communication of uncertainty within the TLS. Recommendations include presenting model results with confidence intervals and adopting clearer language for communicating uncertainty to stakeholders (R8, R9, R10).

5. **Expert Elicitation**: While expert judgement is integral to the TLS, the process of expert elicitation is poorly documented and does not adhere to best practice standards. The committee recommends that expert assessment processes be described and justified according to established protocols (R11).

6. **System Performance**: The report highlights the absence of an assessment of whether the TLS achieves conservation outcomes. It calls for external validation of the TLS against river conservation outcomes and suggests embedding the TLS within an iterative DPSIR (Driver-Pressure-State-Impact-Response) framework for ongoing evaluation (R14, R15).

## Limitations and caveats

The report does not provide an exhaustive technical audit of every model line or evaluate alternative regulatory frameworks. It also excludes assessments of sea trout and Arctic char, despite ongoing policy intentions to expand the TLS to these species. The committee acknowledges that the absence of documented reassessments of mortality thresholds could undermine confidence in the TLS.

## What this paper does not claim

The EvalComm does not claim to have conducted a comprehensive review of all aspects of the TLS or to have proposed a new regulatory framework. It does not evaluate the efficacy of the TLS in achieving conservation outcomes, nor does it provide definitive solutions to the identified weaknesses but rather offers recommendations for improvement.

## Relevance to this corpus

This evaluation is foundational for understanding the ongoing debates surrounding the legitimacy of threshold and calibration issues in the TLS, which are critical for regulatory frameworks in aquaculture. It links to priority questions Q10 and has been cited in subsequent critiques, including those by Van Nes et al. (2024) and Vannes (2024).

## Related evidence in corpus

The findings and recommendations of this report may inform ongoing discussions and research related to the impacts of sea lice on wild salmon populations and the effectiveness of regulatory frameworks in aquaculture management.
