# Summary: Van Nes, Imsland & Jones 2025 Response to Stige LC, Vollset KW, Diserud O, et al.

**Full title:** Response to Stige LC, Vollset KW, Diserud O, et al. "Comment on Van Nes, Imsland and Jones 'Salmon Lice Biology, Environmental Factors, and Smolt Behaviour With Implications for the Norwegian Salmon Farming Management System: A Critical Review'"  
**Journal:** Reviews in Aquaculture  
**DOI / URL:** 10.1111/raq.70050  
**Document type:** Letter / response (open access)  
**Priority questions:** Q5, Q7, Q8, Q10  
**Funding / COI:** All relevant interests disclosed; no competing interests reported.

---

## What this paper does

In this response, Van Nes, Imsland, and Jones (2025) address the critiques presented by Stige et al. (2025) regarding their earlier critical review of the Traffic Light System (TLS) used in Norwegian salmon farming. The authors defend the scientific integrity and peer-reviewed status of their original review, which highlighted significant uncertainties in the TLS related to salmon lice (*Lepeophtheirus salmonis*) management. The paper emphasizes the importance of incorporating accurate biological and ecological data into the TLS to enhance its effectiveness in managing lice-induced mortality in salmon smolts (*Salmo salar*).

The authors argue that the TLS, as currently implemented, relies on outdated and potentially misleading data regarding smolt migration timing and lice mortality estimates. They contend that the migration models used by the Expert Group (EG) are based on limited observations and do not adequately reflect the complexities of smolt behavior in various Norwegian watercourses. This response is crucial for ongoing discussions about the management of salmon lice in aquaculture, particularly in the context of wild salmon conservation.

---

## Methods and data

The authors reference a variety of data sources and methodologies in their defense. They highlight that their original review was subjected to extensive peer review, involving five reviewers, which they argue lends credibility to their findings. The response critiques the EG's reliance on a fixed 40-day migration window derived from observations of only 25% smolt emigration, contrasting this with their own findings based on acoustic telemetry data that indicate earlier and shorter migration periods for smolts across multiple years and fjords.

The authors also discuss the calibration of lice estimates, asserting that both the production estimates of lice larvae from fish farms and the calibration data used in the TLS are unreliable. They reference the work of Eliasen et al. (2021), which noted high variability in calibration data, further complicating the reliability of the TLS outputs.

---

## Key findings

1. **Peer Review & Bias**: The original review underwent rigorous peer review, and allegations of bias from the EG are rejected as unconstructive.

2. **Migration Timing**: The authors argue that the TLS model uses a fixed migration window that does not account for the variability observed in acoustic telemetry studies, which show that smolts migrate earlier and for shorter durations than the TLS assumes.

3. **Calibration Argument**: The authors counter the EG's claims that calibration neutralizes overestimation of lice larvae by stating that if both input and calibration data are unreliable, the calibration cannot compensate for inaccuracies.

4. **Post-Attachment Loss**: The EG's recent adjustment of a 40% correction in the NVI model acknowledges the issue of post-attachment lice loss, yet five out of seven TLS mortality endpoints still assume 100% survival of attached lice, leading to potential overestimation of mortality.

5. **Conflicts of Interest**: The authors assert that all relevant conflicts of interest were disclosed during the review process, dismissing allegations of bias as unfounded.

---

## Limitations and caveats

The authors acknowledge that their response is limited to addressing the specific critiques raised by Stige et al. and does not encompass all potential uncertainties related to the TLS. They emphasize the need for ongoing research to refine the models used in the TLS and to incorporate a broader range of ecological data. The reliance on existing literature and the potential for variability in the data used for calibration are also noted as limitations.

---

## What this paper does not claim

The authors do not claim to provide a definitive solution to all issues related to salmon lice management in aquaculture. They focus specifically on defending their original review's findings and addressing the critiques raised by the EG. The response does not present new empirical data but rather reinforces the arguments made in their previous work.

## Relevance to this corpus

This response is significant for the ongoing discourse surrounding the reliability of the TLS in managing salmon lice impacts on wild salmon populations. It directly addresses key questions related to the accuracy of mortality estimates (Q5), the implications of migration timing (Q7), and the calibration of ecological models (Q10). The exchange between Van Nes et al. and Stige et al. highlights unresolved scientific disagreements that are critical for future research and policy decisions in aquaculture.
