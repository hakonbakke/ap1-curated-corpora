# Comment on “Salmon Lice Biology, Environmental Factors, and Smolt Behaviour With Implications for the Norwegian Salmon Farming Management System: A Critical Review”

Van Nes et al. [1] review the literature that forms the basis of the traffic light system (TLS) for regulating Norwegian salmon aquaculture capacity. The TLS is based on an expert group's (EG) assessment of juvenile wild Atlantic salmon mortality caused by farm-origin salmon lice. The review points to uncertainties and potential biases in the observational data and models used in the assessment. We agree that the assessment has many uncertainties and welcome a constructive critique. The EG's methodology is continuously developed and improved in response to new knowledge, and constructive critique as exemplified by the international evaluation of the TLS [2] is important to this process. However, the argumentation by van Nes et al. appears biased towards evidence that suggests a low impact from aquaculture on wild fish. With this comment, we aim to clarify some apparent misunderstandings of the literature and methodology applied by the EG.

• The review [1] argues that the assumed timing and duration of smolt migration contribute to unreliable observational and modeled data and to an overestimation of infestation pressure. Data from five selected river years of a single Norwegian region were used to support the review's claim that the EG's assumed migration timing is too late. The review ignores that these data, along with data from 343 more river years spanning the Norwegian coast, were also input to a model that estimated migration times while accounting for the significant influence of observation method [3]. The EG uses this model to predict migration timing for all

salmon-producing rivers. That five selected data points are lower than predicted does not demonstrate that smolt migration begins earlier than assumed by the TLS. Furthermore, the review erroneously claims the EG assumes juvenile salmon reside in coastal waters for 28 days, whereas virtual post-smolt models assume swimming speeds that imply much shorter coastal residence time for post-smolt from most rivers [4].

- • A central claim in the review is that lice larvae production from farmed salmon is overestimated. The review fails to mention that such possible overestimation is unlikely to bias mortality estimation, as models scale the calculated infestation pressure to observed lice counts on salmon smolts [4].
- • The review [1] points to several factors that can hypothetically lead to the overestimation of lice infestations in the different observational data and model products considered by the EG, whereas factors that can lead to underestimation are generally downplayed or ignored. For example, the review ignores that the trawl index may underestimate infestations on wild smolts by only covering the infestations from the river mouth to the trawling sites, and not the outermost parts of the migration routes to the open sea. The review also fails to mention that empirical analysis suggests sentinel cages underestimate infestation levels by an order of magnitude compared to trawl data [5]. #### Appendix A

This appendix addresses more (but not all) of the points raised in the review by van Nes et al. [1]. We focus on some points relevant to the mandate of the EG of the TLS, which is to assess the salmon lice-induced mortality of wild salmonids with a main focus on out-migrating postsmolt salmon.

- • The review [1] states that observational data overestimate infestation rates on juvenile salmon by being collected late in the season, when infestation pressure tends to be higher than earlier in the season. A similar argument is made for the location of data collection. To avoid these potential biases, the EG always takes into account the timing and location of collection when interpreting the observational data. As noted in the review, “all available evidence used in the TLS are considered along with the uncertainty in each line of evidence to build towards a single combined evaluation.” The review fails, however, to acknowledge that these potential biases in the raw data are corrected for when using the data to calibrate models. The models avoid such bias by using the modelled infestation pressure and the calibration factor (i.e., the scaling factor between infestation pressure and infestation levels) to predict lice infestation levels for out-migrating post-smolts in the migration period [5–8]. We also note that the migration data the review uses to argue that out-migration is too early are solely data from acoustically tagged fish and not any other methods. Such data consistently show an earlier out-migration time compared to other methods [3], most likely because acoustic tagging studies use a selection of larger fish during spring and larger fish migrate earlier than smaller fish [9].
- • The review states that there is a mismatch between trawling (and other observational data) and out-migration that “likely increases overestimates lice-induced mortality of wild salmon smolt.” However, in their analysis they do not consider the migration time from river mouth until where the trawling is performed (in Sognefjorden, 10–14 days according to the review). If this is included, the trawl period should capture fish that leave the rivers from about April 21–25, which is early in the migration period. Trawling is usually terminated in the beginning of June (e.g., June 5), thus only capturing fish that have migrated earlier than about May 26. Trawl catch data in Sognefjorden indicate that the largest catches, for example in 2020–2021, are observed the last days of the trawling period. In such years, the trawling may underestimate the number of lice. This suggests that the statement that “some of the lice data from trawls … is collected after the passage of smolt through the fjord” is misleading and contradicts that “estimates are biased towards later migrating smolts with associated elevated temperatures and elevated levels of infectious lice larvae.”


- • The review points to some good reasons for caution when using salmon lice counts on sea trout as proxy for infestation pressure for juvenile salmon. The tendency of generally higher lice levels on sea trout than juvenile salmon is also discussed and considered in the EG reports [4]. We find, however, the description that “there is poor correlation between lice levels on trout and salmon” to be highly misleading: As reference for this statement is the study “Salmon lice infestations on sea trout predicts infestations on migrating salmon post-smolts” [10], which reports a correlation of 0.71 between a sea trout derived index and log-transformed lice abundance on juvenile salmon. Ignoring this source of information would increase, not reduce, the uncertainty of the expert assessment.
- • Regarding the inclusion of data from sea trout and sentinel cages in the assessment, and the timing of the sampling periods: First, it should be reiterated that the assessment is not a mean of the seven indices, but an expert elicitation that explicitly considers the quality of the data obtained, including the spatial and temporal distribution of the data included. However, as the smolts migrate through an area, while the sentinel cages are fixed in position, no sentinel cage will reflect the actual infestation pressure a smolt experiences. Therefore, the sentinel cages are used to acquire knowledge of the temporal and spatial distribution of the infestation pressure. For the sea trout and Arctic charr caught using nets and traps much the same consideration is used, as also referred to by van Nes et al. However, what they do not include is that all stages of lice are counted and the stage distribution is also considered by the EG. As the temperature in the area the fish have stayed is known from both measurements in farms and models, infestation for a longer period can be considered. However, as pointed out in the review, trout and charr have different behaviours than juvenile salmon, and therefore the EG writes as referred to in the review that: “We therefore consider that sea lice on sea trout cannot be used to directly estimate salmon lice-induced mortality on salmon smolt, but in cases where large numbers of lice are observed on sea trout, this is a clear indication that migrating salmon smolts are also more susceptible to lice infection in a given area.”
- • As already noted, the large focus in the review on the reliability of farm origin infestation pressure is mostly irrelevant for biases in mortality estimates. If, for example, the concentration of infective lice larvae was generally only half as high as calculated, this would be compensated for by a doubling of the calibration factor, which translates the calculated infestation pressure to observed lice levels on juvenile salmon. The review is therefore simply wrong in their inference: “Therefore, TLS-driven assessments likely overestimate the production and survival of lice larvae from fish farms, leading to an overestimation of farm derived infection pressure and resulting smolt mortality.” Potential biases and uncertainties in farm origin infestation pressure could nonetheless contribute to the uncertainty in mortality estimates, and we address some of the points relating to the calculation of lice larvae production below.
- • The claim that only a small proportion of the adult females reproduce at low salmon lice concentrations (the “Allee” effect) is based on a single study that assumed that female lice could only reproduce if found on fish where male lice were also observed [11]. The review does not cite an observational study that shows that the Allee effect can be offset by the host switching of male lice [12], and ignores the ability of female lice to store male sperm, making it unnecessary for a male to remain present for a female to continue producing fertilised eggs [13]. Furthermore, statistical modelling of farm-to-farm spread of salmon lice have estimated the Allee effect to only occur at so low lice level that it is unlikely to be of practical importance in calculations of infestation pressure [14, 15].
- • The review argues that “not implementing salinity as a variable in estimates of lice larvae recruitment and survival is likely to contribute to overestimates of lice larvae production from fish farms in the TLS.” The review also points out that such overestimation is most likely to occur in inner fjord areas, where salinity is lowest. Including more complexity, however, does not always improve the models. Stige et al. [16] found for example that inclusion of salinity effects led to higher, not lower, uncertainty in predictions of lice infestations on smolts in sentinel cages. The prediction errors also increased if the natural mortality assumed for the lice larvae was increased [16]. The review cites Stige et al. [16], but does not mention these findings. Accounting for temperature effects on infectivity, on the other hand, improved predictions [16], and is done both in the Norwegian Veterinary Institute's and the Institute of Marine Research's virtual post-smolt models, in contrast to what is written in the review about the latter model.

- • The review states that “lice stage and reduction in lice load on fish post attachment must be considered when interpreting the impact of infection.” As written in the review, a correction for post-attachment loss of lice is made in two of the models, showing that such loss is indeed considered by the EG. We think, furthermore, that the review under-communicates the uncertainty and over-assesses the most likely magnitude of such loss. Estimates of post-attachment loss of lice derive from experimental studies with unknown relevance to fish in a natural setting. As exemplified by the results summarised in table 2 in the review, post-attachment losses range from near zero to > 80%, but the reasons for these differences between studies are not known. Experimental fish can, for example, physically get rid of lice by contact with, for example, the aquaria walls, in outlet pipes and through contact with other fish. Different models have made different assumptions about postattachment loss (and other uncertain model parameters) so that the outcomes of various models reflect this uncertainty (similar to the use of different models for climate projections). And as already noted, the observational indices are not interpreted by the EG as direct estimates of mortality. Given the experimental data available (summarised in table 2 in the review), we disagree that 40% reduction is an underestimate: This is approximately the loss from the Chalimus stages—which typically dominate the observation data, to the pre-adult stages—which are more harmful to the host. We may add that some lice on the observed fish in the surveillance programme are likely lost due to handling of the fish prior to counting, an error source that has the opposite effect to post-attachment loss and which is not mentioned in the review.
- • We do not agree in the interpretation of the studies used to arrive on a tolerance limit for post-smolt salmon. The review concludes from the study by Grimnes and Jakobsen [17] that results are “suggesting a threshold of approximately 30 or more pre-adult (mobile) lice causing mortality in a 40 g smolt.” Actually, Grimnes and Jakobsen [17] conclude: Because of a high infection intensity obtained in this experiment, all infected fish became moribund prior to the appearance of adult female lice. Thus, it was not possible to gain data on levels of infection tolerance in Atlantic salmon post-smolt for salmon lice reaching the adult stage. The adult stage is known to have an even higher impact on the fish [18]. Hence, lower infection intensity than tested for here will probably cause death of Atlantic salmon post-smolt. In the Fjelldal et al. paper [19] where 39 fish died, 2 died and 10 were moribund at the day the experiment was terminated. This indicates that the threshold is lower than observed in the experiment. Holst et al. [20] is based on observations of number of lice on post-smolts caught during July, and Holst et al. also refers to the study referred to as Nilsen et al. unpublished in table 3. They state that no fish with more than 10 lice were observed among thousands of post-smolts, and they also state that post-smolt with nearly 10 lice were in a bad condition, with nearly no growth and haemoglobin levels < 20, and write “Many of these fishes appears to be in a state where their chances of survival are small.” This points to that the threshold should be < 10 lice per fish.


• Regarding the release experiments, Vollset et al. [21] showed that the reason for lack of an effect of emamectin benzoate (Slice (R)) in the controlled release experiments is likely that the lice have developed immunity against this treatment. This point was not mentioned in the review. Also, there are data from Ireland [22] that indicate a strong effect on salmon post-smolt mortality that is not included in the review. Finally, the review does not mention the important point that the treatment chemical emamectin benzoate may cause mortality in the treated group [23], which would lead to underestimation of salmon lice-induced mortality; exemplifying the review's tendency to not point out sources of uncertainty that potentially cause the effects of salmon lice on wild fish to be underestimated.
