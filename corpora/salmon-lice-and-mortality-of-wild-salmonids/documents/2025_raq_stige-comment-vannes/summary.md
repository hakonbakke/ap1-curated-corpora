# Summary: Stige et al. 2025 Comment on 'Salmon Lice Biology, Environmental Factors, and Smolt Behaviour With Implications for the Norwegian Salmon Farming Management System: A Critical Review'

**Full title:** Comment on "Salmon Lice Biology, Environmental Factors, and Smolt Behaviour With Implications for the Norwegian Salmon Farming Management System: A Critical Review"  
**Journal:** Reviews in Aquaculture, 17, e70040  
**DOI / URL:** [10.1111/raq.70040](https://doi.org/10.1111/raq.70040)  
**Document type:** Letter / commentary (open access)  
**Priority questions:** Q5, Q7, Q8, Q10  
**Funding / COI:** Supported by Norwegian Veterinary Institute, Institute of Marine Research, and Norwegian Institute for Nature Research; all authors are current members of the TLS Expert Group, with some having declared conflicts of interest related to industry funding.

---

## What this paper does

Stige et al. (2025) present a commentary in response to the critical review by Van Nes et al. (2024) regarding the Traffic Light System (TLS) used for regulating salmon farming in Norway. The TLS is designed to assess the impact of salmon lice (*Lepeophtheirus salmonis*) on wild juvenile Atlantic salmon (*Salmo salar*), particularly during their migration. The authors argue that the Van Nes review presents a biased view that underestimates the impact of aquaculture on wild salmon populations, highlighting several methodological flaws and misinterpretations in the review.

The commentary is significant within the context of ongoing debates about the ecological impacts of salmon farming, particularly in Norway, where salmon aquaculture is a major industry. The authors emphasize the importance of accurate assessments of lice-induced mortality to inform management practices and regulatory frameworks. By addressing specific claims made in the Van Nes review, Stige et al. aim to clarify the methodologies employed by the TLS Expert Group and reinforce the validity of their findings.

---

## Methods and data

The authors critique the methodologies used in the TLS, which is based on a comprehensive analysis of data from 343 river-years across Norway, contrasting this with the five river-years cited by Van Nes et al. They argue that the TLS employs a robust calibration process that adjusts for potential biases in larval production estimates from farmed salmon. The authors also discuss the use of expert elicitation to assess mortality rates rather than relying solely on an average of multiple indices, which they argue is a more nuanced approach.

Key definitions include:
- **Calibration Factor:** A scaling factor that adjusts the predicted infestation pressure to observed lice counts on salmon smolts.
- **Expert Elicitation:** A process that incorporates expert judgment to assess the impact of various factors on salmon lice-induced mortality.

The authors also reference various studies and models, including those that account for environmental factors such as salinity and temperature, which influence lice survival and infestation rates.

---

## Key findings

1. **Smolt Migration Timing:** The authors argue that the five river-years used by Van Nes et al. do not invalidate the coast-wide model derived from 343 river-years, which suggests that migration timing is accurately predicted by the TLS.

2. **Farm Larvae Production:** Even if larval production estimates are overestimated, the authors contend that the calibration process would adjust mortality estimates accordingly, indicating that the overall impact on mortality remains largely unchanged.

3. **Bias in Data Interpretation:** Stige et al. highlight that the Van Nes review emphasizes overestimation pathways while neglecting underestimation factors, such as the limited coverage of trawl data and the significant discrepancy in infestation levels observed in sentinel cages compared to trawl data.

4. **Correlation with Sea Trout Data:** The authors correct the claim of poor correlation between sea trout lice levels and salmon smolt abundance, citing a correlation coefficient of **0.71** from relevant studies.

5. **Post-Attachment Lice Loss:** The authors assert that the TLS accounts for post-attachment lice loss, which can vary significantly, and that the estimated correction of ~40% is reasonable based on available data.

6. **Mortality Thresholds:** They challenge the mortality threshold suggested by Van Nes et al., arguing that field data support a threshold of **<10** mobile lice rather than the ~30 proposed.

7. **Release Experiments and Drug Resistance:** The authors note that the Van Nes review omits critical findings regarding lice resistance to treatments and the potential mortality effects of treatment chemicals, which could bias mortality estimates.

8. **Conflicts of Interest:** Stige et al. raise concerns about undisclosed conflicts of interest in the Van Nes review, specifically regarding funding sources and affiliations with the aquaculture industry.

---

## Limitations and caveats

The authors acknowledge that their commentary is based on existing data and methodologies, which are subject to uncertainties and biases inherent in ecological modeling. They emphasize that while their rebuttal addresses specific claims, the TLS methodology is continually evolving in response to new evidence. The authors also recognize that their interpretations may not encompass all possible biases or uncertainties in the data.

---

## What this paper does not claim

Stige et al. do not claim that the TLS is without flaws or that all uncertainties have been resolved. They acknowledge the complexity of ecological interactions and the need for ongoing research to refine models and improve assessments of salmon lice impacts. The commentary is focused on clarifying specific misunderstandings and defending the current methodologies rather than asserting absolute certainty in the findings.

## Relevance to this corpus

This commentary is a critical contribution to the ongoing debate regarding the impact of salmon lice on wild salmon populations and the efficacy of the TLS. It directly addresses questions related to model calibration (Q5), threshold and dose-response logic (Q7–Q8), and the design of regulatory systems (Q10). The document serves as a defender-side response in the controversy surrounding the TLS methodology.
