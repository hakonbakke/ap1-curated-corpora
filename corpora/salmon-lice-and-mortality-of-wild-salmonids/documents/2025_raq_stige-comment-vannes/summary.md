# Stige et al. 2025 — Comment on "Salmon Lice Biology, Environmental Factors, and Smolt Behaviour With Implications for the Norwegian Salmon Farming Management System: A Critical Review"

## Background
The Expert Group (EG) members of the Norwegian TLS authored this comment in response to Van Nes et al. (2024), which argued the TLS systematically overestimates lice-induced mortality. The comment aims to clarify "apparent misunderstandings of the literature and methodology" applied by the EG.

## Key Arguments
1. **Migration timing**: The 5 data points from acoustic telemetry used by Van Nes et al. are part of a larger dataset (343+ river-years) used to model migration timing. That 5 points are lower than predicted does not demonstrate systematic bias. Also, Van Nes et al. erroneously claims 28-day coastal residence — virtual post-smolt models assume faster swimming.
2. **Lice larvae overestimation**: Models calibrate calculated infestation pressure against observed lice counts. Even if larvae production were halved, the calibration factor would double, compensating. So overestimation of larvae is "unlikely to bias mortality estimation."
3. **One-sided bias argument**: Van Nes et al. only points to factors that overestimate while ignoring underestimation factors (e.g., trawl only captures inner fjord migration; sentinel cages underestimate by order of magnitude).
4. **Post-attachment lice loss**: 40% correction is approximately the loss from chalimus to pre-adult stages. Experimental data are variable (0-80%) and reasons for variation unknown. Handling loss during observation has opposite effect and is not mentioned.
5. **Mortality thresholds**: Grimnes & Jakobsen (1996) concluded lower infection intensity than tested would "probably cause death." Holst et al. observed no fish with >10 lice survived among thousands of post-smolts.
6. **Conflicts of interest**: Van Nes et al. omitted that "Salmon Tracking 2030" is funded by aquaculture companies, and first author is on the board of a salmon farming company.
