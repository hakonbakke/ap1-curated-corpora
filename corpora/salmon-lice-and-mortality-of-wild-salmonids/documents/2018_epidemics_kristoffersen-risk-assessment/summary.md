## What this paper does

Kristoffersen et al. (2018) present a quantitative risk assessment model to evaluate the impact of salmon lice (*Lepeophtheirus salmonis*) on the mortality of seaward-migrating post-smolt Atlantic salmon (*Salmo salar*). The study is situated within the context of Norwegian aquaculture, where the government has implemented a management system to regulate salmon farming based on the effects of salmon lice on wild salmon populations. The model assesses the expected salmon lice infestations and subsequent mortality for post-smolts migrating from 401 rivers into Norwegian coastal waters, focusing on 13 production zones. This research is critical as it addresses the environmental sustainability of salmon farming, which has seen significant growth alongside declining wild salmon populations.

The model integrates various parameters, including farm-reported lice counts, water temperature, and empirical data from sentinel cage trials conducted between 2013 and 2016. The findings are particularly relevant for understanding the spatial and temporal risks associated with salmon lice infestations, which vary significantly across different production zones. The study emphasizes the need for effective management strategies to mitigate the impact of salmon lice on wild salmon populations, particularly in regions with high aquaculture density.

## Methods and data

The risk assessment model developed by the authors is structured around a series of interconnected processes:

1. **Farm-origin infestation pressure:** The model begins with the production of salmon lice larvae on farms, which is calculated based on reported numbers of reproductive female lice and water temperatures at a depth of 3 meters. The daily production of salmon louse eggs is estimated, leading to an internal infestation pressure (IIP) that is spatially interpolated within a 100 km radius of each farm.

2. **Exposure of post-smolts:** The model assumes that migrating post-smolts follow the shortest path from river outlets to the high seas, with a constant progression rate of 8.2 cm/s over a 40-day migration period. Infestation rates on migrating salmon are derived from a functional relationship established through sentinel cage trials involving 7,911 fish across 329 trials.

3. **Mortality estimation:** The model employs a mortality scheme based on thresholds established by Taranger et al. (2015), where mortality rates are linked to the number of lice per fish. For example, fish with fewer than 2 lice have a 0% mortality rate, while those with more than 6 lice face a 100% mortality rate.

The study covers data from 401 rivers and spans the years 2014 to 2016, providing a comprehensive overview of the risks associated with salmon lice infestations in Norwegian aquaculture.

## Key findings

- **Density dependence:** The output of louse eggs per biomass varies significantly across production zones, with an 8.1-fold difference observed. The local biomass density (LBD) shows a strong correlation with louse egg production (r=0.70, p=0.008).
  
- **Infestation pressure:** The model predicts that rivers draining into production zone 3 are at the highest risk, with estimated proportional mortality rates in 2016 ranging from approximately 2% to 17% under basic scenarios and 10% to 72% under worst-case scenarios.

- **Yearly variation:** The risk of louse-induced mortality was notably higher in 2016 compared to 2014 and 2015, indicating potential treatment efficacy concerns.

## Limitations and caveats

The authors acknowledge several limitations within their model. Key uncertainties include:

- **Infestation random effects:** The model's predictions are sensitive to variations in infestation rates, which can fluctuate significantly based on environmental conditions and farm management practices.
- **Timing of migration:** The timing of smolt migration is critical, with potential variations of ±2–4 weeks that could impact exposure to infestation.
- **Mortality thresholds:** The mortality estimates are based on existing literature, which may not fully capture the complexities of wild salmon interactions with salmon lice.
- **Migration pathways:** The assumption that smolts follow the shortest path may not accurately reflect actual migratory behavior, which could lead to misestimations of risk.

## What this paper does not claim

The study does not claim to provide definitive mortality rates for all wild salmon populations, nor does it account for other potential environmental stressors affecting salmon survival. The model is specifically focused on the impact of farm-origin salmon lice and does not address the effects of other pathogens or environmental changes that may also influence wild salmon populations.

This paper is relevant to several priority questions regarding the ecological impacts of aquaculture on wild salmon populations (Q2, Q5, Q7, Q8, Q10).
