The relative susceptibility of sea trout (Sabno trutta L.) and Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar L.) to sea lice infections was investigated under experimental conditions. Skin damage was described for both sea trout and Atlantic salmon, associated with the accumulation of feeding copepodids and chalimus on the dorsal fin and the settlement patterns of the pre-adult and adult lice, on the head and operculum, dorsal ridge, and behind the anal fin. Attachment and survival of chahmus stages were significantly lower on Atlantic salmon than on sea trout because of non-selective settlement of the copepodids, followed by differential mortality. The survival of pre-adult and adult sea lice declined more rapidly on sea trout than on Atlantic salmon, but ultimately, Atlantic salmon had a lower abundance of lice than sea trout. Two possibilities were considered to explain the differences in skin damage-parasite numbers and distribution of lice between sea trout and Atlantic salmon. A greater antibody response by Atlantic salmon to sea lice infection may indicate an immunological strategy rather than the possible behavioural adaptation of a freshwater return by infected sea trout, Alternatively, differences may reflect the parasite’s adaptation to the host’s migratory patterns in order to enhance survival of the sea lice population.

Fish mortalities due to sea lice infections are highly dependent on water temperature, size of the fish, and time in sea water. Recent studies have quantified lethal numbers of sea lice on Atlantic salmon under specific environmental and physiological regimes (Grimnes and Jakobsen, 1996). Parasite-stage-specific skin damage similarly has not been quantified for either sea trout or Atlantic salmon, but some fin erosion has been observed on Scottish sea trout (McVicar et al., 1994). The present study was designed to investigate the relative susceptibility of sea trout and Atlantic salmon to sea lice infections, and to assess the stage-specific lesions associated with juvenile and adult sea lice on both species.

Data collection

![image 38](<Dawson et al_1997_Comparison of the susceptibility of sea trout and Atlantic salmon to sea lice infections_images/imageFile38.png>)

All fish were anaesthetized weekly after infection and the number, developmental stage, and position of the lice were determined microscopically in seven areas of the fish surface. Below the lateral line these were: head region, posterior ventral from the caudal fin to the anal fin, mid-ventral from the pectoral fins to the pelvic fins, anterior ventral from the pectoral fins to the head. Above the lateral line these were: posterior dorsal from the caudal fin to the adipose fin, mid-dorsal from the adipose fin to the dorsal fin, and anterior dorsal from the caudal fin to the head.

Materials and methods

Maintenance of fish

The fish were randomly divided into quadruplicate groups of sea trout (n=8) and Atlantic salmon (n=8) and quadruplicate groups of an equal mixture of sea trout and Atlantic salmon (sea trout, n = 4; Atlantic salmon, n =4) and acclimated for a period of 3 weeks. By the end of the experiment, all sea trout had significantly more sea lice than Atlantic salmon. Adaptation of sea lice to the behaviour of migrating sea trout and Atlantic salmon may have accounted for the differences observed in the mean numbers of lice at the end of the experiment. Sea-going Atlantic salmon are ultimately oceanic, whereas sea trout remain in coastal waters. Enhanced transmission potential of sea lice in coastal and estuarine areas may reflect the higher abundance of sea lice, including gravid females on sea trout, although some evidence exists for transmission of sea lice in the open sea (Jacobsen and Gaard, 1997). A more rapid and greater humoral antibody response to sea lice infection has been demostrated for Atlantic salmon than for sea trout, with ultimately lower sea lice abundance (ICES, 1997). The greater humoral response of Atlantic salmon to sea lice infection may be indicative of an immunological strategy in response to sea lice infection rather than the possible behavioural adaptation of freshwater return for infected sea trout.

The limited dorsal fin erosion observed for the control fish is probably a consequence of nipping and abrasion (Heimer et al., 1985). Both Atlantic salmon and sea trout in mixed and single species tanks had similar cumulative categories of dorsal fin erosion, even though sea trout had significantly more sea lice on the dorsal fin. The structure of the mouth tube of sea lice suggests that feeding of both the juvenile and adult stages of sea lice is responsible for the development of skin lesions (Kabata, 1974) causing erosion of fin and skin tissues (Wootten et al., 1982; Jones et al., 1990; J6nsd6ttir et al., 1992).
