# Summary: Jones et al. (2025)

**Full title:** Trends in sea lice infestations on chum and pink salmon in the Broughton Archipelago remain unchanged despite removal of finfish aquaculture
**Journal:** Diseases of Aquatic Organisms, 163: 107–112
**DOI:** 10.3354/dao03866
**Document type:** Research article (note / short paper)
**Priority questions:** Q1, Q9, Q10

---

## What this paper does

This paper reports a 9-year longitudinal study (2016–2024) of sea lice infestations on juvenile chum salmon (*Oncorhynchus keta*) and pink salmon (*O. gorbuscha*) in the Broughton Archipelago (BA), coastal British Columbia — the most intensively studied sea lice system in BC and one of the most studied globally. The study was designed to exploit the declining presence of finfish aquaculture in the BA as a natural experiment to understand the relative contributions of farm and non-farm sources to wild salmon lice infestations.

The BA has been a focal area for salmon lice research for ~25 years because of its historically high density of Atlantic salmon netpen facilities and its role as a migration corridor for juvenile Pacific salmon from spawning streams in Knight Inlet and nearby waterways. The dataset analysed was a subset of a larger monitoring dataset (2016–2024, provided by Mowi and Cermaq through their public sustainability reporting), restricted to 14 fixed beach seine sites sampled in both April and May each year to ensure comparability. A total of 2,868 juvenile salmon were examined: 1,463 chum and 1,405 pink. All fish were euthanized, identified to species, measured, and examined by microscopy for sea lice (identified to species and developmental stage).

The study covers three analytically distinct phases: extensive aquaculture (2016–2018, 14–15 facilities, mean biomass ~14,900 metric tonnes); phased removal (2019–2023, declining from 17 facilities and 21,645 t to 5 facilities and 8,882 t); and near-complete removal (2024, 2 facilities, 614 t). This phased design allowed examination of trends across the full arc of aquaculture removal.

---

## Main findings

- **In 2024, prevalence of both lice species increased to levels similar to or exceeding values from the full-aquaculture era (2016–2021).** Prevalence of all lice on chum salmon reached 40.7% and on pink salmon 40.3% — among the highest values in the entire time series.

- **Prevalence of *L. salmonis*** on chum salmon increased from 8.6% (2023) to 21.3% (2024); on pink salmon from 2.3% (2023) to 12.4% (2024). Both values are in the range of, or higher than, many pre-removal years.

- **Prevalence of *C. clemensi*** on chum increased from 3.9% (2023) to 28.7% (2024); on pink from 5.1% (2023) to 32.6% (2024), exceeding most prior years.

- **All-time lows occurred in 2023** — not 2024. The steep decline in 2023 was followed by an equally sharp rebound in 2024, despite virtually no aquaculture in either year. This pattern strongly implicates environmental and natural reservoir dynamics rather than aquaculture as the driver.

- **Aquaculture biomass** declined from a peak of 21,645 metric tonnes in 2019 to 614 t in 2024 — a 97% reduction — yet this did not produce a sustained reduction in lice infestations on wild fish.

- **High proportions of *C. clemensi*** were observed in years of both high and low aquaculture presence (2017, 2020, 2024 on chum; 2017, 2018, 2024 on pink), consistent with this species drawing on non-salmonid reservoir hosts (herring, stickleback) that are unrelated to aquaculture.

## Limitations and transferability to Norway

- **No statistical modelling:** The paper is entirely descriptive; no formal test of the aquaculture–lice relationship is presented.
- **No reservoir host sampling:** The paper asserts that natural reservoirs are important but does not sample them directly, limiting quantification of their contribution.
- **Sample size reduction from 2019:** The shift from 30 to 15 fish per site per collection from 2019 onwards reduces the precision of prevalence estimates for post-removal years.
- **Phased removal design:** Farms were removed gradually over 5 years (2019–2023), not in a single event, making the before/after comparison less clean than an abrupt cessation. This is unavoidable given the regulatory context.
- **Industry data provenance:** Part of the dataset derives from industry (Mowi, Cermaq) monitoring programmes; while standardised and peer-reviewed, this warrants note for independence considerations.
- **Single region:** Results from Tribune Channel/Fife Sound may not represent broader BA or BC coastal dynamics.

**Transferability to Norway:** The Broughton Archipelago shares structural similarities with Norwegian fjord systems: restricted waterways, multiple farm sites, and anadromous salmonids migrating through farm zones. The central finding — that sea lice on wild fish rebounded after near-total farm removal, demonstrating the importance of natural reservoir hosts — is directly relevant to debates about the Norwegian TLS, which attributes wild fish lice burden primarily to farms. If natural reservoirs in Norwegian fjords play a comparable role, then the TLS attribution framework would overestimate the degree to which farm management can protect wild fish. This is an empirical question requiring investigation in a Norwegian context, and these BC data provide a clear scientific precedent and methodological template.

## Place in the literature

The Broughton Archipelago has been the epicentre of the Pacific salmon sea lice controversy for over two decades, with foundational papers by Krkošek et al. (2007) and Morton et al. establishing the farm-attribution narrative that drove Canadian aquaculture policy. This paper directly challenges that narrative using the same geographic system, the same methods, and a longer time series than most earlier work. It joins a set of 2025 papers (Jones et al. JFD; van Nes et al.; Jeong et al.) that collectively form a substantial revision of the earlier scientific consensus. The companion paper in *Journal of Fish Diseases* (Jones et al. 2025) reports identical findings from the Discovery Islands, another BC coastal region where farm removal had been complete since 2022, providing independent corroboration. Together, these two Jones et al. 2025 papers represent the most timely and empirically direct challenge to the farm-attribution assumption in the international sea lice literature.
