# Trends in sea lice infestations on chum and pink salmon in the Broughton Archipelago remain unchanged despite removal of finfish aquaculture

## Simon R. M. Jones1, Crawford W. Revie2, Lance Stewardson3,*

1Fisheries and Oceans Canada, Pacific Biological Station, 3190 Hammond Bay Road, Nanaimo, British Columbia, V9T 6N7 Canada 2Department of Computer and Information Sciences, University of Strathclyde, 26 Richmond Street, Glasgow, G1 1XQ, Scotland, UK 3Mainstream Biological Consulting, 1310 Marwalk Cres, Campbell River, British Columbia, V9W 5X1 Canada

ABSTRACT: To better understand the relative contributions of sea lice Lepeophtheirus salmonis and Caligus clemensi from farmed and non-farmed sources, infestations with sea lice are described on juvenile chum salmon Oncorhynchus keta and pink salmon O. gorbuscha from the Broughton Archipelago (BA) in coastal British Columbia, Canada, during a period of declining salmon aquaculture presence. A total of 2868 salmon were collected by beach seine from 14 sites between 2016 and 2024 and examined for sea lice infestation by microscope. During this time, production of Atlantic salmon in open netpens in the BA declined from a high of 21645 metric tonnes (t) in 17 facilities in 2019 to 614 t in 2 facilities in 2024. The annual prevalence of all sea lice on chum salmon ranged from 53.7% in 2022 to 12.5% in 2023 and on pink salmon from 62.9% in 2022 to 7.3% in 2023. In 2024, the prevalence of L. salmonis and C. clemensi on both salmon species increased and was similar to or higher than annual prevalence values measured between 2016 and 2021, in dicating the importance of natural reservoirs as sources of sea lice infestation in the BA.

KEY WORDS: Sea lice · Lepeophtheirus salmonis · Caligus clemensi · Salmon aquaculture · Wild salmon · Oncorhynchus

#### 1. INTRODUCTION

Sea lice are parasitic copepods with life cycles that include non-parasitic and dispersive stages (nauplii, copepodid) and those that are parasitic on marine fishes (copepodid, chalimus, preadult, adult). In British Columbia (BC), Canada, the salmon louse Lepe ophtheirus salmonis and the herring louse Caligus clemensi are 2 sea lice species common on salmonid fish. Furthermore, sea lice infestations may adversely affect the health of juvenile Pacific salmon Oncorhynchus spp. with conservation implications (Morton & Wil-

liams 2003, Krkošek et al. 2007, Krkošek & Hilborn 2011). In BC, the proximity of juvenile salmon migratory routes to open netpen salmon aquaculture sites led to an assumption that most, if not all, sea lice infesting the juvenile salmon originate from infestations on the farmed salmon (Peacock et al. 2016). In contrast, a recent study (van Nes et al. 2025) concluded that some models may have over-estimated the degree to which infestation pressure from sea lice originating from aquaculture contribute to juvenile wild salmon infestations. Another study (Jeong et al. 2025) found that sea lice infestation on juvenile salmon in several

Disagreements related to the strength of association between sea lice infestations on cultured and wild salmon in BC have persisted in part because of a poor understanding of natural infestations on the juvenile salmon in the absence of aquaculture. However, in recent years, finfish aquaculture facilities have been removed from several BC coastal regions including the Broughton Archipelago (BA). For almost 25 yr, the BA has been the focus of a robust scientific effort to understand sea lice interactions between cultured and wild juvenile salmon. Therefore, the recently reduced aquaculture presence in the BA presents an opportunity to understand the ecology of sea lice infestations on juvenile Pacific salmon as the influence of aquaculture is minimized (see Routledge & Morton 2023).

The purpose of this paper is to summarize and compare sea lice infestations on juvenile chum salmon O. keta and pink salmon O. gorbuscha in the BA over 9 yr

including 3 yr (2016–2018) with extensive aquaculture operations, 5 yr (2019–2023) with phased removal of farms, and 1 yr (2024) in which aquaculture facilities were almost entirely removed from the study area.

#### 2. MATERIALS AND METHODS

The study area focused on Tribune Channel and Fife Sound (Fig. 1), which had the highest density of continuously operating Atlantic finfish aquaculture facilities in the BA and is an important migration corridor for juvenile salmon emerging from streams in Knight Inlet, Thompson Sound, Bond Sound and Viner Sound (Morton et al. 2005, Krkosek et al. 2006). These sites were located within a zone potentially influenced by lice originating from nearby aquaculture facilities (Krkošek et al. 2005, Rees et al. 2015). Samples were collected from each site in April and May in all years except 2019, when the second collection occurred in early June.

Procedures used for beach seining, fish collection and field data recording between 2016 and 2024 were adapted from those used by the Department of Fisheries and Oceans (DFO) in the Broughton Archipelago between 2004 and 2009 (Hargreaves et al. 2004).

Between 2016 and 2018, up to 30 individual fish per species per site were collected in both months, and this was reduced to 15 per species between 2019 and 2024. Individual fish were haphazardly recovered from the net and packaged separately in re-sealable bags labelled with the site name, date and sample number. Bagged fish were euthanized by freezing with dry ice on board the sampling boat and stored on dry ice until delivered to the laboratory.

Frozen samples were delivered to the BC Centre for Aquatic Health Sciences (BC-CAHS) for laboratory analysis. Each fish was partially thawed, identified to species, and the weight and fork length measured. All sea lice observed during microscopic examination of the fish were identified to species and to developmental stage: copepodid, chalimus, pre-adult (male or female) or adult (male or female) (Kabata 1972, Hamre et al. 2013). All life stages of sea lice were used in the analysis. Sea lice infestations were described using prevalence (percent of sample infested) and intensity (number of lice per infested fish).

A total of 1463 juvenile chum salmon and 1405 juvenile pink salmon were analysed for sea lice. The mean weight of the chum salmon ranged from 0.81 g (2019)

The annual prevalence of the 2 sea lice species varied from year to year (Fig. 2) and displayed broadly similar patterns of infestation on both salmon species. The prevalence of Lepeophtheirus salmonis on chum salmon ranged from 36.5% in 2022 to 7.0% in 2017 and increased from 8.6% in 2023 to 21.3% in 2024. The prevalence on pink salmon ranged from 38.1% in 2022 to 2.3% in 2023, increasing to 12.4% in 2024 (Fig. 2A). The prevalence of Caligus clemensi on chum salmon ranged from 45.1% in 2020

On chum salmon, the prevalence of all lice ranged from 53.7% in 2022 to 12.5% in 2023, while on pink salmon, prevalence of all lice ranged from 62.9% in 2022 to 7.3% in 2023. The prevalence of all lice increased in 2024 to 40.7 and 40.3% for chum salmon and pink salmon, respectively.

Regardless of the sources of these infestations, variations in the exposure of wild migrating juvenile salmon to infective larval stages of these parasites are modulated by ambient salinity, temperature and water currents and with regards to L. salmonis from sources in aquaculture, by the proximity, biomass and infection-status of the cultured salmon (Brooks 2005, Rees et al. 2015, Bateman et al. 2016, Peacock et al. 2016, Nekouei et al. 2018, Moriarty et al. 2024, Jeong et al. 2025). However, given the declining presence of salmon aquaculture as a source of the infestations during the present study, the observed similarities in sea lice infestation patterns on wild salmon in 2023 and 2024 will have increasingly resulted from one or more environmental processes. Similar and coincidental patterns of decline and rebound in the prevalence and abundance of both sea lice species on juvenile wild salmon in the Discovery Islands, BC, coastal region following phased removals of salmon aquaculture (Jones et al. 2025) suggest a more widespread influence of environmental factors in coastal BC.

The use of estimated salmon biomass served to illustrate the rate of change of cultured salmon productivity in the region and was not a surrogate for any aquaculture impact. Indeed, cultured salmon infestation data combined with production inventory, salinity and temperature are required to provide meaningful estimates of farm-sourced sea lice infestation pressures (Jeong et al. 2025).

This study provides clear evidence that the prevalence and intensity of sea lice infestations on juvenile salmon in the BA in the virtual absence of salmon aquaculture occur at levels equal to or greater than those observed during full-scale production. Further research is required to better understand the sources of natural infestation and the environmental processes which modulate sea lice prevalence in the BA region.
