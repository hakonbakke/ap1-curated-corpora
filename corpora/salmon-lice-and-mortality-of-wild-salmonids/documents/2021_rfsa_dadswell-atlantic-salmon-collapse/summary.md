## What this paper does

Dadswell et al. (2021) provide a comprehensive review of the decline of *Salmo salar* populations across the North Atlantic since approximately 1985. The authors synthesize evidence from various geographic regions, including North America and Europe, to evaluate the primary drivers of this decline. They critically assess several hypotheses, including climate change, aquaculture impacts (particularly from sea lice), prey availability, and predation, arguing that these factors alone cannot account for the observed population collapses, especially in pristine rivers distant from aquaculture sites. Instead, they propose that illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) high-seas fishing is the primary driver of the decline, supported by historical data on legal fisheries and gaps in post-1985 surveillance.

The significance of this review lies in its challenge to the prevailing narrative that attributes the decline of Atlantic salmon primarily to local factors such as aquaculture and parasites. By highlighting the collapse of stocks in remote areas, the authors argue for a broader understanding of marine mortality factors affecting salmon populations, which is crucial for effective conservation and management strategies.

## Methods and data

The review employs a qualitative synthesis of existing literature and data on Atlantic salmon populations, focusing on trends in adult returns and the characteristics of stock collapses. The authors analyze historical data on salmon abundance, particularly before and after 1985, to illustrate the scale of decline. They reference various studies and datasets, including estimates of North American 1SW and 2SW salmon populations, which have seen declines of approximately 85% and 90%, respectively. The review also discusses the geographic distribution of salmon stocks and their proximity to the North Atlantic Subpolar Gyre (NASpG), which is posited as a critical factor in understanding the spatial patterns of decline.

Key definitions include:
- **1SW and 2SW**: Referring to one-sea-winter and two-sea-winter salmon, respectively, which are critical for understanding age-related return rates.
- **IUU fishing**: Referring to illegal, unreported, and unregulated fishing practices that have been implicated in the decline of salmon populations.

---

## Key findings

### Collapse phenotype
The authors identify four common traits among collapsed salmon stocks:
1. **Cyclic adult returns cease**: Historically, many stocks exhibited 8–11 year return cycles, which have now ceased.
2. **Abundance flatlines**: Many stocks have reached very low levels of abundance, indicative of overexploitation.
3. **Mean adult size declines**: There is a noted decrease in the size of returning adults.
4. **Geographic pattern of collapse**: Stocks in watersheds farthest from the NASpG feeding grounds experienced the earliest and most severe declines.

### Scale of decline
- **North American 1SW abundance**: Approximately 85% decline from 700,000–800,000 to 110,000–150,000 by the 1990s.
- **North American 2SW abundance**: Approximately 90% decline from 900,000 to 100,000.
- **Southern Europe total returns**: Approximately 70% decline since 1985.
- **Pre-1985 North Atlantic prefishery abundance**: Estimated at 6–9 million fish.

### Hatchery return rates
- **Mactaquac 2SW**: Return rates collapsed from 0.35–1.5% before 1985 to none after.
- **Penobscot 2SW**: Return rates collapsed from 0.2–1.3% before 1985 to none after.

### IUU fishing hypothesis
The authors argue that the historical context of legal high-seas fisheries, particularly in Greenland and the Norwegian Sea, has led to significant declines in salmon returns. The lack of surveillance in these areas since the 1980s has created opportunities for IUU fishing, which is suggested to be a major contributor to the observed population declines.

## Limitations and caveats

The review acknowledges several limitations:
- **Indirect evidence for IUU mortality**: The authors note that direct measurements of IUU fishing impacts on salmon populations are lacking.
- **Potential underweighting of Norwegian and British Columbia aquaculture studies**: The focus on North Atlantic stocks may overlook critical findings from these regions.
- **No quantitative attribution matrix**: The authors do not provide a detailed quantitative analysis of the relative contributions of different stressors to the decline.

---

## What this paper does not claim

The authors do not claim that IUU fishing is the sole cause of the decline in Atlantic salmon populations. They acknowledge the complexity of the issue and the potential roles of other factors, such as climate change and local environmental impacts, but argue that these alone cannot explain the widespread and synchronous declines observed across diverse geographic regions.

## Relevance to this corpus

This paper serves as a counterpoint to the narrative that emphasizes lice and aquaculture as primary drivers of salmon population declines (related to Q9). It suggests that regulatory focus on local aquaculture impacts may overlook significant marine mortality factors, particularly for oceanic stocks. The findings imply that management strategies should consider broader marine environmental factors and IUU fishing practices.
