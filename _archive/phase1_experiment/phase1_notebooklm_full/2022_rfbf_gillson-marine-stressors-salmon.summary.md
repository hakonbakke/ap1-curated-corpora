# Summary: Gillson et al. (2022)

**Full title:** A review of marine stressors impacting Atlantic salmon Salmo salar, with an assessment of the major threats to English stocks
**Journal:** Reviews in Fish Biology and Fisheries, 32: 879–919
**DOI:** 10.1007/s11160-022-09714-x
**Document type:** Review article
**Priority questions:** Q9

---

## What this paper does

This paper has two objectives:
1. Provide a comprehensive review of all principal marine stressors affecting Atlantic salmon between fresh water departure and adult return
2. Apply the same semi-quantitative 2D classification system developed by Forseth et al. (2017) for Norwegian rivers to assess relative threats to English salmon stocks over the current period and projected over the next decade

The study covers 42 principal salmon rivers in England, grouped into four regional marine plan areas.

## Principal marine stressors reviewed

The paper systematically covers:
- **Climate change** — temperature, ocean circulation, phenological mismatches
- **Predation** — seals, cetaceans, seabirds, fish predators
- **Pollution and bycatch** — water quality, fishing gear
- **Renewable energy developments** — offshore wind, tidal energy
- **Salmon mariculture** — sea lice, escaped fish, disease transmission
- **Other emerging stressors** — artificial light at night, electromagnetic fields, pile-driving noise, tidal lagoons

## Threat classification for English stocks

**Expanding high-impact stressors** (biggest current threats, likely to worsen):
- **Climate change**
- **Predation**

**Stabilised high-impact stressors** (currently significant, manageable going forward):
- Poor water quality
- Bycatch from fisheries

**Low-impact stressors** (relatively minor for English rivers):
- Salmon mariculture (sea lice, escaped fish)
- Tidal barrages, impingement, thermal discharges, noise pollution
- Artificial light at night, invasive species, electromagnetic fields

**No longer important**:
- Salmon fisheries exploitation — fishery restrictions have substantially reduced fishing pressure

## Why salmon mariculture ranks low for English stocks

The classification of salmon mariculture as a low-impact stressor for England reflects the near-absence of Atlantic salmon aquaculture in proximity to English salmon rivers. In contrast to Norway, Scotland, or British Columbia — where salmon farms are located along the migration corridors of wild smolts — English rivers migrate through waters with minimal farm presence. This illustrates the strong geographic dependence of the lice-related threat.

## The broader context

Despite the low ranking for England, the review acknowledges that marine survival has fallen to historically low levels (<5% of smolts now survive to return as adults in the North East Atlantic). Climate change is projected to be the dominant driver going forward, affecting ocean temperature, circulation, and prey availability.

## Relevance to this corpus

**Q9 (population-level impact):** Demonstrates that the relative importance of salmon lice from aquaculture as a population threat is *context-dependent*. For populations near farms (Norway, BC), lice may be the dominant driver of smolt mortality. For populations without farm proximity (English rivers), other stressors dominate. This is essential for interpreting Norwegian-specific findings in a broader Atlantic salmon management context.

## Limitations

- English-specific; findings on threat ranking should not be extrapolated to Norwegian production areas
- Classification involves expert judgment; quantitative support for some stressors (predation, ALAN) is limited
- Does not provide new primary data; synthesises existing literature
