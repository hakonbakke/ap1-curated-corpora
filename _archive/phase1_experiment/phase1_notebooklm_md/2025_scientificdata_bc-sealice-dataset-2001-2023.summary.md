# Summary: Crawford et al. (2025)

**Full title:** Sea lice infestation dataset for wild and farmed salmon populations on the Pacific coast of Canada (2001–2023)
**Journal:** Scientific Data (Nature Portfolio)
**DOI:** 10.1038/s41597-025-05653-x
**Document type:** Dataset description
**Priority questions:** Q1, Q5

---

## What this paper does

This data descriptor introduces a publicly released, harmonised dataset of sea lice monitoring records from Atlantic salmon farms and wild Pacific salmonid populations along the British Columbia (BC) coast, spanning over two decades (2001–2023). The primary purpose is to consolidate previously fragmented monitoring data from multiple regulatory bodies, operators, and NGO groups into a standardised format enabling long-term spatial and temporal analysis.

## The dataset

The dataset contains two parallel streams:

- **Farm data**: ~10,000 mean monthly sea lice estimates from ~100 Atlantic salmon farms, spread across seven DFO fish health sub-zones (2.3, 2.4 in Vancouver Island; 3.1–3.5 on the Mainland Coast).
- **Wild fish data**: Sea lice observations from over 365,000 individual wild Pacific salmonid hosts, recorded across ~17,000 sampling events in the same zones.

Temporal coverage varies by zone. The dataset spans continuous farm monitoring over two decades, while wild fish sampling is more spatially and temporally uneven.

## Key observations

- Data quality improved over time as monitoring protocols matured. Early records have limited sea lice species resolution (particularly for copepodites and chalimus stages observed in the field vs. in the laboratory).
- Changes in reporting format (monthly to weekly averages; farm-level to pen-level data) across the monitoring period reduce strict comparability.
- Notable declines in farm monitoring events from ~2021/2022 are evident in the Discovery Islands (sub-zone 3.2) and Broughton Archipelago (sub-zone 3.3) following Canadian government policy decisions to reduce salmon farming in those areas.

## Relevance to this corpus

The dataset provides the largest publicly available long-term record linking sea lice on Atlantic salmon farms to infestation of wild Pacific salmonids (Q1: attribution). It also represents a major empirical resource for evaluating the relationship between hydrodynamic infestation pressure models and observed lice loads (Q5: model validation), although in a Canadian rather than Norwegian regulatory context.

The paper explicitly notes that previous studies have made overly generalised claims about BC lice-farm impacts based on limited spatial or temporal datasets; this dataset enables more rigorous, large-scale assessments.

## Limitations

- The dataset is descriptive; no causal analysis of farm-to-wild transmission is performed here.
- Wild fish sampling is not standardised across zones or years, limiting direct comparisons.
- The BC regulatory context and host species composition (Pacific salmon) differ substantially from the Norwegian TLS context.
