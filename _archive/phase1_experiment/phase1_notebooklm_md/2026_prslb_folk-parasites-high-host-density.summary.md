# Summary: Folk et al. (2026)

**Full title:** Parasites perform poorly at high host densities: an experimental test in the salmon louse Lepeophtheirus salmonis
**Journal:** Proceedings of the Royal Society B, 293: 20251782
**DOI:** 10.1098/rspb.2025.1782
**Document type:** Research article (experimental)
**Priority questions:** Q2, Q6

---

## What this paper does

This experimental study examines how host density affects the performance of individual salmon lice (*Lepeophtheirus salmonis*). Using a novel individual-tagging method (RFID micro-chips on adult female lice), the authors tracked the complete adult life histories of 178 female lice across replicated tank populations that mimicked either natural (low density) or aquaculture (high density) conditions. The study uses lice from three geographically distinct Norwegian aquaculture locations.

## Experimental design

- **Treatments**: Low host density (5 fish per tank, 15 copepodites/fish) vs. high host density (15–16 fish, 30 copepodites/fish in reduced water volume)
- **Geographic origins**: Austevoll, Oppedal (Vestland), and Fosså (Rogaland) — three Norwegian farms
- **Temperature**: 8.8–9.5°C throughout
- **Tracking period**: 11 January to 30 December 2021 (up to 322 days post-infection for longest-lived female)
- **Variables measured**: Adult body size (somatic and reproductive), age at first reproduction, early fecundity (2nd clutch), lifetime fecundity, lifespan

## Key findings

At high host density (mimicking aquaculture):
- **Greater pre-reproductive mortality**: Adult parasite loads at maturity were only 25% higher than in the low-density treatment despite double the initial infection dose — indicating substantial juvenile louse mortality
- **Smaller adult body size**: Female lice were significantly smaller across all three geographic origins
- **Delayed maturation**: Females reproduced later (by ~12–15 degree-days post-infection)
- **Lower fecundity**: Both early (2nd clutch) and lifetime fecundity were significantly reduced
- **Size–performance correlation**: Larger females consistently reproduced earlier, were more fecund, and lived longer — body size integrates life conditions and predicts adult performance

## Mechanism

The most likely driver is **host immune response intensity**: hosts exposed to higher lice doses activate stronger immune defences (especially cellular immunity), which increase juvenile louse mortality and reduce growth of surviving lice. Lice may "pay" for resisting host immunity with reduced somatic growth, leading to smaller, less fecund adults. Direct competition among lice for host resources was found less likely as a mechanism.

## Evolutionary implications

Common garden studies had previously shown that lice from aquaculture environments have evolved greater investment in early reproduction at the expense of adult survival and fecundity (higher virulence). This study provides a mechanistic context: aquaculture conditions select for rapid, early reproduction as an adaptive response to stronger host immunity. As farmed fish now overwhelmingly dominate the host population numerically, lice adapted to aquaculture are the primary source of infection for wild salmon — potentially with greater virulence than lice adapted to wild hosts.

## Relevance to this corpus

**Q2 (estimating lice production):** Shows that louse fecundity, size, and reproductive timing are density-dependent and highly context-specific. This matters for models that use fecundity parameters to estimate larval production from farm lice counts.

**Q6 (physiological effects):** The finding that aquaculture-adapted lice may be more virulent than wild-adapted lice has implications for the harm caused by farm-origin lice infesting wild salmon — spillover events may impose greater physiological costs than models based on wild-origin lice assume.

## Limitations

- Laboratory tanks (500L) do not replicate conditions in large open-net cages
- Adult males could not be tagged and are excluded
- Three Norwegian farm origins; responses may vary in other environments
